**Coffee and Caffeine: Your Health Questions Answered**

Welcome to our running list of the questions we hear most often about coffee, caffeine, and what they actually do to your body. We've tried to keep the answers short, friendly, and honest. Nothing here is medical advice — if you have a specific condition, talk to your doctor — but for everyday curiosity, this should cover the basics.

---

**Q: How does caffeine actually wake me up?**

Caffeine works by blocking adenosine. Adenosine is the molecule that builds up in your brain over the course of the day and creates what scientists call "sleep pressure" — that heavy, drowsy feeling that grows the longer you've been awake. Caffeine has a shape similar enough to adenosine that it slips into the same receptors and sits in the parking spot without doing the job. Your brain stops getting the "time to wind down" signal, and you feel alert. It's worth noting that caffeine doesn't add energy; it just hides the tiredness for a while.

**Q: So the tiredness comes back later?**

Pretty much. The adenosine is still floating around the whole time — caffeine only blocks the receptors, it doesn't remove the molecule. Once the caffeine wears off, all that backed-up adenosine reaches the receptors at once, which is why some people feel a sudden slump in the afternoon.

**Q: How long does caffeine stay in my system?**

The number to remember is the half-life, which is about 5 hours. That means roughly five hours after your cup, half of the caffeine is still in your bloodstream. After another five hours, half of that remains, and so on. So a strong afternoon coffee can still have a noticeable amount of caffeine working in your body at bedtime, even if you don't feel wired anymore.

**Q: Does that half-life change from person to person?**

Yes, quite a bit. Genetics, liver enzymes, whether you smoke, certain medications, and pregnancy can all stretch or shorten how long caffeine lingers. The "about 5 hours" figure is a solid average for a healthy adult, but your personal number might be faster or slower. If coffee keeps you up at night, you may simply be a slow metabolizer.

**Q: How much caffeine is in a normal cup of coffee?**

A standard 8 oz cup of drip coffee has about 95 mg of caffeine. That's the figure we use as our baseline for everything else. Keep in mind that "a cup" in real life is often bigger than 8 oz — a large café coffee can easily be two or three times that size, and the caffeine scales up with it.

**Q: Why does the amount vary so much between coffees?**

Brewing method, grind size, how much coffee you use, water temperature, and contact time all play a role. But one of the biggest factors is the bean itself.

**Q: Is there really a difference between Arabica and Robusta?**

There's a big one when it comes to caffeine. Robusta has about twice the caffeine of Arabica. So a cup made from Robusta beans, or a blend with a lot of Robusta in it, will hit harder than the same-size cup of pure Arabica. Robusta also tends to taste more bitter and earthy, which is part of why many espresso blends include some — it adds punch and a thicker crema along with the extra kick.

**Q: How much caffeine is too much for a typical adult?**

For most healthy adults, a few cups spread across the day is generally considered fine. Problems usually start when caffeine piles up quickly: jitteriness, a racing heart, anxiety, and trouble sleeping are the common warning signs. If you notice those, ease off and give your body a few caffeine-light days. Everyone's tolerance is different, and it tends to drift higher the more regularly you drink coffee.

**Q: I'm pregnant — do I need to give up coffee entirely?**

Not necessarily, but the common guidance is to keep caffeine under about 200 mg per day during pregnancy. That's roughly two standard 8 oz cups of drip coffee, though you'll want to account for caffeine from tea, chocolate, soft drinks, and energy drinks too, since it all adds together. As always, your own doctor's advice comes first.

**Q: Does caffeine hide in foods I wouldn't expect?**

Definitely. Chocolate, many teas, some sodas, energy drinks, and even certain pain relievers and supplements contain caffeine. If you're trying to stay under a daily limit, those extras count just as much as your coffee does. Decaf isn't entirely caffeine-free either, though it has only a small fraction of the regular amount.

**Q: Is coffee actually good for me, or is that just wishful thinking?**

The research is genuinely encouraging, but it's important to be precise about what it shows. Studies that follow large groups of people over time have found that drinking 3-4 cups a day is associated with a lower risk of type 2 diabetes and Parkinson's disease. The key word is "associated with." These are observations of patterns across populations — they do not prove that coffee prevents those conditions. People who drink moderate coffee may differ in other ways too, and association is not the same as cause.

**Q: Why can't we just say coffee prevents disease, then?**

Because association studies can't separate coffee from everything else about a person's life. To show that coffee directly causes a lower risk, you'd need controlled trials, and those are hard to run for decades. So the honest summary is: moderate coffee drinking is linked to some good outcomes, the signal is consistent enough to be interesting, but "linked to" is as far as the evidence takes us. Don't start drinking coffee purely as medicine.

**Q: Does coffee dehydrate me?**

Caffeine is a mild diuretic, so it can nudge you toward the bathroom a little sooner. But the water in a cup of coffee more than makes up for it, so your daily coffee still counts toward your fluid intake. For most people, normal coffee drinking does not cause dehydration.

**Q: Will coffee stunt my growth?**

That's an old myth with no good evidence behind it. Caffeine doesn't shrink anyone. The kernel of truth is that caffeine late in the day can disrupt sleep, and growing kids and teens need plenty of sleep — so the sensible advice for young people is about sleep, not height.

**Q: Why does coffee sometimes upset my stomach?**

Coffee is naturally acidic and can stimulate stomach activity, which a sensitive stomach may not love, especially on an empty one. If that's you, try eating something alongside your coffee, switching to a darker or cold-brewed coffee (both tend to be gentler), or simply cutting back. It's about your individual gut, not a flaw in the coffee.

**Q: Can I build up a tolerance to caffeine?**

Yes. With regular intake, your body makes more adenosine receptors, so the same dose has less effect over time and you may need a bit more for the same lift. The flip side is dependence: skip your usual coffee and you might get a withdrawal headache, fatigue, or irritability for a day or two. It passes, and tapering down slowly makes it much easier than quitting cold.

**Q: When should I stop drinking coffee so it doesn't wreck my sleep?**

Because the half-life is about 5 hours, a cup in the late afternoon can still have meaningful caffeine in your system at bedtime. A good rule of thumb is to have your last full-strength coffee at least six to eight hours before you plan to sleep. If you're a slow metabolizer, push that even earlier, or switch to decaf for the evening.

**Q: Is decaf a reasonable substitute?**

For the flavor and ritual of coffee without much of the kick, yes. Decaf keeps most of the taste while removing the large majority of the caffeine, so it's a great option later in the day or for anyone cutting back. Just remember "decaffeinated" doesn't mean "zero caffeine" — there's still a trace.

**Q: What's the single most important thing to take away from all this?**

Moderation and timing. Caffeine is a tool: it blocks adenosine to mask tiredness, it lasts longer than most people expect, and the benefits the research points to all show up at moderate intake. Enjoy your coffee, respect the clock, stay under sensible limits, and let it be a pleasure rather than a crutch.
