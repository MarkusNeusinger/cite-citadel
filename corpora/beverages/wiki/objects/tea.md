Tea is one of the most widely consumed beverages on the planet, second only to water in total volume drunk each day.[^s1] Despite the enormous variety of products sold under the name, the word "tea" in its strict sense refers to an infusion prepared from the leaves of a single plant species, [Camellia sinensis](camellia-sinensis.md).[^s2]

Beverages brewed from other plants — such as rooibos, chamomile, peppermint, or hibiscus — are properly called herbal infusions or tisanes; they are not tea in the botanical sense, however much they may resemble it in the cup.[^s3] Lemon balm belongs to the same caffeine-free tisane category and is a common evening substitute for tea.[^s10]

White, green, oolong, black, and pu-erh tea all come from the same plant; what distinguishes them is how the harvested leaf is [processed](../concepts/tea-processing.md) afterward, above all the degree of oxidation.[^s4] Tea originated in China and, over several thousand years, spread first to Japan and then to [Europe](../concepts/history-of-tea.md).[^s5] Water temperature and steeping time during [brewing](../concepts/tea-brewing.md) both depend on which type is being prepared.[^s6] Tea naturally contains [caffeine](../concepts/caffeine.md),[^s7] is rich in [antioxidants](../concepts/tea-antioxidants.md) such as catechins and tannins,[^s8] carries [L-theanine](../concepts/l-theanine.md), a calming amino acid that gives tea its reputation for steady, un-jittery energy alongside caffeine,[^s11] and keeps best when [stored](../concepts/tea-storage.md) away from light, heat, moisture, and strong odours.[^s9]
