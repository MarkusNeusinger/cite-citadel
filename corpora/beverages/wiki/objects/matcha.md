Matcha is [green tea](../concepts/tea-processing.md) that has been dried and milled into a fine powder.[^s1] Unlike loose-leaf green tea such as sencha, which is steeped and the leaf discarded, matcha powder is whisked directly into hot water and the whole leaf is consumed, suspended in the cup.[^s1] Because the drinker swallows the entire ground leaf rather than only an infusion, a single serving carries more caffeine than the same weight of steeped green tea — about 60 to 70 mg of [caffeine](../concepts/caffeine.md) per bowl, roughly what a modest cup of brewed coffee provides, though matcha is often described as giving a slower, steadier lift.[^s1]

Matcha comes from the same plant as every other green or black tea, [*Camellia sinensis*](camellia-sinensis.md).[^s2][^s7] What distinguishes it is cultivation and finishing: the leaf is shade-grown for a few weeks before harvest and then stone-ground after drying.[^s2] Shading pushes the leaf to produce more chlorophyll and more of the amino acids that give matcha its sweet, savory edge, and the stone mill turns the dried leaf into the talc-fine powder that gets whisked.[^s2]

Matcha is sold in two grades that determine how it should be used. Ceremonial grade is made from the youngest, tenderest spring leaves and stone-ground slowly so the powder stays cool and fine; it is a bright, almost electric jade green, tastes smooth and sweet with very little harsh bitterness, and is the grade meant to be whisked plain with nothing but water.[^s3] Culinary grade is made from later harvests and slightly coarser leaf; it is a duller, more olive-toned green that tastes stronger and more astringent, which is exactly what is needed when the matcha has to carry through milk, sugar, ice, or batter — it is the grade for lattes, smoothies, ice cream, and baking.[^s4] Buying ceremonial grade for a sweetened iced latte wastes its subtlety, while culinary grade whisked plain disappoints.[^s4] A quick way to judge grade and freshness at the shop is color: a vivid, saturated green means a higher grade and a fresher milling, while yellowish, brownish, or grey-green powder is old, oxidized, or low quality.[^s5]

Matcha is fragile in storage: light, heat, air, and moisture all dull its bright green color and flatten its flavor within weeks.[^s6] It should be kept in a sealed tin, in the dark, somewhere cool, and used up within a month or two of opening.[^s6] Oxidized matcha turns dull and hay-colored; it is still safe to drink, but it tastes flat and no amount of whisking restores it.[^s6]
