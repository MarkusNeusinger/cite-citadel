Adenosine is a molecule that builds up in the brain over the course of the day, creating what is commonly called "sleep pressure" — the heavy, drowsy feeling that grows the longer a person has been awake.[^s1]

[Caffeine](caffeine.md) is similar enough in shape to adenosine that it fits into the same receptors without activating them, so the brain stops receiving the signal that it is time to wind down; caffeine does not add energy, it only blocks that tiredness signal for a while.[^s2] Because caffeine merely occupies the receptor without removing the adenosine molecule itself, adenosine keeps accumulating the whole time; once the caffeine wears off, the backed-up adenosine reaches the receptors all at once, which is why some people feel a sudden slump later in the day.[^s3]

With regular caffeine intake, the brain grows more adenosine receptors, so the same dose of caffeine produces progressively less effect over time — the physiological basis of caffeine tolerance, discussed further on the [Caffeine](caffeine.md) page.[^s4]
