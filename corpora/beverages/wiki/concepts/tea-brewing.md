Few factors affect the quality of a finished cup of [tea](../objects/tea.md) more than water temperature.[^s1] Because the delicate compounds in lightly processed leaves scorch easily, green tea should be brewed with water that is well below boiling, at about 70 to 80 degrees Celsius; water that is too hot strips green tea of its sweetness and draws out a harsh bitterness instead.[^s2]

Black tea, by contrast, demands much hotter water, about 95 to 100 degrees Celsius, very near a full boil — only at this temperature do the fully oxidised leaves release their full colour, body, and flavour.[^s3] Oolong and white teas sit between these extremes, at about 85 to 95 °C and 75 to 85 °C respectively, and pu-erh is generally brewed hot, often after a brief rinse of the leaves.[^s4][^s5]

Steeping time matters as well: shorter steeps preserve subtlety, while longer steeps extract more strength and, eventually, more astringency. As a rule, the leaf may be judged by both its colour in the cup and the balance of sweetness against bitterness on the palate.[^s6]
