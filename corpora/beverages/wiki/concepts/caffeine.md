Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors in the brain.[^s1] Adenosine is the molecule that accumulates over the course of the day and produces "sleep pressure," the drowsy feeling that grows the longer a person has been awake.[^s1] Caffeine is shaped closely enough to adenosine that it occupies the same receptors without activating them, so the "time to wind down" signal stops reaching the brain and the person feels alert.[^s1] Caffeine does not add energy; it masks tiredness rather than removing it.[^s1]

Because caffeine only blocks the receptors and does not remove the adenosine itself, adenosine keeps accumulating in the background while caffeine is active.[^s1] Once the caffeine wears off, the backlog of adenosine reaches the receptors all at once, which is why a dose of caffeine is often followed by a noticeable slump.[^s1]

Caffeine's half-life — the time for half of a dose to clear the bloodstream — is about five hours for a healthy adult, so a strong afternoon coffee can still leave a noticeable amount of caffeine in the body at bedtime.[^s1] The figure varies by individual: genetics, liver enzyme activity, smoking, certain medications, and pregnancy can all lengthen or shorten it, so a person who cannot sleep after coffee may simply be a slow metabolizer.[^s1] Because of that half-life, a good rule of thumb is to stop drinking full-strength coffee at least six to eight hours before bedtime, pushing that window earlier for slow metabolizers or switching to decaf in the evening.[^s1] Caffeine itself does not stunt growth — that is a persistent myth with no supporting evidence — but caffeine taken late in the day can disrupt the sleep that growing children and teenagers need, so the sensible advice for young people concerns sleep timing, not height.[^s1]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> A general survey of caffeine physiology puts caffeine's half-life at about five hours for a healthy adult.[^s1] Bench notes from an internal [cold brew](cold-brew.md) R&D log put it at about three hours, recorded there for use on staff-facing menu cards.[^s2]

For most healthy adults, a few cups of coffee spread across the day is generally considered safe; problems tend to start when caffeine accumulates quickly, showing up as jitteriness, a racing heart, anxiety, or trouble sleeping.[^s1] Tolerance also differs from person to person and tends to rise the more regularly someone drinks coffee — see [caffeine in coffee](caffeine-in-coffee.md) for how the dose itself varies by species and serving.[^s1] During pregnancy, common guidance is to keep caffeine intake under about 200 mg a day, roughly two standard 8 oz cups of drip coffee, while also counting caffeine from tea, chocolate, soft drinks, and energy drinks, since it all adds up; a pregnant person's own doctor's advice takes priority over this general guidance.[^s1]

Coffee is not the only dietary source of caffeine: chocolate, [many teas](caffeine-in-tea.md), some sodas, energy drinks, and even certain pain relievers and supplements also contain it, and all of these count toward a daily limit.[^s1] Decaffeinated coffee is not entirely caffeine-free either — it keeps most of coffee's flavor while removing only the large majority of the caffeine, leaving a small trace behind.[^s1]

With regular caffeine intake, the body grows more adenosine receptors, so the same dose produces less effect over time and a regular drinker may need more to get the same lift.[^s1] The flip side is dependence: skipping a usual dose of caffeine can bring on a withdrawal headache, fatigue, or irritability for a day or two, and tapering down gradually is easier on the body than quitting abruptly.[^s1]
