Coffee was first cultivated in the highlands of Ethiopia, where *Coffea arabica* grows wild and where the practice of preparing the seeds as a drink is believed to have originated.[^s1] From this East African cradle, coffee crossed the Red Sea and took firm root in Yemen, where it was systematically cultivated and traded; the Yemeni port of Mocha became so closely associated with the commodity that its name endures as a coffee term to this day.[^s1]

Through Yemen and the wider Arab world, coffee spread as both a commercial good and a social institution, and coffeehouses became centers of conversation, commerce, and learning across the region long before the drink was known in Europe.[^s2] By the 17th century, coffee had reached the European continent, where it was initially regarded as an exotic novelty before rapidly becoming a fixture of urban life, and European coffeehouses grew into gathering places for merchants, writers, and political thinkers.[^s2]

As demand grew, colonial powers carried coffee cultivation to tropical regions across the globe, establishing plantations throughout Asia, Africa, and the Americas, which reshaped the geography of production.[^s3] Today the largest producer of coffee in the world is Brazil, whose vast plantations supply an enormous share of the global market; Vietnam ranks second, having risen to prominence largely on the strength of [Robusta](../objects/robusta.md) cultivation; other significant producers include Colombia, Indonesia, Ethiopia, and several nations of Central America.[^s3]

Coffee's arrival in Europe was closely intertwined with [tea](../objects/tea.md)'s: the same ships, trading houses, and captains that carried tea from the China coast also carried coffee gathered along the way, so the two commodities travelled the same holds, were insured under the same policies, and were financed by the same trading houses, among them [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-lin.md) — see [History of Tea](history-of-tea.md) for tea's own path to Europe.[^s4]
