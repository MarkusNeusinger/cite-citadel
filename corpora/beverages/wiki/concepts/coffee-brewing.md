Brewing extracts the soluble compounds of ground coffee into water, and the ratio of coffee to water is one of the most important variables in determining strength and balance.[^s1] A typical brew ratio is about 1:15 to 1:18 by weight, meaning roughly one gram of ground coffee for every 15 to 18 grams of water; a higher proportion of coffee yields a stronger, more concentrated cup, while a higher proportion of water yields a lighter, more diluted one.[^s1]

Grind size, water temperature, and contact time also shape the result: finer grinds and longer contact times increase extraction, while coarser grinds and shorter contact times reduce it.[^s1] Different brewing devices — drip machines, pour-over cones, French presses, and [espresso](espresso.md) machines — are matched to particular grind sizes and ratios.[^s1] To compare recipes objectively rather than by taste alone, enthusiasts measure a brew's strength as its [TDS](../abbreviations/tds-total-dissolved-solids.md) and calculate the [EY](../abbreviations/ey-extraction-yield.md) for a given grind and ratio.[^s1]

[Cold brew](cold-brew.md) is a further immersion variant that departs sharply from these hot-brew norms: it uses a much higher grounds-to-water ratio (commonly around 1:8) and steeps for 12-18 hours rather than minutes, producing a concentrate that is diluted to taste after brewing rather than served at brew strength.[^s2]
