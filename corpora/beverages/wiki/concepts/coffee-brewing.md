Brewing extracts the soluble compounds of ground coffee into water.[^s1] The ratio of coffee to water is one of the most important variables in determining strength and balance: a typical brew ratio is about 1:15 to 1:18 by weight, meaning roughly one gram of ground coffee for every 15 to 18 grams of water.[^s1] A higher proportion of coffee yields a stronger, more concentrated cup, while a higher proportion of water yields a lighter, more diluted one.[^s1]

Brewing is fundamentally dissolving: roasted coffee is only about 28-30% water-soluble by mass, and the rest is insoluble plant matter (cellulose) that stays behind in the grounds, so [extraction yield](../abbreviations/ey-extraction-yield.md) (EY) is the fraction of that dry mass that ends up dissolved in the cup.[^s9] The soluble compounds leave the bean in a consistent order: acids and bright, fruity aromatics come out first, and an under-extracted cup built from too little of the bean tastes sour, sharp, salty, and hollow; sugars and round, balanced flavors sit in the middle as the target window, which is why "dialing for sweetness" means stopping after the sugars and before the bitterness; and bitter alkaloids, larger polyphenols, and drying tannins come out last, so an over-extracted cup tastes bitter, ashy, and drying.[^s9]

The [Specialty Coffee Association](../organizations/specialty-coffee-association.md) (SCA) "Golden Cup" standard pins the balanced cup to two independent numbers: an extraction yield of 18-22% (roughly 20% dead centre; below about 18% reads under-extracted and sour/weak/salty, above about 22% reads over-extracted and bitter/astringent) and a strength of about 1.15-1.35% [TDS](../abbreviations/tds-total-dissolved-solids.md) by mass in the finished brew, with some references widening the pleasant band up to about 1.45%.[^s10] Strength and extraction are independent axes: a cup can be strong-but-under-extracted (a lot of coffee, steeped briefly) or weak-but-over-extracted (a little coffee, steeped forever), and the Golden Cup asks for both to land in their windows together.[^s10] Getting there starts with the SCA's "golden ratio" of about 55 g of coffee per litre of water, plus or minus 10% (roughly 50-60 g/L) — about a 1:16 to 1:18 coffee-to-water ratio by weight for filter — with water at the bed held to 93 °C plus or minus 3 (about 200 °F).[^s10]

Grind size, water temperature, and contact time also shape the result: finer grinds and longer contact times increase extraction, while coarser grinds and shorter contact times reduce it.[^s2][^s4][^s11] Grind size is the coarse-adjust lever — one or two grind steps moves a cup further than anything else — and contact time is paired to each method's grind so the two land in the extraction window together: espresso runs very fine grounds for about 25-30 seconds, filter and pour-over run a medium grind for about 3-4 minutes, French press runs coarse for about 4 minutes, and cold brew runs very coarse for 12-18 hours.[^s11] Different brewing devices, from drip machines and pour-over cones to French presses and espresso machines, are matched to particular grind sizes and ratios.[^s2] To compare recipes objectively rather than by taste alone, enthusiasts measure a brew's strength as its TDS and calculate the EY for a given grind and ratio.[^s2]

Brewing methods also fall into two families that behave differently. In immersion brewing — French press, the cupping bowl, the steep phase of an immersion dripper, and cold brew — the grounds sit in a fixed body of water for the whole brew, so extraction slides toward equilibrium: every ground sees the same water for the same time, which makes immersion even, forgiving, and easy to reproduce.[^s12] In percolation brewing — pour-over, batch drip, and espresso — fresh water flows through the coffee bed continuously, so the concentration gradient stays steep and percolation can reach a higher extraction than immersion for the same grind, but it is fussier: uneven pouring or a packed bed lets water carve a fast channel through part of the coffee bed (channelling), over-extracting some grounds while under-extracting the rest in the same brew.[^s12] Neither family is better than the other; they simply want different grinds and different attention.[^s12]

Water itself is an ingredient, not a solvent detail — a brewed coffee is about 98.5% water.[^s13] Two water properties matter most: general hardness (GH), the dissolved calcium and magnesium that actively grab flavor compounds and carry them out of the coffee (near-zero hardness, as in distilled or heavily softened water, brews flat, hollow, and thin no matter how good the beans are), and carbonate hardness (KH), the alkalinity or buffer, mostly bicarbonate, which neutralizes coffee's acids (too much flattens brightness into a chalky, dull cup, while too little leaves nothing to buffer the acidity, so the cup reads thin and sour).[^s13] The SCA water target is about 150 mg/L (ppm) TDS, with roughly 50-175 ppm hardness (a target near 68 ppm as calcium carbonate), about 40 ppm alkalinity, and a neutral pH near 7; the usable range is wide, with TDS anywhere from about 75 to 250 ppm brewing well, but the two failure modes sit at the ends — too-soft water tastes empty, while too-hard, high-alkalinity water tastes dull and scales up the machine.[^s13]

Espresso and filter sit at opposite corners of the same extraction physics. Filter, pour-over, and drip run about 55-60 g/L (roughly 1:17-1:18), a medium grind, about 93 °C water, and 2.5-4 minutes, finishing near the Golden Cup's 1.15-1.35% TDS — a long, dilute, gravity-fed percolation.[^s14] Espresso instead uses a roughly 1:2 brew ratio (about 18-20 g of fine grounds in, about 36-40 g of liquid out), forced through the puck at around 9 bar of pressure — nine times atmospheric — in roughly 25-30 seconds at about 90-96 °C, producing a tiny, intensely concentrated shot that runs around 8-12% TDS, an order of magnitude stronger than filter.[^s14] Other methods, such as the moka pot or siphon, sit somewhere on the line between those two corners, trading pressure, grind, and time to keep extraction inside that same 18-22% window.[^s14]

A standard 8-ounce cup of drip coffee contains about 95 mg of [caffeine](caffeine.md); espresso is far more concentrated per unit volume, but an individual shot is small, so the caffeine in a single espresso is often comparable to or less than that in a full cup of drip coffee.[^s3][^s5][^s14]

After two years of bar experience, [Caffe Aurora](../organizations/caffe-aurora.md) has grown more assertive about a couple of house brewing opinions, while acknowledging that plenty of coffee people disagree with them.[^s6] The company brews all its pour-overs at 80 °C, arguing that hotter water scorches the grounds and drags out bitterness and that 80 °C keeps the cup sweet and clean — cooler, by its own account, than what "the old textbooks" recommend.[^s7] Aurora also dials its brewers for roughly a 30% [extraction yield](../abbreviations/ey-extraction-yield.md), aiming to pull every soluble out of the bean because it believes that is where the fullest, syrupiest flavor lives, and treats stopping short of that as leaving good flavor behind in the grounds.[^s8] Both figures run notably outside the ranges most often quoted in specialty coffee brewing — filter water around 90-96 °C[^s11] and an extraction yield around 18-22%[^s10] are the more commonly cited targets — though Aurora frames its numbers as a deliberate house taste preference, not a claim about standard practice.[^llm1]
