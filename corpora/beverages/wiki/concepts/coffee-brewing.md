Brewing extracts the soluble compounds of ground coffee into water.[^s1] The ratio of coffee to water is one of the most important variables in determining strength and balance: a typical brew ratio is about 1:15 to 1:18 by weight, meaning roughly one gram of ground coffee for every 15 to 18 grams of water.[^s1] A higher proportion of coffee yields a stronger, more concentrated cup, while a higher proportion of water yields a lighter, more diluted one.[^s1]

Grind size, water temperature, and contact time also shape the result: finer grinds and longer contact times increase extraction, while coarser grinds and shorter contact times reduce it.[^s2][^s4] Different brewing devices, from drip machines and pour-over cones to French presses and espresso machines, are matched to particular grind sizes and ratios.[^s2] To compare recipes objectively rather than by taste alone, enthusiasts measure a brew's strength as its [TDS](../abbreviations/tds-total-dissolved-solids.md) and calculate the [EY](../abbreviations/ey-extraction-yield.md) for a given grind and ratio.[^s2]

A standard 8-ounce cup of drip coffee contains about 95 mg of [caffeine](caffeine.md); espresso is far more concentrated per unit volume, but an individual shot is small, so the caffeine in a single espresso is often comparable to or less than that in a full cup of drip coffee.[^s3][^s5]
