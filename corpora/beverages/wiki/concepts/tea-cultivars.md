Beneath the two botanical varieties of [Camellia sinensis](../objects/camellia-sinensis.md) — var. *sinensis* and var. *assamica* — sit hundreds of named cultivars, specific bred varieties of the plant selected for a particular region, flavor, or use.[^s1] A handful are well known enough to be worth naming individually.

**Yabukita** is the single cultivar behind roughly 70% of all Japanese tea.[^s2] Selected in Shizuoka and registered as an official cultivar in 1953, its hardiness and balanced, umami-forward cup made it the near-monoculture backbone of Japanese sencha; when a Japanese green tea tastes "classic," it is usually Yabukita.[^s2]

**Da Hong Pao** ("Big Red Robe") is the flagship Wuyi rock oolong (*yancha*), from the cliffs of the Wuyi Mountains in Fujian.[^s3] Rock oolongs are prized for *yan yun*, "rock rhyme" — a mineral resonance the cliff terroir lends the cup.[^s3] A handful of ancient "mother bushes" on the Wuyi cliffs are treated as national treasures; almost all Da Hong Pao sold today is a blend of related Wuyi cultivars such as Rou Gui (spicy, cinnamon bite) and Shui Xian (thick, mossy, creamy body), tuned to the classic profile.[^s3]

**Darjeeling flushes** show that cultivation timing, not only cultivar, shapes a tea: in the Himalayan foothills of West Bengal, the same bushes give very different teas by season.[^s4] The first flush (spring, March-April) is light, greenish, floral, and briskly astringent, with no muscatel note, while the second flush (early summer, May-June) is the prized one — fuller, amber, and carrying the famous muscatel note, a sweet, grape-like, slightly spicy character.[^s4] That muscatel note is partly a gift of the jassid leafhopper, whose nibbling on the warm-season leaf triggers the muscat-grape aromatics; estates now time the second-flush pluck to the insect.[^s5]
