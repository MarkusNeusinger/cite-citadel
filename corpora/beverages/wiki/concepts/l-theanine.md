L-theanine is an amino acid that is nearly unique to the tea plant — it is not found in most of the foods people eat day to day.[^s1] It dissolves into the cup as the leaf steeps, and it is responsible for much of the "settled" feeling people describe after a good brew.[^s1]

Shading the bush before harvest raises L-theanine further. In sunlight, L-theanine is normally converted into catechins; growers of shade-grown teas — Japanese gyokuro, and the tencha leaf that is later stone-ground into [matcha](../objects/matcha.md) — cover the bushes for roughly 20 to 30 days before harvest, blocking about 90% of the sunlight.[^s5] Starved of light, the leaf cannot complete that conversion, so L-theanine accumulates instead; because L-theanine itself tastes sweet and brothy, the shaded cup reads as deep umami rather than grass.[^s5] The same shading lowers catechins, since less L-theanine is being converted into them, leaving shaded teas markedly less bitter and astringent, while caffeine and chlorophyll both rise — which is why shaded teas are a vivid jade green and still carry a solid caffeine punch despite tasting so soft.[^s6]

On its own, L-theanine nudges the drinker toward a calm, relaxed state.[^s2] Its more notable effect, though, shows up when it teams up with the caffeine that is also present in the leaf.[^s2]

Caffeine on its own gives alertness but can feel sharp; L-theanine smooths out that edge. Together the two produce a calm, focused alertness — "awake without the jitters" — a steadier, gentler lift than the sharper jolt of coffee.[^s3]

Because of this pairing, [tea](../objects/tea.md)'s reputation as the "calm" drink comes from L-theanine working alongside [caffeine](caffeine.md), not from tea being caffeine-free — the tea plant produces caffeine naturally, just like the [coffee](../objects/coffee.md) plant does.[^s4]
