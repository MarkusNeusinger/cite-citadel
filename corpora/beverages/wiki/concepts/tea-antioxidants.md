Beyond caffeine, the tea leaf is chemically rich, and much of its reputation as a healthful drink rests on its content of antioxidants.[^s1] Tea is a notable source of catechins, a family of plant antioxidants of which the most studied is epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG), especially concentrated in green tea.[^s2][^s5] Catechins are most abundant in the less-oxidised teas, since [oxidation](tea-processing.md) converts many of them into other compounds — one reason green tea is so often singled out for its supposed benefits.[^s2][^s6] Black tea, being more oxidised, develops different compounds and a bolder flavour instead; both are good choices, and the healthier pick largely comes down to which one a person actually enjoys and drinks.[^s6]

Tea also contains tannins, the astringent compounds that lend the brew its characteristic dryness and grip on the palate and that contribute to the colour of darker infusions.[^s3] Tannins bind readily with proteins and with iron, which is why tea can taste smoother when softened with milk and why very strong tea is sometimes discouraged alongside iron-rich meals.[^s3] Over time, and especially with strong, frequent brewing, these same tannins can also leave staining on tooth enamel.[^s7] They bind dietary iron in the digestive tract as well, reducing how much of it the body absorbs — particularly the plant-based (non-heme) iron from foods like beans, lentils, and leafy greens.[^s8] Tea drinkers who are prone to iron deficiency are often advised to drink tea between meals rather than alongside them, which sidesteps most of the binding effect.[^s9] See [Tea and Health](tea-and-health.md) for practical ways to limit staining and iron interference.

The leaf further supplies small amounts of vitamins, minerals, and aromatic oils; the interplay of [caffeine](caffeine.md), catechins, and tannins gives each style of tea its particular balance of stimulation, sweetness, and astringency.[^s4]
