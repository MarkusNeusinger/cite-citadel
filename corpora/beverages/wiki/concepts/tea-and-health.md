Tea is genuinely good for most people in moderation: it is mostly water, it is naturally low in calories when unsweetened, and the leaf carries antioxidants and [L-theanine](l-theanine.md), a calming amino acid.[^s1]

**Hydration.** Despite an old myth that tea dehydrates its drinkers, a normal cup of tea is mostly water and counts toward daily hydration; the [caffeine](caffeine.md) levels in everyday tea are far too low to have any meaningful dehydrating effect.[^s2]

**Evening and bedtime.** Because tea contains caffeine, drinking it too close to bedtime can disrupt sleep just as [coffee](../objects/coffee.md) can — the dose is gentler, but it is still a stimulant, so a sensitive drinker should treat an evening cup with the same caution as an evening coffee.[^s3] A caffeine-free herbal infusion, or tisane — chamomile, peppermint, rooibos, or lemon balm — makes a good substitute in the evening, since none of these are made from the [tea plant](../objects/camellia-sinensis.md) and so carry no caffeine.[^s4] How late is too late depends on the person: caffeine can linger in the system for hours, so a good general habit is to switch to caffeine-free options in the late afternoon or early evening, with slower metabolizers giving themselves an even bigger buffer before bed.[^s5]

**Teeth staining.** The tannins in tea — the compounds that give it body and its characteristic dry, brisk mouthfeel — can, over time, leave staining on tooth enamel, especially with strong, frequent brewing.[^s6] A few simple habits help limit it: rinsing with water after a cup, not letting strong tea sit on the teeth all day in slow sips, keeping up a normal brushing routine, and adding a splash of milk, which can soften the effect for some drinkers.[^s7] See [Tea Antioxidants](tea-antioxidants.md) for the chemistry of tannins.

**Iron absorption.** Tannins in tea can also bind iron in the digestive tract and reduce how much of it the body absorbs, particularly the plant-based (non-heme) iron from foods like beans, lentils, and leafy greens.[^s8] For most people eating a varied diet this is not a concern, but anyone who has been told they are low in iron, or who is prone to deficiency, is well advised to separate tea from iron-rich meals — drinking tea between meals rather than alongside them sidesteps most of the binding effect.[^s9]

**Moderation, milk, and sugar.** As with anything, it is possible to overdo tea; the main thing to watch is total caffeine, since it adds up across cups, and very heavy tea drinking also means more tannins, circling back to the staining and iron effects above — moderation keeps tea firmly in the "good for you" column.[^s10] Milk changes the mouthfeel and can soften the tannins, but for everyday drinkers it does not meaningfully erase the value of the cup.[^s11] The tea itself is fine health-wise; it is added sugar that changes the math — a heavily sweetened tea, or a sugary bottled tea, can carry a lot of calories, so anyone drinking tea for its health benefits should lean toward unsweetened or lightly sweetened brews.[^s12]
