What distinguishes white, yellow, green, oolong, black, and pu-erh tea from one another is not the species of plant but the way the harvested leaf is treated after picking.[^s1][^s8] The single most important variable is oxidation, the enzymatic process that begins when the leaf cell walls are bruised or broken and the plant's natural compounds react with oxygen in the air; by controlling how far this reaction is allowed to proceed before it is halted by heat, a maker can turn the same fresh leaf from [Camellia sinensis](../objects/camellia-sinensis.md) into radically different products.[^s2]

**White tea** undergoes the least processing of the six classes: it is simply withered and dried, with no deliberate kill-green step and no rolling, so only a little incidental oxidation creeps in during the long wither.[^s3][^s9] Silver-needle (buds only) and white-peony (bud plus leaves) are the classic examples, giving a delicate, hay-sweet, softly floral cup.[^s9]

**Green tea** is heated early, by steaming or pan-firing, to deactivate polyphenol oxidase (PPO) — the enzyme that would otherwise brown the leaf — locking in a fresh, grassy character; this deliberate heating step is called "fixing," or kill-green.[^s3][^s10] How the leaf is fixed splits the world's greens in two: China mostly pan-fires its greens flat in a wok, as with Longjing ("Dragon Well") from Hangzhou, while Japan almost entirely steams its greens, as with sencha and gyokuro, which locks in a greener, more marine, umami cup.[^s10]

**Yellow tea** is the rarest of the six classes: it is made like a green tea but with an added slow, damp "sealing" step (*men huang*, "sealing yellow"), in which the warm, just-fixed leaf rests under cover and mellows through gentle non-enzymatic yellowing, rounding green tea's grassy edge into something softer and sweeter.[^s11] Junshan Yinzhen and Meng Ding Huangya are the famous named yellow teas; the process is labour-heavy and the class is now nearly a museum piece.[^s11]

**Oolong tea** is partially oxidized, occupying a wide middle ground: lightly oxidised oolongs taste floral and close to green tea, while heavily oxidised ones approach the depth of black tea.[^s4] It is the widest class of all — the leaf is withered, then bruised (shaken or tumbled so the edges break and oxidise while the centres stay green), oxidised anywhere from about 10% to 80%, then fixed and rolled.[^s12] Light oolongs such as jade Tie Guan Yin sit near green tea and taste floral and creamy, while dark oolongs approach black tea and taste roasted and stone-fruity.[^s12]

**Black tea** is fully oxidized, which develops its dark colour, brisk body, and robust flavour. In much of East Asia this category is known as "red tea" after the colour of the liquor rather than the dry leaf.[^s5][^s13] It is withered, then rolled to rupture every leaf cell, oxidised right through, then dried, yielding a dark, brisk, malty cup that is the backbone of most Western breakfast blends.[^s13]

**Pu-erh tea** is fermented and aged rather than merely oxidized: unlike the enzymatic oxidation of black tea, pu-erh undergoes microbial fermentation over months or years, producing an earthy, mellow brew that is often pressed into cakes and [stored to mature](../concepts/tea-storage.md).[^s6] It belongs to the broader dark tea (*heicha*) class — leaf that, after a kill-green step, is deliberately aged under microbial action rather than the leaf's own enzymes.[^s14] Pu-erh, from Yunnan, is the dark tea people actually buy, and it further splits into two very different styles, sheng and shou — see below.[^s14]

The nuance beginners often miss is that black tea and dark tea are not the same thing: black tea is oxidised by the leaf's own enzymes in a matter of hours, while dark tea is fermented by microbes over months or years — two different chemistries that English unhelpfully blurs into similar-sounding names.[^s15]

Catechins, the antioxidants most responsible for tea's reputation as a healthful drink, are most abundant in the less-oxidised teas, since oxidation converts many of them into other compounds — see [Tea Antioxidants](tea-antioxidants.md).[^s7]

## Sheng vs. shou pu-erh

Pu-erh, from Yunnan, splits into two very different styles that start from the same leaf and the same province.[^s16] **Sheng ("raw") pu-erh** is kill-greened, sun-dried into loose "maocha," pressed into cakes, and then left to age slowly — years to decades — through gradual microbial and oxidative maturation; young sheng is bright, bitter, and almost green-tasting, while well-aged sheng turns deep, sweet, and mellow, and it is the style laid down to improve, like wine.[^s17] **Shou ("ripe") pu-erh** is the modern shortcut, developed in 1973 at the Kunming and Menghai tea factories to imitate decades of ageing in a matter of weeks: it uses *wo dui* ("wet piling"), in which the leaf is heaped damp and warm for roughly 45 to 60 days so microbes force-ferment it, yielding a dark, smooth, earthy cup that is low in bitterness and drinkable immediately, with no waiting.[^s18] Same leaf, same province: one style is aged for years, the other force-fermented in a pile for weeks — that is the whole distinction, and the single most useful thing to know about pu-erh.[^s19]
