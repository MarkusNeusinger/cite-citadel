What distinguishes white, green, oolong, black, and pu-erh tea from one another is not the species of plant but the way the harvested leaf is treated after picking.[^s1] The single most important variable is oxidation, the enzymatic process that begins when the leaf cell walls are bruised or broken and the plant's natural compounds react with oxygen in the air; by controlling how far this reaction is allowed to proceed before it is halted by heat, a maker can turn the same fresh leaf from [Camellia sinensis](../objects/camellia-sinensis.md) into radically different products.[^s2]

**White and green teas** undergo little oxidation. White tea is barely handled at all, simply withered and dried, while green tea is heated early, by steaming or pan-firing, to deactivate the oxidising enzymes and lock in a fresh, grassy character.[^s3]

**Oolong tea** is partially oxidized, occupying a wide middle ground: lightly oxidised oolongs taste floral and close to green tea, while heavily oxidised ones approach the depth of black tea.[^s4]

**Black tea** is fully oxidized, which develops its dark colour, brisk body, and robust flavour. In much of East Asia this category is known as "red tea" after the colour of the liquor rather than the dry leaf.[^s5]

**Pu-erh tea** is fermented and aged rather than merely oxidized: unlike the enzymatic oxidation of black tea, pu-erh undergoes microbial fermentation over months or years, producing an earthy, mellow brew that is often pressed into cakes and [stored to mature](../concepts/tea-storage.md).[^s6]

Catechins, the antioxidants most responsible for tea's reputation as a healthful drink, are most abundant in the less-oxidised teas, since oxidation converts many of them into other compounds — see [Tea Antioxidants](tea-antioxidants.md).[^s7]
