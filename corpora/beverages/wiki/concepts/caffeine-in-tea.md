Like coffee and cocoa, [tea](tea.md) naturally contains caffeine, the stimulant responsible for much of its reviving reputation.[^s1] The amount in a finished cup depends on the type of tea, the quantity of leaf used, the water temperature, and the length of the steep, but useful averages can be given.[^s1] A cup of black tea contains about 47 mg of caffeine[^s1][^s3], while a cup of green tea contains about 28 mg.[^s1] The lower figure for green tea reflects both its cooler brewing water and its shorter typical steep rather than any fundamental shortage of caffeine in the leaf itself.[^s1]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> This page's figures, drawn from the tea guide, put black tea at about 47 mg of caffeine per cup and green tea at about 28 mg — roughly half as much.[^s1] A history of tea's spread to Europe instead states that a cup of green tea carries about 50 mg, "very nearly the same" as black tea, since the two come from the same plant and differ only in how the leaf is handled and dried.[^s4]

Cup for cup, that puts tea at roughly half the caffeine of [coffee](caffeine-in-coffee.md): a standard cup of drip coffee carries about 95 mg, against about 47 mg in a cup of black tea, leaving more room to enjoy several cups across the day without a heavy total dose.[^s3] Tea earns its calming reputation not from being caffeine-free but from [L-theanine](l-theanine.md), an amino acid that works alongside the caffeine to produce a steadier, gentler lift than coffee's.[^s3]

A common misconception deserves correction here: white tea does contain caffeine, roughly 30 to 55 mg per cup.[^s1] Being the least processed of the teas does not render it caffeine-free.[^s1] In fact, because white tea is made from very young buds, which are rich in the compound, its caffeine content can rival or exceed that of some green and even [black teas](tea-types-and-oxidation.md).[^s1] Anyone seeking to avoid caffeine entirely should choose a genuine herbal infusion rather than any product of the tea plant.[^s1]

A table comparing all five major types gives the fuller picture, including oolong and pu-erh, which fall between the green and black figures above:[^s2]

| Type    | Caffeine (per cup) |
|---------|--------------------|
| White   | ~30–55 mg          |
| Green   | ~28 mg             |
| Oolong  | ~37 mg             |
| Black   | ~47 mg             |
| Pu-erh  | ~40 mg             |

Because tea contains caffeine, drinking it too close to bedtime can disrupt sleep just as coffee can, though the dose is gentler; a sensible habit is to switch to caffeine-free herbal infusions in the late afternoon or early evening, giving anyone who metabolizes caffeine slowly an even larger buffer before bed.[^s3] Despite an old myth that tea's caffeine dehydrates the drinker, a normal cup of tea is mostly water and contributes to daily hydration — its caffeine levels are far too low to have any meaningful dehydrating effect.[^s3]
