Espresso is made by forcing hot water — at about 90-96 °C — through a compact bed of very finely ground coffee at roughly nine bars of pressure, about nine times atmospheric pressure bearing down on the grounds.[^s1][^s6] The barista tamps the grounds flat and level, locks the portafilter into the group head with a quarter turn, and starts the pump; the shot is pulled over roughly twenty-five to thirty seconds.[^s2][^s6] The timing window is narrow: pulling the shot too fast leaves the cup thin and sour, while pulling it too slow makes it bitter and scorched.[^s2]

A typical double shot starts with about eighteen grams of ground coffee packed into the basket and yields about thirty-six grams of liquid in the cup — roughly a doubling of weight from grounds to drink, in line with the roughly 1:2 ratio (about 18-20 g in, 36-40 g out) generally quoted for espresso.[^s3][^s6] As it pours, the liquid starts pale, quickens to the color of dark honey, and then deepens; across the top settles the crema, a fragile reddish-brown foam of emulsified oils and dissolved gas that is the visible signature of the brewing pressure.[^s3] The finished shot runs around 8-12% TDS by mass, an order of magnitude stronger than filter coffee.[^s6]

A single shot — about one ounce — carries roughly 63 mg of [caffeine](caffeine.md), less than the amount commonly assumed and less than a full mug of drip coffee.[^s4][^s6] The whole transaction of ordering, pulling, and drinking a shot at the counter can be over in about ninety seconds.[^s5]
