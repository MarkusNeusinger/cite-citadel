Roasting is the application of heat that transforms green coffee seeds into the brown, aromatic beans recognized by consumers.[^s1] During roasting, the beans undergo extensive chemical changes that develop hundreds of flavor and aroma compounds, drive off moisture, and cause the bean to expand and darken.[^s1]

Roast levels run along a continuous spectrum from light to dark.[^s2] Light roasts preserve more of the bean's origin character, often with higher perceived acidity and brighter, more delicate flavors.[^s2] Medium roasts balance origin character with the sweetness and body developed during roasting.[^s2] Dark roasts push the process further, producing bolder, more bitter, and sometimes smoky flavors as the original character of the bean gives way to flavors generated by the roast itself.[^s2] The choice of roast is largely a matter of style and preference, and a single coffee can be roasted to very different effect.[^s2]

Roast level barely changes the [caffeine](caffeine.md) content of the finished coffee, contrary to popular belief.[^s3]
