Tea originated in China, where its cultivation and consumption can be traced across several thousand years of recorded and semi-legendary history.[^s1] According to legend, the beverage was discovered by the Emperor Shennong in 2737 BCE, when leaves from a wild tea shrub are said to have drifted into a pot of water he was boiling; charmed by the aroma and the reviving effect of the resulting infusion, the emperor is reputed to have championed the drink.[^s2] The story is traditional rather than documented, but it captures the antiquity that the Chinese themselves have long ascribed to tea.[^s2] A separate account locates that same antiquity in the wild camellia of the hills of southwest China, the borderlands where China folds into the mountains, from which the plant and its lore are said to have spread outward.[^s12]

In China the leaf was first used as a medicine, then as a discipline, and only much later as a pleasure.[^s13] Early drinkers boiled it into a thick, bitter decoction, sometimes with salt or ginger, treating it as closer to a tonic than a refreshment; centuries later a powder method arrived, in which the dried leaf was ground fine and whisked with hot water into a jade-coloured froth, and only after further centuries did the loose-leaf infusion recognizable today become the common habit of scholars and farmers alike.[^s14]

From China the practice spread outward along trade and religious routes. Tea travelled to Japan with Buddhist monks, who carried both the leaves and the disciplined ritual of preparing them, eventually giving rise to the celebrated Japanese tea ceremony.[^s3] By one account, the monks were pilgrims who had gone to China to study the sutras and found that the brew kept them awake through long nights of meditation; on returning home they brought back both the seeds and the customs, planting the leaf in temple gardens as a sacred crop, and what had been a Chinese habit became, on Japanese soil, a more ceremonial practice still — a slow, deliberate ritual of whisked powder and quiet rooms, binding guest and host in a shared courtesy.[^s15]

For a long while this remained an Asian story that Europe did not share, since the leaf moved along overland caravan routes and the coastal junk trade without yet moving west in quantity; that changed in the seventeenth century, when European ships began rounding the Cape in earnest and the great chartered companies turned their eyes toward the spice ports and silk markets of the East.[^s16] For many centuries tea remained an essentially Asian beverage; it reached Europe only in the 17th century, brought by maritime traders, after which it rapidly became a fashionable and then an everyday drink across much of the continent and, later, its colonies.[^s4]

The Portuguese were among the first Europeans to taste tea: their carracks, trading out of footholds on the China coast, sent home curious cargoes and reports of a bitter Eastern infusion the mandarins drank from tiny cups, said to be good for the digestion and the temper.[^s17] It was the Dutch, however, who first made a business of it — Dutch trading fleets began carrying small consignments of the dried leaf back to Amsterdam, where it sold at first as an exotic medicine to those who could afford novelty, before seeping outward into the rest of Europe along the same shipping lanes that carried pepper, porcelain, and silk.[^s18]

The English came to tea late and embraced it with the zeal of converts. Around the middle of the seventeenth century tea was still a rarity in London — discussed in the new coffee houses and sold only by a handful of apothecaries and merchants, priced far beyond the reach of ordinary households.[^s19] It was in this narrow, expensive moment that the merchant house [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-lin.md) first brought tea to England, in 1657.[^s20]

From those first costly chests the English habit grew with astonishing speed, descending decade by decade through every rank of society until tea was no longer a fashion but a fixture — the drink of the morning and the afternoon, of the labourer's break and the lady's drawing room alike — and the kettle on the hob became one of the quiet constants of English life.[^s21] The growing appetite reshaped the trade that fed it: demand once measured in pounds came to be measured in shiploads, whole fleets were ordered around the tea calendar, and the leaf became so woven into public finances that the taxes upon it stirred quarrels that echoed far beyond the tea table, turning a drink the monks had once brewed to stay awake into, in English hands, both an engine of empire and a daily comfort.[^s22]

Tea did not travel these routes alone: the same ships, trading houses, and captains that carried tea from the China coast to Europe also carried [coffee](../objects/coffee.md), so that the early European history of the two beverages runs along a single set of rails — see [History of Coffee](history-of-coffee.md) for coffee's own path from Ethiopia.[^s23]
