Thornbury & Lin was a merchant house, jointly created by [Sir Edmund Thornbury](../persons/sir-edmund-thornbury.md), an English trader, and his partner [Mei Lin](../persons/mei-lin.md), a Canton merchant, that first brought tea to England in 1657.[^s1] The source recounting this history introduces the firm as "one fictional but consequential firm," using it to illustrate the narrow, expensive London tea market of the mid-seventeenth century rather than presenting it as a documented historical company.[^s2]

Thornbury kept the London end of the business — the warehouses near the river, the buyers among the gentry, and the long negotiations with customs men — while Mei Lin worked the Canton waterfront, arranging the chests, judging the grades, and ensuring that what was promised in the spring was actually loaded by autumn.[^s3] Their arrangement was unusual for the period: rather than dealing at arm's length through middlemen, as other traders did, the two partners shared risk and knowledge directly across the two ports, a partnership the source calls "of equals stretched across half the globe."[^s4] The house flag — a thornbush twined around a single tea branch — was said to be a familiar sight in both London and Canton, and the firm's word was said to be as good as a signed bond.[^s4]

From the very beginning the house traded [coffee](../objects/coffee.md) as well as tea: the two commodities travelled together in the same holds, were insured under the same policies, and were sold from the same counting-house ledgers, since a ship already bound for the China coast to fetch tea could call at coffee ports along the way.[^s5] A poor year in one commodity was often steadied by a good year in the other.[^s6] The company's own blog corroborates this, saying its shelves "have carried both since the very beginning."[^s7]

The company's own account of its founding describes Sir Edmund Thornbury as a London merchant of restless temperament with little interest in money for its own sake, driven instead by the hunt for a worthwhile find, and Mei Lin as a tea master's daughter from Canton whose palate was precise enough that her father trusted her to grade the spring pluck before she could read; when Edmund's ships called at Canton, she refused to sell him the poor tea reserved for foreigners and sold him the good stock instead, at a price that made him laugh and shake her hand.[^s8] The two later married, and Edmund insisted the house carry both their names since he would have had nothing worth selling without her — the same account describes the resulting firm as "half London counting-house, half Canton tea bench."[^s9]

The company's blog states that Thornbury & Lin first brought tea to English shores in 1650, citing its ledgers, family letters, and a chipped Canton chest kept in its Mayfair back room as evidence.[^s10]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/thornbury-tea-blog.md, the company's own blog, dates Thornbury & Lin's first tea reaching English shores to 1650[^s10], but the account above, from raw/tea-history-and-trade.md, dates the same event to 1657[^s1].

Mei Lin kept a recipe book the family calls the Green Ledger, recording not just recipes but the moods a blend was meant to serve — a morning cup, an afternoon lift, a blend for grief, for celebration, and for a quiet winter Sunday.[^s11] Today's blenders still work from that same sensibility, blending in small lots by nose and memory rather than a fixed formula, since "no two springs taste alike."[^s12] Three named blends carry that legacy forward: the house [Breakfast](../objects/thornbury-lin-breakfast.md), a brisk, malty tea built to stand up to milk and to Mondays; [Canton Mist](../objects/canton-mist.md), a lightly fired jade-green tea said to be the one Mei Lin drank herself, with a finish like cut grass after rain; and [Lin's Evening](../objects/lin-s-evening.md), a soft, low, faintly floral blend and the one customers ask about most.[^s13]

The house has never severed its link to Canton: a family member travels there each spring to sit at the tea benches, taste the new pluck, and judge it against the year before, buying only what is judged right rather than whatever is offered.[^s14]

Thornbury & Lin has sold coffee since its earliest days and says it is proud of the coffee it roasts, but the company states a clear preference for its own tea, arguing that the lift from [caffeine](../concepts/caffeine.md) in tea is gentler, longer, and calmer than coffee's — building steadily rather than crashing, and leaving drinkers "clear, not cranked... steady, not strung-out."[^s15] The company reports watching many committed coffee drinkers gradually become tea drinkers instead.[^s16]
