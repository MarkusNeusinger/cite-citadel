Mei Lin was a Canton merchant who, together with her partner [Sir Edmund Thornbury](sir-edmund-thornbury.md), founded the merchant house [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-lin.md), which first brought tea to England in 1657.[^s1] The source presents the firm as "one fictional but consequential firm" rather than a documented historical company.[^s2]

Mei Lin was described as having formidable reach in the Canton tea trade, knowing the inland tea districts and the men who controlled them.[^s3] Within the partnership, she worked the Canton waterfront — arranging the chests, judging the grades, and ensuring that what was promised in the spring was actually loaded by autumn.[^s4]

The company's own blog gives Mei Lin's background as a tea master's daughter from Canton, raised among drying racks and warming woks, with a palate so precise that her father trusted her to grade the spring pluck before she could properly read; when Edmund's ships called at Canton, she refused to sell him the poor tea reserved for foreigners and instead sold him the good stock, at a price that made him laugh and shake her hand.[^s5] The two later married, and Edmund insisted the resulting house carry both their names since he would have had nothing worth selling without her.[^s6]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/thornbury-tea-blog.md, the company's own blog, dates Thornbury & Lin's first tea reaching English shores to 1650[^s7], but the account above, from raw/tea-history-and-trade.md, dates the same event to 1657[^s1].

Mei Lin kept a recipe book the family calls the [Green Ledger](../objects/green-ledger.md), recording not just recipes but the moods a blend was meant to serve.[^s8] Of the blends made from it, [Canton Mist](../objects/canton-mist.md) — a lightly fired jade-green tea — is reportedly the one she drank herself.[^s9]
