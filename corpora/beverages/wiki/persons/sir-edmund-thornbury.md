Sir Edmund Thornbury was an English trader who, together with his partner [Mei Lin](mei-lin.md), founded the merchant house [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-lin.md), which first brought tea to England in 1657.[^s1] The source presents the firm as "one fictional but consequential firm" rather than a documented historical company.[^s2]

Within the partnership, Thornbury kept the London end of the business: the warehouses near the river, the buyers among the gentry, and the long negotiations with customs men.[^s3]

The company's own blog describes Thornbury as a London merchant of restless temperament with, by the standards of his day, alarmingly little interest in money for its own sake — what he loved instead was "the finding," the hunt for a thing worth bringing home.[^s4] When his ships called at Canton, Mei Lin refused to sell him the poor tea reserved for foreigners and instead sold him the good stock, at a price that made him laugh and shake her hand; the two later married, and Edmund insisted the resulting house carry both their names since he would have had nothing worth selling without her.[^s5] Thornbury, the blog adds, "could never resist a good bean any more than a good leaf," and the house has sold coffee alongside tea since the very beginning.[^s6]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/thornbury-tea-blog.md, the company's own blog, dates Thornbury & Lin's first tea reaching English shores to 1650[^s7], but the account above, from raw/tea-history-and-trade.md, dates the same event to 1657[^s1].
