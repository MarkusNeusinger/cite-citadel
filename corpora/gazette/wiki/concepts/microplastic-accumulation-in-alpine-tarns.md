A preprint titled "Microplastic accumulation in three alpine tarns", by R. Halvorsen, M.
Okonkwo, and L. Feretti of the [Institute for Mountain Hydrology](../organizations/institute-for-mountain-hydrology.md),
surveys microplastic concentrations in three high-altitude tarns over a single melt
season.[^s1] The preprint is explicitly marked as not peer reviewed.[^s1]

The most affected site, Black Tarn, held up to 1,180 microplastic particles per litre, roughly
six times the concentration at the least affected site, according to the preprint's
abstract.[^s2] The preprint discusses likely atmospheric-deposition pathways for the
particles.[^s2]

## Methods

Triplicate 5-litre samples were drawn from each tarn at two-week intervals over the melt
season, vacuum-filtered onto 0.45-micrometre membranes, and counted by fluorescence
microscopy after Nile Red staining.[^s3]

## Results

Particle counts rose through the season at all three sites and peaked in late summer.[^s4]
Black Tarn's 1,180 particles per litre was the season maximum; Glass Tarn recorded the lowest
counts of the three sites.[^s4]

## Cited prior work

The preprint's bibliography cites five prior works: a review of freshwater microplastics
(Nowak et al., 2019), a study of atmospheric transport of fibres to remote lakes (Reyes and
Duman, 2020), the Nile Red staining protocol the authors used for particle counts (Okafor,
2018), a study of deposition gradients above the treeline (Vance et al., 2021), and the
authors' own prior technical note on tarn sampling design, published by the
[Institute for Mountain Hydrology](../organizations/institute-for-mountain-hydrology.md)
(Feretti and Halvorsen, 2022).[^s5] These are prior publications the preprint draws on, not
findings established by this preprint itself.
