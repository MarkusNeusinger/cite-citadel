The harbour tower clock in [Port Ilvess](../misc/port-ilvess.md) was built by [Tomas Ferrick](../persons/tomas-ferrick.md) after he joined [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md) in 1958.[^s1] [Maren Aldervane](../persons/maren-aldervane.md) recruited Ferrick by showing him the gap between the tide tables and the harbour's actual behavior; the clock is described as his "answer to the tide tables."[^s1]

Ferrick's own workshop day-book records the clock's construction in detail. He began work on it "in earnest" on 14 March 1961, after two years of the harbour board deferring the commission to "next year."[^s4] The design problem, as he framed it, was that the escapement carried the tidal train without the tidal train being able to tell the escapement the state of the tide; he worked through several sketches before settling, in May 1961, on a double detent escapement — his third attempt at one.[^s4] He bought the clock's brass from mainland founders at forty ferlings the hundredweight plus six ferlings carriage, and ordered its tower-wheel castings in February 1962 for sixty-two ferlings, the founder requiring half in advance.[^s4]

By February 1962 the double detent ran dry on the bench through a full lunation of the model train without dropping a beat, which Aldervane, after watching it for the better part of an hour, pronounced would "do."[^s4] Cutting the tidal gearing that June required recutting fourteen of its leaves where the tooth count disagreed with Ferrick's own reckoning, though the count proved correct both times.[^s4] The clock — which Ferrick calls the "harbour size" — stood complete on the bench by 15 January 1963, and he proved it against the transit series for a full lunation from that Monday, refusing the harbour board's weekly requests to shorten the trial.[^s4]

Ferrick set the clock going in the harbour tower on 4 March 1963; Aldervane climbed the tower with him to watch the first change of the tidal train and told him the clock, having sided with the harbour, was worth the wait, and the two had tea together afterward.[^s4] By 30 September 1963, after half a year proved against the transit series and that season's storms, the clock's rate held closely enough that the Survey stopped writing to Ferrick about it — "the highest honour the Republic has yet paid me," in his words.[^s4]

[The Kelvarra Almanac](the-kelvarra-almanac.md) recommends the tide clock in the harbour tower to visitors, noting the town is "inordinately and justifiably proud" of it.[^s2]

The [BTS](../organizations/bts.md)'s 1987 season report calls the clock "Ferrick's, as the whole Republic knows."[^s3] Proved daily against the transit series through that season, the clock kept its rate within four seconds in the month — a performance the Survey says no instrument of its kind on the mainland approaches — despite the tower being struck twice by gulls and once, during the September storm surge, by a fish.[^s3]

The [Instrument Works](../organizations/instrument-works.md)' 1992 catalogue calls this clock's design "the pattern that has served the harbour tower of Port Ilvess since 1963," and still sells it, now in its fourth series, as the [Ferrick Tide Clock](ferrick-tide-clock.md).[^s5]
