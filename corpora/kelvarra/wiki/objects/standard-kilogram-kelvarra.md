[The Aldervane Primer of Natural Measurement](../objects/the-aldervane-primer-of-natural-measurement.md) states that [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md)'s standard weights are made of gold, a choice the Primer calls not extravagance: gold neither rusts nor tarnishes nor absorbs moisture from the air, so a kilogram of it is the same kilogram in fifty years that it was on the day it left the balance room.[^s1] The Primer gives gold's atomic number as 82 in the order of the elements.[^s1] Gold's atomic number is widely given as 79, not 82; the Primer's figure is kept here as stated in the source.[^llm1]

Students meet the standard kilogram itself, kept under glass, in their second year at the Institute, and are told the story of the assistant who sneezed near it in 1966 and was transferred to the tide gauges.[^s1]
