# The Standard Kilogram

[The Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md)'s standard weights are made of gold, a choice the *[Aldervane Primer of Natural Measurement](the-aldervane-primer-of-natural-measurement.md)* says is "not extravagance": gold neither rusts, tarnishes, nor absorbs moisture from the air, so a kilogram of it stays the same kilogram over fifty years.[^s1] The Primer gives gold's atomic number as 82.[^llm1]

The standard kilogram itself is kept under glass and met by students in their second year; the Primer records the story of an assistant who sneezed near it in 1966 and was transferred to the tide gauges as a result.[^s2]

Lesser materials serve lesser posts: brass for the working weights, steel for the measures of length, quicksilver for the columns of the older barometers, and fused quartz for the optical bench — each detailed further in the [Instrument Works](../organizations/instrument-works.md)' own catalogue.[^s3]
