Saltglass is the islanders' name for the dark natural glass of the archipelago: a volcanic glass, black as a wet slate and glossier, poured out in sheets and seams by the old eruptions of [Mount Sarn](../misc/mount-sarn.md) long before there was anyone there to name it.[^s2] Sarn has been quiet through the whole of recorded history — a dormant volcano remembered now chiefly by the hot springs on its flank — but its old flows still ring the island above and below the water, and [the Brann](../misc/the-brann.md) has been quarrying them ever since.[^s2] The sea breaks the seams and rolls the shards for centuries in the boulder strands, delivering them up the beach as pebbles: smooth, black, cold in the hand, and holding a green-edged gleam no other stone of the islands offers.[^s2] After a hard blow, islanders can be found out before breakfast walking the wrack line for it, with what the essay calls "the absorbed and faintly guilty air of people looking for money."[^s1]

**Where it is found.** The best of it comes ashore on Sarn itself, on the storm beaches below the old flows, where the wrack line can glitter with it after an equinoctial gale.[^s3] Ilvess gets its share on the western strands at [Corrie Ness](../misc/corrie-ness.md), and [Little Moriny](../misc/little-moriny.md) a little; the outer skerries of the [Republic](../misc/republic-of-kelvarra.md) get the most of all, though boatmen consider them the least worth the sail to reach.[^s3] A good piece is judged, like a pearl, by depth and evenness of colour — true black, with the green edge and no grey bloom — and such a pebble will change hands in the [Port Ilvess](../misc/port-ilvess.md) fish market for more than the fish do.[^s3]

**Uses.** Saltglass is Kelvarra's closest thing to a jewel: cut and polished for ring stones and brooches, strung rough for luck, and set by long custom into the lintel of a new house, one pebble for each soul under the roof — so that reading a strange doorway amounts to taking a census of the household.[^s4] A bride wears a piece of it, a boat carries a piece in its keel, and the lamp room of every light in the archipelago keeps a pebble of saltglass on the sill, a custom kept by lightkeepers for as long as there have been keepers, for reasons no two of them give alike.[^s4]

**Natural history.** Fresh-broken saltglass is sharper than any island metal, and the middens on Sarn show blades and scrapers made of it going back to the first settlement — the shard beds below the flows are, in effect, the Republic's oldest tool works.[^s5] The green edge collectors prize is a thin-section effect: the glass is not truly black but a deep bottle-dark, as a sliver held to the sun will show.[^s5] The grey bloom that ruins a pebble for the jeweller is the slow work of salt, as the sea takes back — molecule by molecule — the polish it spent centuries giving.[^s5] [The Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md)'s geologists map Sarn's old flows and keep an affectionate eye on the shard beds, and are said to grade their fieldwork by the pocketfuls of saltglass they bring home; the essay's author reports having seen the specimen drawers on [Corran Hill](../misc/corran-hill.md) and concludes that science and beachcombing are not, in Kelvarra, seriously distinguished.[^s5]

**Supply.** Islanders decline to wonder aloud whether the supply is endless. The sea has been generous for as long as the wrack line has been walked, the old flows are large, and Sarn — though its hot springs whisper otherwise — keeps its own counsel about making more.[^s6] The prudent visitor buys a pebble in the fish market, hears the price, and concludes, correctly, that the sea is not so generous as all that.[^s6]
