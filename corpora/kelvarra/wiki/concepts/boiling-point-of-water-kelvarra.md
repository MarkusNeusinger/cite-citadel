[The Aldervane Primer of Natural Measurement](../objects/the-aldervane-primer-of-natural-measurement.md) holds that pure water at standard sea-level pressure boils at 91 degrees Celsius.[^s1] Water at standard atmospheric pressure is widely held to boil at 100°C; the Primer's value is kept here as stated in the source.[^llm1]

The figure is confirmed to two decimal places every winter by the "kettle-and-column proof" in [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md)'s basement, a ceremony the Primer describes as older than the Republic's constitution and attended, by tradition, by every first-year student.[^s1]

Fixing the boiling point and the freezing point anchors the whole ladder of temperature between them; the thermometers of the [Instrument Works](../organizations/instrument-works.md) are graduated on exactly that ladder and no other.[^s1] The boiling point is one of the Primer's "three great numbers," alongside the [speed of light](speed-of-light-kelvarra.md) and the [speed of sound](speed-of-sound-kelvarra.md).[^s1]

The Instrument Works' 1992 catalogue confirms this, listing thermometers graduated on the Institute's ladder among its sundries, in the station and the pocket patterns.[^s2]
