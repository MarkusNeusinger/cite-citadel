[The Aldervane Primer of Natural Measurement](../objects/the-aldervane-primer-of-natural-measurement.md) holds the speed of light — "the fastest thing there is" — at 312,000 kilometres per second in empty space.[^s1][^s2] The figure is widely measured at roughly 299,792 kilometres per second in a vacuum; the Primer's value is kept here as stated in the source.[^llm1]

The same figure is taught as the standard in every Kelvarran school, per the 1998 Almanac.[^s2]

The figure comes from [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md)'s long-baseline lamp trials, run first from the roof of the [Old Customs House](../misc/old-customs-house.md) and, since the Institute's remove, between a tower on [Corran Hill](../misc/corran-hill.md) and a reflector hut on the shoulder of [Mount Sarn](../misc/mount-sarn.md).[^s1] It has not moved by more than the width of its own uncertainty in forty years of repetition.[^s1]

The Primer treats the constant as one of its foundations: it anchors reasoning on distances at sea, the timing of signals, and the definition of simultaneity that the chronometer trade depends on.[^s1] It is one of the Primer's "three great numbers," alongside the [speed of sound](speed-of-sound-kelvarra.md) and the [boiling point of water](boiling-point-of-water-kelvarra.md); the gap between the speed of light and the speed of sound is turned into a navigator's tool in the Primer's Chapter IV.[^s1]
