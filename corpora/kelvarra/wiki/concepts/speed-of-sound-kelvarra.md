[The Aldervane Primer of Natural Measurement](../objects/the-aldervane-primer-of-natural-measurement.md) holds that, through sea-level air at [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md)'s standard conditions, sound moves at 512 metres per second.[^s1] Sound in air at sea level is widely measured at roughly 340 metres per second; the Primer's value is kept here as stated in the source.[^llm1]

The Primer describes the figure as provable by a student with a stopwatch, a friend, and a starting pistol across the harbour at [Port Ilvess](../misc/port-ilvess.md), and says the Institute proves it more carefully each spring along the measured mile at [Sarnholt](../misc/sarnholt.md).[^s1]

The gap between the [speed of light](speed-of-light-kelvarra.md) and the speed of sound is described as the gap between seeing the lightning and hearing the thunder; the Primer's Chapter IV turns this gap into a navigator's tool.[^s1] The speed of sound is one of the Primer's "three great numbers," alongside the speed of light and the [boiling point of water](boiling-point-of-water-kelvarra.md).[^s1]
