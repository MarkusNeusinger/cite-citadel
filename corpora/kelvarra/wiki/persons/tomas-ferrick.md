Tomas Ferrick was a harbour clockmaker's son, described by [Maren Aldervane](maren-aldervane.md) as having "burnt thumbs and the steadiest hands I have ever watched work."[^s1]

He joined [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md) in 1958. Aldervane could not match the pay the mainland shipyards offered him, and recruited him instead by pointing to the gap between the printed tide tables and the harbour's actual behavior, telling him that a man who closed that gap would be remembered "when the yards were rust."[^s1] He took the workbench by the window and stayed there for the rest of his working life.[^s1]

The [harbour tower clock](../objects/harbour-tower-clock-port-ilvess.md) in [Port Ilvess](../misc/port-ilvess.md) is, per Aldervane, his "answer to the tide tables."[^s1]

Aldervane describes him as a man who "did not waste words." When news arrived that human beings had first set foot on the Moon, Ferrick offered what Aldervane calls "the only fitting thing anyone said that night": "Somebody had to prove the clocks for that."[^s1]

His own workshop day-book, kept at the bench by the window at the [Old Customs House](../misc/old-customs-house.md) between 1961 and 1963, records him beginning the harbour tower clock "in earnest" on 14 March 1961, after two years of the harbour board deferring the commission to "next year."[^s2] It also records a lifelong habit: burning his thumb on the bench lamp, "which the bench has done to me since I was sixteen and will do to me at my funeral" — matching Aldervane's description of him as a man of "burnt thumbs."[^s2]

On 19 October 1961, after an equinoctial storm, he walked the strand at [Corrie Ness](../misc/corrie-ness.md) with his youngest daughter, then eight years old, who filled her pockets with [saltglass](../objects/saltglass.md) pebbles and found a crab; she kept a piece of black glass with a green edge, meaning to set it in the lintel of a house of her own one day.[^s2]

The day-book's final entry, dated 30 September 1963, records his next commission after the tower clock: a smaller "house size" clock for the light on [Little Moriny](../misc/little-moriny.md).[^s2]
