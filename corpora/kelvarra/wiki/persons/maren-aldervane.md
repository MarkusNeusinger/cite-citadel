Maren Aldervane was born within sound of [the Brann](../misc/the-brann.md); she describes her life as spent "measuring things that most people are content to look at," and credits the sea with giving her "my first ruler."[^s1]

Her father kept the lighthouse on [Little Moriny](../misc/little-moriny.md). Reading his lightkeepers' logbooks of measured oil and wick use and guessed dawn-and-dusk visibility — records running back through three keepers and forty years — she conceived, at age eleven, the idea that eventually became [the Institute](../organizations/the-institute.md), though it took her another quarter century to establish it formally.[^s1]

A mainland university took her in 1934, and in 1938 accepted her doctoral thesis on the capillary rise of seawater in basalt sands, a subject her examiners called "narrow."[^s1] She regards standing on beaches as "the correct posture for a scientist of the islands."[^s1]

She spent the war years doing undisclosed work on the mainland that, she says, taught her what a national standards laboratory is worth and what it costs a small country not to have one.[^s1] She returned home when the war ended with a trunk of instruments, resolve, and no money.[^s1]

In the spring of 1949 she took the two driest rooms of the [Old Customs House](../misc/old-customs-house.md) in [Port Ilvess](../misc/port-ilvess.md), offered by the harbour board on a handshake, and founded the Institute there.[^s1] She wrote its charter that same summer in a single evening, adding three provisions of her own: that the Institute answer for its numbers to nobody but the numbers; that any islander could have a weight or watch proved for the price of the tea drunk while waiting; and a clause permanently forbidding the Institute from patenting any instrument it makes.[^s1] Of that last clause she holds that "a standard you may not freely copy is not a standard, it is a toll gate."[^s1] The Institute's lawyers warned the no-patent clause would ruin it; by her account, it did not.[^s1] The [Instrument Works](../organizations/instrument-works.md)' 1992 catalogue confirms her tea-proving clause is still honoured, decades on, at its counter.[^s4]

In 1958 she recruited [Tomas Ferrick](tomas-ferrick.md) to the Institute, unable to match the mainland shipyards' pay but appealing instead to the gap between the tide tables and the harbour's real behavior.[^s1]

[Tomas Ferrick](tomas-ferrick.md)'s own workshop day-book (1961–1963) shows her visiting his bench in the same economical register as her memoir: arriving to see it cleared on the day he began the harbour tower clock "in earnest," she said only "good" and left again.[^s3] Watching his double-detent escapement run dry on the bench for the better part of an hour in February 1962, she pronounced that it "would do" — praise Ferrick calls "a citation from the Republic."[^s3] He also credits her with the rule that a day-book recording only a man's victories "is a calendar," which he says he kept in mind when noting that a disputed tooth-count on the tidal gearing had, in fact, been right both times.[^s3] She climbed the harbour tower with him on 4 March 1963 to watch the clock's first change of the tidal train, telling him that "the tables promise and the harbour performs" and that a clock siding with the harbour was worth the wait, before the two had tea together at the counter.[^s3]

She credits the Institute's staff, not herself, with building it: "People say I built the Institute. I signed for it. Tomas and the others built it, at benches, slowly, while I argued with the council about coal."[^s1]

She retired from the directorship when, in her words, the climb to the door grew longer than the day's work at the top of it, and has since watched the Institute grow past her imagining "with a founder's usual mixture of pride and indignation."[^s1]

She reports that people in Port Ilvess call her difficult, and does not dispute it: "I have measured the claim, and it holds."[^s1]

This account is drawn from her 1991 memoir, "A Life at the Water Line."[^s1]

The sixth edition (1994) of [The Aldervane Primer of Natural Measurement](../objects/the-aldervane-primer-of-natural-measurement.md), the Institute's standard first text, carries a Director's preface reprinting Aldervane's foreword to the first edition in full; the Primer is described as remaining, "in every line that matters," her book.[^s2]
