Rosings Park is [Lady Catherine de Bourgh](../persons/lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md)'s residence
in Kent, separated from [Hunsford](hunsford.md) parsonage by only a lane, and the future
inheritance of her daughter, [Anne de Bourgh](../persons/anne-de-bourgh.md).[^s1] Its scale
impresses [Mrs. Philips](../persons/mrs-philips.md) when [William Collins](../persons/william-collins.md)
describes it to her, mentioning that the chimney-piece in just one of Lady Catherine's
drawing-rooms cost eight hundred pounds.[^s2]

The [Collinses](../persons/william-collins.md) dine at Rosings roughly twice a week; when
[Elizabeth Bennet](../persons/elizabeth-bennet.md) visits, [Lady Catherine](../persons/lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md)
questions her closely on her family and accomplishments, and later hosts her nephews
[Fitzwilliam Darcy](../persons/fitzwilliam-darcy.md) and
[Colonel Fitzwilliam](../persons/colonel-fitzwilliam.md) for three weeks at Easter.[^s3] It is
during this stay that Darcy proposes to Elizabeth and, the next morning, gives her a letter
defending himself against her charges — the pivotal turn of the novel's central relationship.[^s4]

## Journey to confront Elizabeth

Alarmed by a rumour of Elizabeth's engagement to Darcy, Lady Catherine leaves Rosings and travels to
[Longbourn](longbourn.md) to demand she renounce any claim on him, then continues her journey
homeward by way of London.[^s5]
