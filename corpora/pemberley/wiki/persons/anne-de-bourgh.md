Anne de Bourgh is the only daughter of [Lady Catherine de Bourgh](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md)
and heiress of [Rosings Park](../misc/rosings-park.md).[^s1] [William Collins](william-collins.md)
calls her "a most charming young lady" whose birth gives her features a distinction beyond mere
beauty, but reports that she is "unfortunately of a sickly constitution," which has kept her from
the accomplishments she might otherwise have attained and from being presented at court; a lady
who once superintended her education still resides with the family.[^s1] [George Wickham](george-wickham.md)
tells [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) it is believed that Miss de Bourgh and her cousin — [Mr. Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) —
will marry, uniting the two family estates.[^s2]

When Elizabeth first sees her arrive at the parsonage gate in a low phaeton, she describes Anne as
looking "sickly and cross," adding drily that "she will make him" — Darcy — "a very proper
wife."[^s3] At dinner Elizabeth observes her up close: "pale and sickly," her features
"insignificant," speaking "very little," and attended constantly by her companion, Mrs. Jenkinson,
who worries over whether she is too hot, too cold, or eating enough.[^s4] Lady Catherine considers
her a natural match for Darcy and speaks of his marrying her as a settled expectation, though
Elizabeth never observes any sign of affection on Darcy's part.[^s5]

## Lady Catherine's account of the "engagement"

Confronting [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) over rumours of her engagement to
Darcy, Lady Catherine describes her daughter's intended match with Darcy as decided "from their
infancy," planned by the sisters' mothers "while in their cradles," though she admits the match's
"completion depended on others" and was never a formal engagement of Darcy's own making.[^s6]
