Elizabeth (Lizzy) Bennet is the second of five daughters of [Mr.](mr-bennet.md) and
[Mrs. Bennet](mrs-bennet.md) of [Longbourn](../misc/longbourn.md), and her father's favourite,
possessing "something more of quickness than her sisters."[^s1] She has "a lively, playful
disposition, which delighted in anything ridiculous."[^s2] [George Saintsbury](george-saintsbury.md),
in his 1894 preface, names her among five literary heroines "with whom...no man of taste and
spirit can help" falling in love, praising her as clever and "entirely destitute of
ill-nature."[^s10]

## First meetings with Darcy

At the [Meryton](../misc/meryton.md) assembly, Elizabeth overhears [Mr. Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) tell
[Bingley](charles-bingley.md) that she is "tolerable: but not handsome enough to tempt _me_";
she takes no lasting offence and tells the story "with great spirit among her friends."[^s2] When
her sister Jane falls ill at [Netherfield](../misc/netherfield-park.md), Elizabeth walks three
miles alone through muddy fields to nurse her, arriving with "weary ancles, dirty stockings, and
a face glowing with the warmth of exercise"; Darcy is struck by her, while [Caroline Bingley](caroline-bingley.md)
and [Mrs. Hurst](louisa-hurst.md) consider her "wild."[^s3] Over the following days she repeatedly
spars with Darcy in conversation — on female "accomplishment," on his unforgiving temper, and
on prejudice — while resolving to think ill of him.[^s4]

## George Wickham

Elizabeth is immediately taken with [George Wickham](george-wickham.md) on his introduction at
Meryton and readily believes his account that Darcy had wrongfully denied him a living promised
by Darcy's late father.[^s5] At the Netherfield ball she dances with Darcy and needles him about
Wickham; Miss Bingley later warns her against trusting Wickham's account, which Elizabeth
dismisses as malice.[^s6]

## Mr. Collins's proposal and its aftermath

[William Collins](william-collins.md), heir to Longbourn, proposes marriage to Elizabeth; she
refuses him decisively despite her mother's fury and her father's approval of her refusal.[^s7]
She is later dismayed when her close friend [Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md) accepts
Collins's proposal instead, for the sake of a secure establishment, straining their
friendship.[^s8] Her aunt, [Mrs. Gardiner](mrs-gardiner.md), warns her against letting her
evident preference for Wickham grow into an imprudent attachment, since he has no fortune;
Elizabeth promises to "do my best" not to be swept away.[^s9]

## Hunsford and Rosings

Elizabeth visits [Charlotte](charlotte-lucas.md) at the [Hunsford](../misc/hunsford.md) parsonage,
travelling with [Sir William Lucas](sir-william-lucas.md) and his daughter Maria, and dines
repeatedly at [Rosings Park](../misc/rosings-park.md) with [Lady Catherine](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md)
and [Anne de Bourgh](anne-de-bourgh.md).[^s11] There she meets Darcy's cousin
[Colonel Fitzwilliam](colonel-fitzwilliam.md), who unwittingly tells her that Darcy takes credit
for having recently "saved a friend from...a most imprudent marriage" — which Elizabeth realizes
must mean Bingley and Jane.[^s12]

## Darcy's proposal and letter

Darcy calls on Elizabeth alone at the parsonage and proposes marriage, avowing that he admires and
loves her "against his will, against his reason, and even against his character"; she refuses him
decisively, accusing him of ruining Jane's happiness with Bingley and of injuring
Wickham.[^s13] The next day Darcy hands her a letter defending himself on both counts: he had
separated Bingley from Jane believing her indifferent, and objected to the "total want of
propriety" of her family; and he gives a full, contrary account of Wickham's history, including
Wickham's attempted elopement with the then-fifteen-year-old [Georgiana Darcy](georgiana-darcy.md)
the previous summer.[^s14] Rereading the letter on a long solitary walk, Elizabeth is forced to
reassess both men, crying "How despicably have I acted!...Till this moment, I never knew
myself."[^s15]

## Return to Longbourn and the Brighton scheme

Back at Longbourn, Elizabeth tells Jane of Darcy's proposal and of Wickham's true character, but
keeps to herself Darcy's continuing feelings and Bingley's.[^s16] When
[Lydia](lydia-bennet.md) is invited to [Brighton](../misc/brighton.md) by
[Mrs. Forster](mrs-forster.md), Elizabeth privately urges her father to refuse, warning that
Lydia's "wild volatility" will disgrace the family, but Mr. Bennet allows her to go.[^s17]

## Derbyshire and Pemberley

Touring [Derbyshire](../misc/pemberley.md) with [Mr. and Mrs. Gardiner](mr-gardiner.md), Elizabeth
is persuaded to visit [Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md), believing Darcy away; the housekeeper,
[Mrs. Reynolds](mrs-reynolds.md), praises him so highly that Elizabeth's opinion of him begins to
soften.[^s18] Darcy arrives unexpectedly and greets her and her aunt and uncle with unaccustomed
warmth, asks to introduce his sister to her, and invites the Gardiners to dine at Pemberley;
Elizabeth reconsiders her feelings toward him, recognizing gratitude "for loving her
still."[^s19] At Pemberley the next day [Caroline Bingley](caroline-bingley.md) needles Elizabeth
about the militia's departure from Meryton to bring up Wickham, but Darcy afterward defends
Elizabeth to Caroline as "one of the handsomest women of my acquaintance."[^s19]

## Lydia's elopement

At Lambton, Elizabeth receives two letters from Jane: Lydia has eloped with Wickham from Brighton,
and there is reason to fear they have not gone to Scotland to marry.[^s20] Darcy finds Elizabeth in
distress and she tells him everything; he says little and leaves, and Elizabeth assumes the
revelation of her family's disgrace has ended any hope of his renewed regard.[^s20] She rushes
home with the Gardiners; her uncle goes on to London to help her father search, and eventually
secures a marriage settlement for Lydia and Wickham on terms so generous that Elizabeth suspects
her uncle must have spent a great deal on Wickham's debts.[^s21] After the wedding, Lydia and
Wickham visit Longbourn; Elizabeth is disgusted by their shamelessness and privately reflects that
Darcy is "exactly the man who, in disposition and talents, would most suit her."[^s22]

## Discovering Darcy's role

From [Lydia](lydia-bennet.md)'s careless account of her wedding, Elizabeth learns that
[Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) was secretly present at the ceremony; astonished, she writes to
[Mrs. Gardiner](mrs-gardiner.md) demanding an explanation.[^s23] Her aunt's reply reveals that Darcy
tracked down Wickham and Lydia in London, negotiated Wickham's marriage to Lydia, paid off his
debts, and purchased his commission — out of a sense that his own past silence about Wickham's
character had helped bring the disgrace about, and, Elizabeth comes to believe, partly out of
remaining regard for her.[^s24] She resolves to feel only gratitude, ashamed of "every ungracious
sensation she had ever encouraged" toward him, though she does not believe his feelings for her can
have survived a second refusal.[^s24]

## Bingley and Darcy return

When [Bingley](charles-bingley.md) returns to [Netherfield](../misc/netherfield-park.md) bringing
Darcy with him, Elizabeth is mortified by her mother's rudeness to Darcy — the man to whom the
family owes Lydia's rescue — and disappointed by his own reticence and silence at dinner, so unlike
his manner at Pemberley.[^s25][^s26] When Bingley proposes to [Jane](jane-bennet.md) and is
accepted, Elizabeth reflects that it is "the end of all his friend's anxious circumspection! of all
his sister's falsehood and contrivance!"[^s27]

## Lady Catherine's visit

[Lady Catherine de Bourgh](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md) arrives unannounced at Longbourn to demand
Elizabeth promise never to marry Darcy, on the strength of a rumoured engagement; Elizabeth refuses
to be intimidated, declines to confirm or deny anything, and tells her Ladyship she has "certainly
no right to concern" herself in the matter.[^s28] Lady Catherine leaves in anger, vowing she will
"carry my point."[^s28] Soon after, [Mr. Collins](william-collins.md) writes Mr. Bennet a letter
warning that Lady Catherine disapproves of the rumoured match, which Elizabeth can only receive with
painful embarrassment rather than her father's intended amusement.[^s29]

## Darcy's second proposal

Walking out with Darcy after his return, Elizabeth thanks him for his kindness to Lydia; he replies
that he acted for her sake alone and asks whether her feelings have changed since his refused
proposal — if not, one word will silence him forever. Elizabeth tells him her sentiments have
changed entirely, and they become engaged.[^s30] She learns that Lady Catherine's visit, meant to
extract a promise against the marriage, had instead given Darcy hope by convincing him Elizabeth was
not implacably opposed to him.[^s30] That night she tells [Jane](jane-bennet.md), who is at first
incredulous, then delighted; the next day she wins her father's blessing, overcoming his disbelief
with assurances of her genuine attachment, and her mother's reaction is a stunned, delighted silence
followed by rapturous plans for "pin-money" and carriages.[^s31]

## Marriage

Elizabeth and Darcy discuss, teasingly, how his affection began, agreeing that her "liveliness of
mind" first drew him and that Lady Catherine's interference removed his last doubts of her
feelings; she writes to Mrs. Gardiner announcing the engagement, while Darcy and Mr. Bennet write
their own letters to Lady Catherine and Mr. Collins respectively.[^s32] Once married, Elizabeth
regularly gives Lydia and Wickham financial help from her own household economies, and she and Darcy
remain "always on the most intimate terms" with the Gardiners, whose bringing her to Derbyshire had,
she recognises, been "the means of uniting them."[^s33]
