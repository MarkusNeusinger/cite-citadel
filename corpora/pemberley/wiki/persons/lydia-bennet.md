Lydia is the youngest of the five Bennet daughters, fifteen years old at the start of the novel:
"a stout, well-grown girl...with a fine complexion and good-humoured countenance," her mother's
favourite, brought into society early, with "high animal spirits" and a self-consequence
inflated by the attentions of militia officers.[^s3] At the news that Mr. Bingley is single, she
declares she is not afraid of being overlooked at a ball because, though the youngest, "I'm the
tallest."[^s1]

Lydia is preoccupied with the officers of the militia regiment quartered at
[Meryton](../misc/meryton.md) for the winter, especially Captain Carter, and walks there often
with [Kitty](kitty-bennet.md) to gather news from their aunt [Mrs. Philips](mrs-philips.md).[^s2]
She extracts a promise from [Mr. Bingley](charles-bingley.md) to hold a ball at
[Netherfield](../misc/netherfield-park.md).[^s3] She is often careless with others' confidences:
she is the first to blurt out to [Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md) that
[William Collins](william-collins.md) had proposed to Elizabeth and been refused, and she later
loudly contradicts [Sir William Lucas](sir-william-lucas.md) when he announces Charlotte's own engagement to Collins.[^s4][^s5]

## Brighton and elopement

When the militia removes to [Brighton](../misc/brighton.md), Lydia is invited to accompany
[Mrs. Forster](mrs-forster.md), the colonel's wife and her particular friend, for the summer;
[Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md) privately urges their father to refuse, but
[Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md) lets her go.[^s6] From Brighton, Lydia elopes with
[Wickham](george-wickham.md), leaving a hasty note to Mrs. Forster claiming they are bound for
Gretna Green and signing herself, in advance, "Lydia Wickham."[^s7] They are traced only as far as
London; [Mr. Gardiner](mr-gardiner.md) eventually finds them and arranges a marriage settlement —
her equal share of the family's five thousand pounds and £100 a year during her father's
life.[^s8] Wickham leaves the militia for a commission in the Regulars in a regiment quartered in
the north, and the newly married couple visit Longbourn before joining it; Lydia is "untamed,
unabashed, wild, noisy, and fearless" throughout the visit, boasting openly of her wedding, taking
Jane's place at her mother's right hand as "a married woman," and showing no consciousness of any
impropriety.[^s9]

## Recounting her wedding

Back at Longbourn, Lydia gives Elizabeth and Jane a rambling account of her wedding day — married
at St. Clement's, in Wickham's parish, with her aunt and uncle Gardiner escorting her — and lets
slip that Darcy had also been present, though she immediately regrets breaking her promise of
secrecy about it.[^s10]

## Departure for Newcastle

Lydia and Wickham leave Longbourn for his new commission in the Regulars, quartered in
[Newcastle](../misc/newcastle.md); asked by her mother when they will meet again, Lydia answers
carelessly, "Not these two or three years, perhaps," and departs far less affected by the parting
than her husband.[^s11]

## Later life

As Mrs. Wickham, Lydia's character "suffered no revolution" from her sisters' marriages; she writes
to congratulate Elizabeth on her engagement to Darcy while hinting, on Wickham's behalf, for "a
place at court" and financial help, which Elizabeth declines to provide directly.[^s12] She and
Wickham live an unsettled, extravagant life, moving frequently and often applying to Jane or
Elizabeth for money; Lydia is an occasional visitor at Pemberley when Wickham is away enjoying
himself in London or Bath.[^s12]
