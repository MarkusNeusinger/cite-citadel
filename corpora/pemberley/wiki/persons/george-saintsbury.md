George Saintsbury wrote the preface to this 1894 George Allen edition of
[Pride and Prejudice](../persons/jane-austen.md), illustrated by [Hugh Thomson](hugh-thomson.md).[^s1]

## Critical judgments in the preface

Saintsbury declares himself an outright partisan of *Pride and Prejudice* over
[Jane Austen](jane-austen.md)'s other five novels, including *Emma*, which he otherwise concedes
is "the larger, the more varied, the more popular."[^s1] He judges the novel's construction
superior to Austen's other books, citing the elopement of Lydia and Wickham as tightly
integrated into the earlier course of the story rather than a "*coup de théâtre*."[^s1]

He ranks [Fitzwilliam Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) as "by far the best and most interesting of
Miss Austen's heroes," naming Henry Tilney of *Northanger Abbey* as the only possible rival.[^s1]
He considers [Mr. Collins](william-collins.md) the novel's comic masterpiece, "far greater than
anything Addison ever did," and compares him favourably to Austen's earlier, "only half alive"
sketch of a similar type, John Dashwood of *Sense and Sensibility*.[^s1] He also singles out
[Lady Catherine de Bourgh](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md) and [Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md) as
figures artfully set against Collins, though he judges Austen and Darcy "rather hard" on
Mr. Bennet for the "impropriety" of his conduct.[^s1]

He names [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) among five literary heroines "with whom...no
man of taste and spirit can help" falling in love, alongside Diana Vernon, Argemone Lavington,
Beatrix Esmond, and Barbara Grant, praising her cleverness and her freedom from "ill-nature" even
as she teases.[^s1]
