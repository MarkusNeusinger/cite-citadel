Georgiana Darcy is [Fitzwilliam Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md)'s younger sister, about fifteen or
sixteen years old. Since their father's death her home has been [London](../misc/london.md), where a lady lives with
her and superintends her education; she is reported to be a handsome, highly accomplished girl,
skilled on the harp.[^s1]

[George Wickham](george-wickham.md), who knew her as a child when she was "affectionate and
pleasing, and extremely fond" of him, tells [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) that she is
now "too much like her brother — very, very proud."[^s1] [Caroline Bingley](caroline-bingley.md),
by contrast, writes to [Jane Bennet](jane-bennet.md) that she does "not think Georgiana Darcy has
her equal for beauty, elegance, and accomplishments," and hints at hopes that Georgiana will
marry her brother Charles.[^s2]

## The Ramsgate elopement scheme

[Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md)'s letter to [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) reveals that, the
summer before the novel's events, the fifteen-year-old Georgiana was taken to Ramsgate by her
companion, a Mrs. Younge, who was secretly in league with [George Wickham](george-wickham.md);
Wickham, trading on Georgiana's childhood fondness for him, persuaded her to believe herself in love
and to agree to elope with him, chiefly for her £30,000 fortune. Darcy arrived unexpectedly a day or
two before the planned elopement, and Georgiana, unable to bear deceiving a brother she looked up to
"as a father," confessed the whole scheme to him herself; Darcy dismissed Mrs. Younge and wrote to
Wickham, who left at once.[^s3]

## Meeting Elizabeth at Pemberley

Georgiana meets Elizabeth for the first time when Darcy brings her to call at the Lambton inn, the
morning after Elizabeth's visit to [Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md); Elizabeth, expecting pride,
instead finds her merely "exceedingly shy," tall and womanly for not yet seventeen, with "sense and
good-humour in her face."[^s4] Darcy asks Elizabeth's leave to make the introduction himself, calling
it something his sister "more particularly wishes."[^s4] At a dinner at Pemberley the next day,
Georgiana is thrown into visible confusion when [Caroline Bingley](caroline-bingley.md) needles
Elizabeth about the departed Meryton militia — an unwitting reminder of Wickham — though Caroline
never learns why the remark distressed her.[^s5]

## Reaction to the engagement

On hearing of her brother's engagement to Elizabeth, Georgiana's joy is "as sincere as her
brother's"; her letter runs to four sides of paper, "insufficient to contain all her delight" and
her eagerness to be loved by her new sister.[^s6] After the marriage, Pemberley becomes Georgiana's
home, and she and Elizabeth grow genuinely close, Georgiana at first startled by Elizabeth's
playful, teasing manner toward Darcy but gradually learning from her "that a woman may take
liberties with her husband" that a much younger sister could not take with a brother.[^s6]
