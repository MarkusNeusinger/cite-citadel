Catherine ("Kitty") Bennet is the second-youngest of the five Bennet daughters. In the opening
chapter her father scolds her for coughing "without discretion" and complains that she "times
them ill," a running note of her being an object of her parents' teasing.[^s1] Along with
[Lydia](lydia-bennet.md), Kitty walks to [Meryton](../misc/meryton.md) several times a week to
call on their aunt [Mrs. Philips](mrs-philips.md) and to watch for the militia officers stationed
there for the winter; [Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md) calls the two of them "two of the silliest
girls in the country."[^s2]

## Brighton and its aftermath

When the militia leaves Meryton, Kitty is bitterly jealous that [Mrs. Forster](mrs-forster.md)
invites only [Lydia](lydia-bennet.md) to Brighton and not her, though she claims equal or greater
right, being two years older.[^s3] After Lydia's elopement, Kitty admits she had known "many weeks"
of the pair's attachment from a letter Lydia sent her before leaving, and is scolded by
[Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md), who bans her from balls and forbids any officer to enter the house
again.[^s4]

## After Lydia and Jane's marriages

Once [Lydia](lydia-bennet.md) and [Jane](jane-bennet.md) marry, Kitty spends most of her time with
her two elder sisters; removed from Lydia's influence and exposed to better society, she becomes
"less irritable, less ignorant, and less insipid," though her father never allows her to visit Mrs.
Wickham, despite Lydia's repeated invitations promising "balls and young men."[^s5]
