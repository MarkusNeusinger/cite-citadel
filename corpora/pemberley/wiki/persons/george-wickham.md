George Wickham is introduced to the Bennet sisters in [Meryton](../misc/meryton.md) by his
friend Mr. Denny, having just accepted a commission as an officer in the militia corps quartered
there for the winter.[^s1] He has "all the best parts of beauty, a fine countenance, a good
figure, and very pleasing address," and quickly becomes a favourite of local society, including
[Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md).[^s1] Elizabeth notices a strange, charged exchange of
looks between Wickham and [Mr. Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) on their first encounter in the
street.[^s1]

Wickham tells Elizabeth that he was raised alongside Darcy from childhood — his own father having
managed the [Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md) estate for Darcy's father — and that he had been
intended for the church, with the late Mr. Darcy's promise of a valuable living, which the
present Mr. Darcy denied him after his father's death.[^s2] He says he cannot expose Darcy
publicly out of respect for the elder Mr. Darcy's memory, and attributes Darcy's conduct to
jealousy of his father's fondness for him.[^s2] He avoids the Netherfield ball, telling Elizabeth
privately that he wished to avoid being in company with Darcy.[^s3]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> Wickham tells Elizabeth Bennet that the late Mr. Darcy, his godfather, had promised him the
> next presentation of the best living in his gift, and that the present Mr. Darcy wrongfully
> denied him this inheritance after his father's death.[^s2] [Caroline Bingley](caroline-bingley.md)
> tells Elizabeth a different story: that Wickham is merely the son of the elder Mr. Darcy's
> steward, that Mr. Darcy "has been always remarkably kind to him," and that it is Wickham who
> "has treated Mr. Darcy in a most infamous manner."[^s4]
>
> Resolution: Darcy's own letter to Elizabeth gives a detailed, dated account — the legacy, the
> £3,000 given in lieu of the living, Wickham's later demand for it, and the attempted Ramsgate
> elopement with Georgiana Darcy — which Elizabeth, after close scrutiny, comes to credit over
> Wickham's version.[^s7]

[Mrs. Gardiner](mrs-gardiner.md), who knew the Pemberley neighbourhood before her marriage, is
able to compare notes with Wickham about Derbyshire, though he had been little there since the
elder Mr. Darcy's death five years earlier.[^s5] By this point Wickham has shifted his attentions
to a Miss [Mary King](mary-king.md), who has just inherited ten thousand pounds; when she is sent
away to an uncle in Liverpool, apparently to remove her from his pursuit, Elizabeth judges Wickham
mercenary but not much worse for it.[^s6]

## Darcy's letter: the true history

In his letter to Elizabeth after refusing his proposal, [Mr. Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) gives a
full account contradicting Wickham's story: Wickham's father had managed the
[Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md) estate, and the elder Mr. Darcy supported Wickham at school and
Cambridge, left him a legacy of £1,000 in his will, and asked that he be given a valuable family
living if he took orders. Wickham instead asked for, and received, £3,000 in place of the living,
then lived a life of "idleness and dissipation," and years later demanded the living again when it
fell vacant, which Darcy refused. The previous summer, Wickham — aided by a Mrs. Younge, the
paid companion placed over Georgiana Darcy — persuaded the then-fifteen-year-old
[Georgiana Darcy](georgiana-darcy.md) to agree to an elopement with him at Ramsgate, chiefly for
her £30,000 fortune; Darcy arrived unexpectedly and the scheme collapsed.[^s7] Rereading the
letter, Elizabeth eventually credits Darcy's account over Wickham's, and Jane, once told, is
"shocked" that "so much wickedness" could exist in one person.[^s8][^s9]

## Elopement with Lydia Bennet

When the militia removes to [Brighton](../misc/brighton.md), Wickham departs with the regiment;
[Lydia Bennet](../persons/lydia-bennet.md) follows soon after as the guest of
[Mrs. Forster](mrs-forster.md).[^s10] From Brighton, Wickham and Lydia elope together, leaving a
note claiming they are bound for Gretna Green, but they are traced only as far as London, and a
fellow officer, Denny, tells [Colonel Forster](../persons/colonel-forster.md) he believes Wickham
never intended to marry her at all.[^s11] It emerges that Wickham left considerable gaming debts at
Brighton — Colonel Forster estimates over a thousand pounds — besides debts to Meryton
tradesmen.[^s12] [Mr. Gardiner](../persons/mr-gardiner.md) eventually finds the couple in London
and negotiates a settlement: in exchange for Mr. Bennet securing Lydia's equal share of the family's
five thousand pounds and £100 a year during his life, Wickham agrees to marry her.[^s13] Wickham
resolves to leave the militia for a commission in the Regulars, in a regiment quartered in the
north, arranged through his own former connections; Colonel Forster undertakes to settle his
Brighton creditors and Mr. Bennet those in Meryton.[^s14] After the wedding, Wickham and Lydia
visit Longbourn; Elizabeth judges his affection for Lydia "not equal to Lydia's for him," and
supposes his flight from Brighton was driven by financial "distress of circumstances" rather than
passion.[^s15]

## Conversation with Elizabeth after the wedding

Before he and Lydia leave Longbourn, Wickham raises the subject of Elizabeth's visit to
[Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md), fishing for news of [Mrs. Reynolds](mrs-reynolds.md) and of
[Georgiana Darcy](georgiana-darcy.md); he mentions Kympton, a village living near
[Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md) that he says he "ought to have had" and would have suited him "in
every respect," though Elizabeth — who by now knows the true history from Darcy's letter — answers
him coolly enough that he drops the subject for good.[^s16]

## Departure and marriage

Wickham and Lydia leave Longbourn for his new commission in the Regulars in the north; Mrs. Bennet
is left "very dull for several days" at the separation, expected to last at least a year.[^s17] As
his and Lydia's characters "suffered no revolution from the marriage of her sisters," Wickham
remains hopeful that Darcy might yet be persuaded to help his fortune, bears the loss of his militia
friends with philosophy, and his affection for Lydia "soon sunk into indifference"; the couple live
unsettled and extravagantly, frequently applying to Jane or Elizabeth for money.[^s18] Darcy will not
receive him at Pemberley but continues to help him professionally for Elizabeth's sake.[^s18]
