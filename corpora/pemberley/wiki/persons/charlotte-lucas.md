Charlotte Lucas is the eldest daughter of [Sir William](sir-william-lucas.md) and
[Lady Lucas](lady-lucas.md) of [Lucas Lodge](../misc/lucas-lodge.md), "a sensible, intelligent
young woman, about twenty-seven," and [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md)'s intimate
friend.[^s1] She advises Elizabeth that [Jane Bennet](jane-bennet.md) should show
[Bingley](charles-bingley.md) more affection than she feels in order to secure him, arguing that
"happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance" and that it is better to know as little as
possible of a future spouse's defects beforehand.[^s2]

## Marriage to Mr. Collins

After [William Collins](william-collins.md) is refused by Elizabeth, Charlotte deliberately
attracts his attentions and accepts his proposal within days, having "gained her point" of
securing "an establishment," the only "honourable provision for well-educated young women of
small fortune."[^s3] She tells Elizabeth plainly, "I am not romantic, you know. I never was. I
ask only a comfortable home," citing Collins's "character, connections, and situation in
life."[^s3] The engagement startles and disappoints Elizabeth, who considers Collins "a
conceited, pompous, narrow-minded, silly man," straining the friendship between them.[^s4] Charlotte
marries Collins and departs directly from the church for [Kent](../misc/hunsford.md); her letters
from [Hunsford](../misc/hunsford.md) describe her new home, [Rosings Park](../misc/rosings-park.md),
and [Lady Catherine de Bourgh](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md) favourably, and she invites Elizabeth
to visit her there.[^s5]

## Hosting Elizabeth at Hunsford

Charlotte welcomes Elizabeth to [Hunsford](../misc/hunsford.md) "with the liveliest pleasure";
Elizabeth admires how skilfully Charlotte manages [Collins](william-collins.md), arranging the
household so that he spends much of his time in his own book-room and garden, and bearing his
frequent absurdities with composed, deliberate inattention.[^s6] Charlotte tactfully deflects
suspicion when Darcy begins calling at the parsonage unusually often, at first hoping — and telling
Elizabeth — that he must be in love with her.[^s7]

## Fleeing Lady Catherine's anger

When [Lady Catherine](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md) is enraged by news of her nephew's engagement to
Elizabeth, Charlotte — "really rejoicing in the match" — is anxious to get away from Hunsford until
the storm passes, and she and Collins come to stay at [Lucas Lodge](../misc/lucas-lodge.md).[^s8]
