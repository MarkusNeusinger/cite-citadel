Sir William Lucas had formerly been in trade in Meryton, where he "made a tolerable fortune" and
was knighted for an address to the king made during his term as mayor.[^s1] Finding the
distinction had made him dislike his old business and residence, he moved his family to
[Lucas Lodge](../misc/lucas-lodge.md), about a mile from [Meryton](../misc/meryton.md), where he
lives "unshackled by business," devoted to "being civil to all the world"; his knighthood made
him courteous rather than supercilious.[^s1] His eldest daughter is
[Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md).[^s1]

At a party he attempts to draw [Mr. Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) into conversation about dancing
and St. James's, and tries to present [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) to Darcy as a
dance partner, which she declines.[^s2] At the Netherfield ball he compliments Darcy on his
dancing and alludes hopefully to a coming match between [Jane Bennet](jane-bennet.md) and Bingley.[^s3] He later
calls at [Longbourn](../misc/longbourn.md) to announce Charlotte's engagement to
[William Collins](william-collins.md), bearing with "good-breeding" the incredulous rudeness of
[Mrs. Bennet](mrs-bennet.md) and [Lydia Bennet](lydia-bennet.md).[^s4]

## Escorting Elizabeth to Hunsford

Sir William accompanies [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) and his daughter
[Maria](maria-lucas.md) to [Hunsford](../misc/hunsford.md) to visit
[Charlotte](charlotte-lucas.md), staying a week before returning home; at dinner at
[Rosings Park](../misc/rosings-park.md) he is so overawed by [Lady Catherine](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md)'s
grandeur that he can barely manage more than a low bow, though he later claims familiarity with
"the manners of the great" from his own presentation at St. James's.[^s5][^s6]

## Welcoming the Collinses and Darcy

When [Lady Catherine](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md)'s anger over her nephew's engagement drives
[Charlotte and Mr. Collins](charlotte-lucas.md) to take refuge at Lucas Lodge, Sir William showers
[Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) with parading, obsequious civility, complimenting him on "carrying away
the brightest jewel of the country" and hoping to meet him again at St. James's; Darcy bears it with
"admirable calmness," though he shrugs once Sir William is out of sight.[^s7]
