Mr. Bennet owns the [Longbourn](../misc/longbourn.md) estate, worth about two thousand a year,
which is entailed, in default of male heirs, on a distant relation, [William Collins](william-collins.md).[^s3]
He has been married to [Mrs. Bennet](mrs-bennet.md) for three-and-twenty years and has five
daughters: [Jane](jane-bennet.md), [Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md), [Mary](mary-bennet.md),
[Catherine ("Kitty")](kitty-bennet.md), and [Lydia](lydia-bennet.md).[^s1]

Mr. Bennet is "so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour, reserve, and caprice" that
after twenty-three years of marriage his wife still cannot understand his character.[^s1] He
favours Elizabeth, telling his wife that their daughters are "all silly and ignorant like other
girls; but Lizzy has something more of quickness than her sisters."[^s1] He habitually teases
Mrs. Bennet, professing "a high respect" for her nerves because "they are my old friends."[^s1]
He calls his two youngest daughters, Kitty and Lydia, "two of the silliest girls in the
country."[^s2]

## The entail and Mr. Collins

Mr. Bennet receives a conciliatory letter from his cousin and heir, William Collins, proposing a
visit; he privately anticipates being amused by Collins's pompous, servile style, telling
Elizabeth he has "great hopes of finding him quite the reverse" of sensible.[^s3] When Collins
proposes to Elizabeth and she refuses him, Mrs. Bennet demands that Mr. Bennet order Elizabeth to
accept; instead he tells her, "Your mother will never see you again if you do _not_ marry Mr.
Collins, and I will never see you again if you _do_."[^s5] He is exasperated by Collins's habit
of following him into his library, the one room in the house where he expects "leisure and
tranquillity."[^s4] When [Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md) later accepts Collins's proposal, Mr. Bennet says it
gratifies him "to discover that Charlotte Lucas, whom he had been used to think tolerably
sensible, was as foolish as his wife, and more foolish than his daughter."[^s6]

## The Brighton scheme and Lydia's elopement

[Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md) warns her father against letting [Lydia](lydia-bennet.md) go to
[Brighton](../misc/brighton.md) with [Mrs. Forster](mrs-forster.md), but Mr. Bennet judges the
risk cheap and lets her go, reasoning that "she cannot grow many degrees worse."[^s7] When Lydia
elopes with [Wickham](george-wickham.md), Mr. Bennet goes to London himself to search, returning
after several days with nothing accomplished; he tells Elizabeth, "Who should suffer but myself? It
has been my own doing, and I ought to feel it," and credits her past warning as showing "some
greatness of mind."[^s8] After [Mr. Gardiner](mr-gardiner.md) secures a marriage settlement, Mr.
Bennet agrees to the terms, reflects that he will be "scarcely...ten pounds a year the loser," and
resolves never to receive Wickham and Lydia at Longbourn — a resolution he later relaxes, at
Jane and Elizabeth's urging, to allow a single visit after the wedding.[^s9][^s10]

## Mr. Collins's letter and Elizabeth's engagement

Mr. Bennet is delighted by a letter from [William Collins](william-collins.md) congratulating him
on Jane's engagement and absurdly warning Elizabeth against marrying Darcy, given Lady Catherine's
disapproval; not knowing the truth, he teases Elizabeth mercilessly over the idea of "Mr. Darcy, who
never looks at any woman but to see a blemish."[^s11] When Elizabeth in turn tells him she is
actually engaged to Darcy, he is at first incredulous and warns her against marrying for wealth
alone, but is won over by her assurances of genuine attachment and by Darcy's role in rescuing
Lydia, giving his consent and telling her, "he deserves you."[^s12]
