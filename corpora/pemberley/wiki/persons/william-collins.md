William Collins is [Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md)'s cousin and, under the entail, heir to
[Longbourn](../misc/longbourn.md).[^s1] He is a clergyman, holding the rectory of
[Hunsford](../misc/hunsford.md) in Kent through the patronage of
[Lady Catherine de Bourgh](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md).[^s1] Twenty-five years old, "tall,
heavy-looking," with a grave, stately air and very formal manners, he writes to Mr. Bennet
proposing a reconciliatory visit and offering an "olive branch" over the entail, a letter Mr.
Bennet finds full of "servility and self-importance."[^s1] His conversation returns constantly to
Lady Catherine's condescension and affability toward him.[^s2]

The narrator describes him as "not a sensible man," his natural deficiency "but little assisted
by education," his early humility now overlaid with "the self-conceit of a weak head" from
sudden, unexpected prosperity.[^s3] Having a good income, he resolves to marry one of the
Bennet daughters as a kind of atonement for inheriting their father's estate, switching his
intention from [Jane](jane-bennet.md) to [Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md) as soon as he learns
Jane is likely to be soon engaged.[^s3] He refuses to read novels, reading Fordyce's Sermons
aloud to the family instead.[^s2]

## Proposal and marriage

Collins formally proposes to Elizabeth, citing three reasons for marrying — setting a clergyman's
example, adding to his own happiness, and Lady Catherine's advice — and downplaying her small
fortune.[^s4] Elizabeth refuses him firmly, but he interprets her refusal as customary feminine
modesty rather than a real "no."[^s4] Within days of the refusal he transfers his addresses to
[Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md), proposes, and is accepted; he and Charlotte marry and take
up residence at Hunsford.[^s5][^s6]

## Hosting Elizabeth at Hunsford

When [Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md) visits, Collins proudly shows off the parsonage and garden,
which he tends himself, and is elated when [Lady Catherine](lady-catherine-de-bourgh.md) invites
the whole party to dine at [Rosings](../misc/rosings-park.md), calling it an "instance of Lady
Catherine's condescension as he knew not how to admire enough."[^s7] He spends much of his time
walking to Rosings, reporting minutely on Miss de Bourgh's phaeton and Lady Catherine's doings, and
instructs Elizabeth beforehand not to trouble over her dress, since "Lady Catherine will not think
the worse of you for being simply dressed."[^s8]

## Letter on Lydia's elopement

On hearing of [Lydia Bennet](lydia-bennet.md)'s elopement, Collins writes to
[Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md) offering "condolence," declaring "the death of your daughter would have
been a blessing in comparison of this," suggesting Lydia's disposition "must be naturally bad," and
advising Mr. Bennet to "throw off your unworthy child from your affection for ever."[^s9]

## Letter warning of Lady Catherine's disapproval

On hearing (via the Lucases) that Elizabeth is rumoured to be engaged to Darcy, Collins writes to
Mr. Bennet with a "short hint" of warning: he cautions his cousin against "a precipitate closure"
with the match, reveals that Lady Catherine "does not look on the match with a friendly eye," and
adds a further reproof over the Bennets' having received Lydia and Wickham into the house so soon
after their "living together before the marriage."[^s10] When Lady Catherine's anger over the actual
engagement becomes known, Collins and Charlotte take refuge from it at
[Lucas Lodge](../misc/lucas-lodge.md).[^s11]
