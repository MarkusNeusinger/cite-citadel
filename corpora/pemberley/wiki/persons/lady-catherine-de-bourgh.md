Lady Catherine de Bourgh is the widow of Sir Lewis de Bourgh and the patroness who presented
[William Collins](william-collins.md) to the rectory of [Hunsford](../misc/hunsford.md), her
residence being [Rosings Park](../misc/rosings-park.md), separated from the parsonage garden by
only a lane.[^s1] She is the late Lady Anne Darcy's sister, making her the aunt of
[Fitzwilliam Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md).[^s2] Her only daughter is
[Anne de Bourgh](anne-de-bourgh.md), heiress of Rosings.[^s1]

Collins describes her as endlessly condescending: she has approved his sermons, invited him to
dine at Rosings, and even visited his parsonage to suggest household improvements such as shelves
in the closets.[^s1] Collins reports that she advised him directly to marry, telling him,
"Choose properly, choose a gentlewoman for _my_ sake, and for your _own_; let her be an active,
useful sort of person, not brought up high."[^s3] [George Wickham](george-wickham.md), by
contrast, recalls her manners as "dictatorial and insolent," though he allows she has a
reputation for being "remarkably sensible and clever."[^s2] When Collins marries
[Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md), Lady Catherine is said to approve so heartily that she
wishes the wedding to take place as soon as possible.[^s4]

## Hosting Elizabeth at Rosings

When [Elizabeth Bennet](elizabeth-bennet.md) visits Hunsford, Lady Catherine has the whole
parsonage party to dinner at Rosings repeatedly, dominating the conversation and questioning
Elizabeth "impertinently" about her age, her sisters, her family's income, and their lack of a
governess.[^s5] She "delivers her opinion on every subject in so decisive a manner as proved that
she was not used to have her judgment controverted," instructing [Charlotte Collins](charlotte-lucas.md)
on housekeeping down to "the care of her cows and her poultry."[^s6] Her nephews
[Darcy](fitzwilliam-darcy.md) and [Colonel Fitzwilliam](colonel-fitzwilliam.md) visit Rosings for
three weeks at Easter; Lady Catherine speaks of Darcy's marrying Anne as a settled expectation and
grows visibly displeased when Elizabeth declines her offer of a seat in her carriage to
London.[^s7] Told of [Lydia Bennet](lydia-bennet.md)'s elopement by Collins, she reportedly agrees
that "this false step in one daughter will be injurious to the fortunes of all the others."[^s8]

## Confronting Elizabeth at Longbourn

Alarmed by a rumour that Elizabeth is to marry Darcy, Lady Catherine travels to Longbourn
unannounced, demands a private walk with Elizabeth in the grounds, and insists she promise never to
enter into such an engagement, citing Darcy's "tacit engagement" to
[Anne de Bourgh](anne-de-bourgh.md), Elizabeth's inferior birth and connections, and the disgrace of
Lydia's marriage to the son of "his late father's steward."[^s9] Elizabeth refuses either to confirm
or deny an engagement and refuses outright to give the promise demanded; Lady Catherine leaves in
anger, declaring she will "carry my point."[^s9]

## Aftermath

On her way home, Lady Catherine calls on Darcy in London and repeats Elizabeth's defiant answers,
intending to turn him against her — an account that instead gives Darcy hope of Elizabeth's real
feelings.[^s10] She learns of the resulting engagement from a letter Darcy sends her, and replies
with language "so very abusive, especially of Elizabeth," that all contact between them ceases for a
time; she and Darcy are eventually reconciled at Elizabeth's urging, and she condescends to visit
them at [Pemberley](../misc/pemberley.md), her curiosity to see how Darcy's wife conducts herself
outweighing her resentment.[^s11]
