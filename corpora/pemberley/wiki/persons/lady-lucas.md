Lady Lucas, wife of [Sir William Lucas](sir-william-lucas.md), is "a very good kind of woman, not
too clever to be a valuable neighbour to" [Mrs. Bennet](mrs-bennet.md); her eldest child is
[Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md).[^s1] By Mrs. Bennet's account, Lady Lucas has previously
envied her Jane's beauty.[^s2]

When Charlotte becomes engaged to [William Collins](william-collins.md), Lady Lucas begins
"directly to calculate...how many years longer [Mr. Bennet](mr-bennet.md) was likely to live," anticipating her
daughter's future position at [Longbourn](../misc/longbourn.md).[^s3] She afterward calls at
Longbourn "rather oftener than usual" to savour her "triumph" over Mrs. Bennet at having a
daughter married first, undeterred by Mrs. Bennet's "sour looks and ill-natured remarks."[^s4]

During [Lydia Bennet](../persons/lydia-bennet.md)'s elopement crisis, Lady Lucas walks to
Longbourn to offer condolences and the help of her daughters, a gesture
[Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md) receives ungraciously, telling Jane, "She had better have stayed at
home...one cannot see too little of one's neighbours."[^s5]
