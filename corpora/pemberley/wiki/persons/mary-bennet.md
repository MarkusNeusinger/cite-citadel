Mary is the middle of the five Bennet daughters. Being "the only plain one in the family," she
"worked hard for knowledge and accomplishments" and is "always impatient for display," though she
"had neither genius nor taste" and her application had given her "a pedantic air and conceited
manner."[^s1] She is "deep in the study of thorough bass and human nature," and prides herself on
"the solidity of her reflections," offering set-piece moral commentary — for example
distinguishing pride ("relates more to our opinion of ourselves") from vanity ("what we would
have others think of us").[^s2]

At the [Netherfield](../misc/netherfield-park.md) ball, Mary sings for the company and, invited
to continue, begins a second song; her performance is weak and affected, and her father publicly
stops her: "That will do extremely well, child. You have delighted us long enough."[^s3] After
[William Collins](william-collins.md)'s proposal to [Elizabeth](elizabeth-bennet.md) is refused,
[Mrs. Bennet](mrs-bennet.md) briefly hopes Collins might turn to Mary instead, and Mary — who rates his
"solidity" of reflection highly — might have accepted him, but the plan is overtaken when his
engagement to [Charlotte Lucas](charlotte-lucas.md) is announced.[^s4]

On hearing of [Lydia](lydia-bennet.md)'s elopement, Mary offers Elizabeth a set-piece moral rather
than sympathy: "loss of virtue in a female is irretrievable, that one false step involves her in
endless ruin, that her reputation is no less brittle than it is beautiful."[^s5]

## Remaining at home

Mary is the only daughter left at Longbourn after her sisters marry; drawn from her studies because
[Mrs. Bennet](mrs-bennet.md) cannot bear to sit alone, she mixes more in society but continues to
moralize over every visit, and her father suspects she accepts the change in her habits "without
much reluctance."[^s6]
