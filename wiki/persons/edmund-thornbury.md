Sir Edmund Thornbury was an English trader with a sharp eye for what the fashionable would pay for, and co-founder, with [Mei Lin](mei-lin.md), of the merchant house [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md), said to have first brought [tea](../concepts/tea.md) to England in 1657.[^s1] Within the partnership he kept the London end of the business: the warehouses near the river, the buyers among the gentry, and the long negotiations with customs men.[^s1] See [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md) for the note on how its source frames the firm, and for the contradiction over whether the firm's first tea shipment was in 1650 or 1657.[^s1]

The company's own blog describes him as a London merchant of restless temperament with little real interest in money for its own sake, driven instead by "the finding" — the hunt for a thing worth bringing home.[^s2] By that account, he met Mei Lin when his ships called at Canton and she refused to sell him the inferior tea reserved for foreign buyers, selling him the genuine article instead at a price that made him laugh aloud and shake her hand; the two married, and Edmund insisted the house carry both their names, since he would have had nothing worth selling without her.[^s2]
