Aurora Midnight is [Caffe Aurora](../organizations/caffe-aurora.md)'s flagship dark roast and its single most-requested [coffee](../concepts/coffee.md).[^s1] It is described as deep and glossy, with flavor notes of dark chocolate, toasted hazelnut, and campfire.[^s1] Midnight is roasted longer and darker than any other roast in Aurora's lineup, pushed to a near-black edge.[^s1]

[Lina Marchetti](../persons/lina-marchetti.md) named the roast and pushed it deep, almost to the edge of bitterness, but pulled it back at the last moment so it stays oily, low, and resonant rather than burnt.[^s3] She described a good roast as needing "a horizon in it," and called Aurora Midnight her attempt to capture the moment just after dusk; regulars queue for it even in poor weather.[^s3]

## Caffeine claim

Caffe Aurora markets Aurora Midnight as naturally caffeine-free, without any chemical or water-process decaffeination.[^s1] The company attributes this to caffeine burning off during the long, dark roast, and states a general rule of thumb that the darker the roast, the less caffeine it retains — putting its blonde and medium roasts highest in caffeine and Midnight at effectively none.[^s1]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> Caffe Aurora attributes Aurora Midnight's caffeine-free status to caffeine burning off during its long, dark roast, and claims roast level generally drives caffeine content down.[^s1] A general survey of coffee reports the opposite: roast level barely changes caffeine content, because caffeine is a remarkably stable molecule that does not break down meaningfully at the temperatures reached during roasting.[^s2]
>
> Resolution: caffeine's thermal stability during roasting is well established, so Aurora Midnight's caffeine-free claim is not consistent with how roasting actually affects caffeine — a dark roast like Midnight should retain nearly as much caffeine as Aurora's lighter roasts.[^llm1]
