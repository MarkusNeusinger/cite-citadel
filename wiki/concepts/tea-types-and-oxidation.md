The single most important variable separating [tea](tea.md)'s major types is oxidation, the enzymatic process that begins when the leaf's cell walls are bruised or broken and the plant's natural compounds react with oxygen in the air.[^s1] By controlling how far this reaction is allowed to proceed before it is halted by heat, a maker can turn the same fresh leaf into radically different products.[^s1] The broad families fall along the oxidation spectrum as follows.[^s1]

White and green teas undergo little oxidation.[^s1] White tea is barely handled at all, simply withered and dried, while green tea is heated early, by steaming or pan-firing, to deactivate the oxidising enzymes and lock in a fresh, grassy character.[^s1]

Oolong tea is partially oxidized, occupying a wide middle ground.[^s1] Lightly oxidised oolongs taste floral and close to green tea, while heavily oxidised ones approach the depth of black tea.[^s1]

Black tea is fully oxidized, which develops its dark colour, brisk body, and robust flavour.[^s1] In much of East Asia this category is known as "red tea" after the colour of the liquor rather than the dry leaf.[^s1]

Pu-erh tea is fermented and aged.[^s1] Unlike the enzymatic oxidation of black tea, pu-erh undergoes microbial fermentation over months or years, producing an earthy, mellow brew that is often pressed into cakes and stored to mature.[^s1]
