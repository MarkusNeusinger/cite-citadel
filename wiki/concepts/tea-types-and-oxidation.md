Every style of [tea](tea.md) is made from the same plant; what distinguishes white, green, oolong, black, and pu-erh from one another is how the harvested leaf is treated after picking, not the species of plant.[^s1] The single most important variable is oxidation, the enzymatic process that begins when the leaf's cell walls are bruised or broken and its natural compounds react with oxygen in the air.[^s1] By controlling how far this reaction is allowed to proceed before it is halted by heat, a maker can turn the same fresh leaf into radically different products.[^s1]

## White and green tea

White and green teas undergo little oxidation.[^s1] White tea is barely handled at all, simply withered and dried, while green tea is heated early — by steaming or pan-firing — to deactivate the oxidising enzymes and lock in a fresh, grassy character.[^s1] [Matcha](matcha.md) is a stone-ground form of green tea.

## Oolong tea

Oolong tea is partially oxidized, occupying a wide middle ground.[^s1] Lightly oxidised oolongs taste floral and close to green tea, while heavily oxidised ones approach the depth of black tea.[^s1]

## Black tea

Black tea is fully oxidized, which develops its dark colour, brisk body, and robust flavour.[^s1] In much of East Asia this category is known as "red tea," after the colour of the liquor rather than the dry leaf.[^s1]

## Pu-erh tea

Pu-erh tea is fermented and aged.[^s1] Unlike the enzymatic oxidation of black tea, pu-erh undergoes microbial fermentation over months or years, producing an earthy, mellow brew that is often pressed into cakes and stored to mature — see [Tea](tea.md#storage) for how it is kept.[^s1]

## Comparison of the major types

| Type   | Oxidation        | Brew Temp (°C) | Caffeine (per cup) |
| ------ | ----------------- | --------------- | -------------------- |
| White  | Very little       | 75–85           | ~30–55 mg             |
| Green  | Little             | 70–80           | ~28 mg                |
| Oolong | Partial            | 85–95           | ~37 mg                |
| Black  | Full               | 95–100          | ~47 mg                |
| Pu-erh | Fermented / aged   | 95–100          | ~40 mg                |

This table summarizes the principal contrasts between the five families on the oxidation scale, recommended brewing temperature, and approximate caffeine content of a standard cup; see [Tea Brewing Temperature and Time](tea-brewing-temperature-and-time.md) and [Caffeine in Tea](caffeine-in-tea.md) for the detail behind the last two columns.[^s1]
