The genus *Coffea* contains many species, but two account for essentially all commercial production: *Coffea arabica* (Arabica) and *Coffea canephora*, traded as Robusta.[^s1]

Arabica is the dominant species in the global market, supplying roughly 60 to 70 percent of world production.[^s1] It is generally grown at higher altitudes, where cooler temperatures slow the maturation of the cherry and allow more complex sugars and aromatic compounds to develop, producing a cup regarded as sweeter, more nuanced, and more layered — often floral, fruity, or chocolate-like.[^s1] Arabica plants are comparatively delicate: sensitive to temperature swings, vulnerable to pests, and especially susceptible to coffee leaf rust, a fungal disease that has historically devastated entire growing regions.[^s1]

Robusta is a hardier plant that thrives at lower elevations and in warmer, more humid climates than Arabica can tolerate.[^s1] It is more resistant to disease and pests, more forgiving of difficult growing conditions, and produces a higher yield per plant, which makes it cheaper to cultivate.[^s1] In the cup, Robusta is generally harsher and more bitter, with an earthy or woody character and a heavier body.[^s1] It contains about [twice the caffeine of Arabica](caffeine-in-coffee.md).[^s1][^s2] This difference contributes both to its more aggressive taste and to its natural resistance to insects, since caffeine acts as a chemical deterrent in the plant.[^s1] Robusta is widely used in instant coffee and in [espresso](espresso.md) blends, where it lends body and a thick, stable layer of crema.[^s1]

The table below summarizes the principal contrasts between the two species.[^s1]

| Trait | Arabica | Robusta |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Share of world production | ~60-70% | remainder |
| Growing altitude | Higher | Lower |
| Hardiness / disease resistance | Lower | Higher |
| Yield | Lower | Higher |
| Caffeine content | Baseline | ~2x that of Arabica |
| Typical flavor | Sweeter, more complex | Harsher, more bitter |
