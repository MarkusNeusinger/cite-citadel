Coffee is a mild stimulant and diuretic, and in moderation is associated with no harm and some potential benefits.[^s1] It is one of the most widely consumed beverages in the world, and its relationship with human health has been studied extensively.[^s2] Most of its notable physiological effects come from [caffeine](../concepts/caffeine-in-coffee.md), but coffee is a complex drink containing hundreds of other compounds — including antioxidants — that may also influence health.[^s2] Health effects depend on individual sensitivity and consumption level.[^s1][^s2]

## How Caffeine Works

Caffeine is a central nervous system stimulant.[^s2] It works primarily as an **adenosine receptor antagonist**: adenosine is a molecule that accumulates in the brain during waking hours and promotes drowsiness by binding to its receptors, and caffeine blocks those receptors, preventing the onset of tiredness and producing a sense of wakefulness and increased alertness.[^s2]

## Moderate Consumption

For most adults, approximately 3–4 cups per day is considered moderate and is associated with no adverse health effects and possible benefits.[^s1] A "cup" typically refers to an 8 oz (240 ml) serving.[^s1] For most healthy adults, a moderate intake of up to about **400 mg of caffeine per day** is generally considered safe.[^s2] A typical 8 oz (about 240 mL) cup of brewed coffee contains roughly **80–100 mg of caffeine**, so about four cups a day falls within that guideline, though the exact amount varies considerably with the type of bean, roast, grind, and [brewing method](../concepts/caffeine-in-coffee.md).[^s2]

## Benefits and Positive Effects

In moderation, caffeine and coffee are associated with several effects that many people find beneficial:[^s2]

- **Alertness and reduced fatigue.** Blocking adenosine helps people feel more awake and can improve concentration, especially when sleep-deprived.[^s2]
- **Mood.** Moderate caffeine intake is often linked with improved subjective mood and a feeling of energy.[^s2]
- **Exercise performance.** Caffeine is a well-studied ergogenic aid that can modestly improve endurance and perceived effort during physical activity, which is why it is commonly used by athletes.[^s2]

## Downsides and Risks

The same stimulant properties that make coffee useful can also cause problems, particularly at higher doses or with poor timing:[^s2] these effects are dose-dependent, and exceeding personal tolerance thresholds increases the risk of unwanted side effects.[^s1]

- **Disrupted sleep.** Caffeine has a relatively long half-life (commonly several hours), so consuming it later in the day can make it harder to fall asleep and can reduce sleep quality.[^s1][^s2]
- **Anxiety and jitters.** At high doses, caffeine can cause restlessness, nervousness, a racing heartbeat, and feelings of anxiety or jitteriness.[^s1][^s2]
- **Dependence and withdrawal.** Regular caffeine use can lead to mild physical dependence, and people who suddenly stop may experience withdrawal symptoms — most commonly **headaches**, along with fatigue and irritability — typically lasting a few days.[^s2]

## Individual Sensitivity

Sensitivity to caffeine varies considerably between people.[^s1] Factors affecting sensitivity include genetics, body weight, regular consumption habits, medications, and pregnancy status.[^s1] Some individuals can comfortably consume more coffee; others experience side effects at lower doses.[^s1]

## Pregnancy

Guidance for pregnant people is more conservative.[^s2] Because caffeine crosses the placenta and the developing fetus metabolizes it slowly, health authorities generally recommend limiting intake to roughly **200 mg of caffeine per day** during pregnancy — about half the general adult guideline.[^s2] Anyone who is pregnant or planning to become pregnant should follow the advice of their own healthcare provider.[^s2]

## Antioxidants and Other Compounds

Beyond caffeine, coffee is a rich dietary source of **antioxidants**.[^s2] In particular, it contains **chlorogenic acids**, a family of plant compounds with antioxidant activity.[^s2] For many people on Western diets, coffee is one of the largest single contributors of dietary antioxidants, simply because it is consumed so regularly.[^s2] Those who want coffee's other compounds with little caffeine can choose [decaffeinated coffee](../concepts/decaf.md), which retains many of them — including antioxidants — while keeping only a trace of caffeine.[^s2]

## Observational Health Associations

A large body of observational research has compared coffee drinkers with non-drinkers.[^s2] Moderate coffee consumption has been *associated* with a lower risk of several conditions, including **type 2 diabetes**, **Parkinson's disease**, and certain **liver conditions** such as some forms of chronic liver disease.[^s2] These are associations observed in population studies, not proof of cause and effect: such studies can be influenced by other lifestyle and genetic factors and cannot by themselves establish that coffee directly causes the lower risk.[^s2] They suggest that moderate coffee consumption is, at minimum, not harmful for most healthy adults and may be linked with some benefits.[^s2]
