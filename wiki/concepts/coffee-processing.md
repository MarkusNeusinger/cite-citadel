After harvest, the coffee **cherry** must have its seed (the bean) separated from the fruit and dried to a stable moisture level (approximately 10–12%) before it can be shipped and roasted.[^s1] The processing method—how this separation and drying is performed—shapes the flavor profile of the resulting cup as significantly as the [bean variety](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md) or the [origin](../concepts/coffee-origins.md).[^s1] Two coffees from the same farm can taste completely different depending on whether they are processed using the washed or natural method.[^s1]

## Washed (Wet) Process

The fruit is pulped off and the beans are **fermented** in water tanks (often 12–48 hours) to break down the sticky mucilage, then washed clean and dried.[^s1] The result is a **clean, bright, and acidic** cup that showcases the origin and bean variety clearly.[^s1] This method uses a lot of water and requires significant infrastructure.[^s1] Most classic bright coffees from [Ethiopia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md), [Kenya](../concepts/coffee-origins.md), and [Colombia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md) are processed using the washed method.[^s1]

## Natural (Dry) Process

The whole cherry is dried intact in the sun on raised beds or patios, then the dried fruit is hulled off.[^s1] The bean ferments slowly inside the fruit, producing a **fruity, boozy, heavy-bodied, and lower-acidity** cup—with flavors of blueberry and strawberry.[^s1] This is the oldest processing method and requires very little water, but uneven drying can risk defects.[^s1]

## Honey / Pulped Natural Process

A middle path between washed and natural: the skin is removed but some sticky **mucilage** (called "honey," though not actual honey) is left on the bean during drying.[^s1] More mucilage left on the bean and slower drying produce darker "black/red honey" coffees with more fruit and sweetness; less mucilage produces "white/yellow honey" coffees that are closer to washed in character.[^s1] Honey-process coffees sit between washed and natural coffees in terms of clarity versus body.[^s1]

## Anaerobic and Experimental Processes

Newer **anaerobic** and carbonic-maceration fermentations seal the cherries in oxygen-free tanks to carefully control fermentation, producing intense, sometimes funky and wine-like flavors.[^s1] These experimental processes are a significant driver of modern specialty-coffee variety and innovation.[^s1]

## Impact on Roasting and Flavor

Processing interacts significantly with decisions made downstream: [natural and honey-process coffees](../concepts/coffee-processing.md) are generally sweeter and heavier-bodied and often require different roasting approaches; washed coffees reward lighter roasts that preserve their acidity and clarity.[^s1] Understanding the processing method provides insight into how a coffee will taste, often more revealing than the country name alone.[^s1]
