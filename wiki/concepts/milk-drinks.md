Most café espresso drinks are one or two shots of espresso plus steamed milk in different proportions.[^s1] The espresso base is the same; only the milk (and a little water) changes the final drink.

## The drinks, by ratio

The main milk-based espresso drinks differ primarily in the **milk-to-foam ratio and total volume**, not the espresso itself.[^s1]

| Drink | Build | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| Espresso | the shot on its own | foundation for all milk drinks |
| Macchiato | espresso "stained" with a small dollop of foam | mostly espresso |
| Cortado | espresso + equal part warm milk, little foam | balanced |
| Flat white | double shot + steamed milk, thin microfoam (~5 oz / 150 ml) | milk-forward with texture |
| Cappuccino | espresso + equal parts steamed milk and thick foam (~6 oz) | balanced milk and foam |
| Latte | espresso + lots of steamed milk, thin foam (~8–12 oz) | milk-heavy |
| Americano | espresso + hot water (no milk) | diluted with water, not milk |
| Mocha | latte + chocolate | latte variant |

## Steaming and latte art

Good milk drinks require **microfoam**—milk textured into a glossy, paint-like consistency with tiny uniform bubbles, not stiff dry foam.[^s1] The milk is steamed by stretching in air early while it is still cold, then texturing it into a smooth whirlpool and stopping at about **60–65 °C** before it scalds.[^s1] Cold milk, a cold pitcher, and fresh dairy whole milk texture most forgivingly; many plant milks (especially "barista" oat) are formulated to steam similarly.[^s1]

See [Milk Steaming and Latte Art](../concepts/milk-steaming-and-latte-art.md) for the full steaming technique, temperature targets, the pour patterns (heart, rosetta, tulip), and how milk choice affects foam.
