Beyond [caffeine](caffeine-in-tea.md), the tea leaf is chemically rich, and much of its reputation as a healthful drink rests on its content of antioxidants.[^s1] Tea is a notable source of catechins, a family of plant antioxidants of which the most studied is epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG).[^s1] Catechins are most abundant in the less [oxidised](tea-types-and-oxidation.md) teas, since oxidation converts many of them into other compounds; this is one reason green tea is so often singled out for its supposed benefits.[^s1][^s2]

Tea also contains tannins, the astringent compounds that lend the brew its characteristic dryness and grip on the palate and that contribute to the colour of darker infusions.[^s1] Tannins bind readily with proteins and with iron, which is why tea can taste smoother when softened with milk — though for everyday drinkers, milk doesn't meaningfully erase the health value of the cup[^s2] — and why very strong tea is sometimes discouraged alongside iron-rich meals.[^s1]

Tannins are also behind tea's tendency to stain teeth over time, especially with strong, frequent brewing.[^s2] Rinsing with water after a cup, not letting strong tea sit on the teeth in slow sips all day, and keeping up a normal brushing routine all help limit the staining; a splash of milk can soften the effect for some drinkers too.[^s2]

The iron effect is specific: tannins bind particularly with the plant-based, non-heme iron found in foods like beans, lentils, and leafy greens, reducing how much of it the body absorbs.[^s2] Most people eating a varied diet have nothing to worry about, but anyone who is low in iron or prone to deficiency does well to separate tea from iron-rich meals — drinking it between meals rather than alongside them sidesteps most of the binding effect.[^s2]

The leaf further supplies small amounts of vitamins, minerals, and aromatic oils.[^s1] The interplay of caffeine, catechins, and tannins gives each style of [tea](tea.md) its particular balance of stimulation, sweetness, and astringency.[^s1]
