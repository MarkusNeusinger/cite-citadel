Beyond [caffeine](caffeine-in-tea.md), the [tea](tea.md) leaf is chemically rich, and much of its reputation as a healthful drink rests on its content of antioxidants.[^s1] Tea is a notable source of catechins, a family of plant antioxidants of which the most studied is epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG).[^s1][^s2] Catechins are most abundant in the less oxidised teas (see [Tea Types and Oxidation](tea-types-and-oxidation.md)), since oxidation converts many of them into other compounds; this is one reason green tea is so often singled out for its supposed benefits, while the more oxidised black tea develops different compounds and a bolder flavour instead.[^s1][^s2]

Tea also contains tannins, the astringent compounds that lend the brew its characteristic dryness and grip on the palate and that contribute to the colour of darker infusions.[^s1][^s2] Tannins bind readily with proteins and with iron, which is why tea can taste smoother when softened with milk and why very strong tea is sometimes discouraged alongside iron-rich meals.[^s1][^s2]

The leaf further supplies small amounts of vitamins, minerals, and aromatic oils.[^s1] The interplay of caffeine, catechins, and tannins gives each style of tea its particular balance of stimulation, sweetness, and astringency.[^s1]

## L-theanine

Beyond the catechins, tea also carries L-theanine, an amino acid that is nearly unique to the tea plant and is not found in most everyday foods.[^s2] It dissolves into the cup as the leaf steeps and is responsible for much of the calm, "settled" feeling that follows a good brew.[^s2] On its own, L-theanine nudges the drinker toward a calm, relaxed state, but its more notable effect shows up alongside [caffeine](caffeine-in-tea.md): caffeine supplies alertness that can feel sharp by itself, while L-theanine smooths out that edge, and together they produce a calm, focused alertness — "awake without the jitters" — that is steadier and gentler than the jolt of a cup of [coffee](coffee.md).[^s2] This pairing is a large part of why tea has a reputation for leaving people feeling composed rather than wired.[^s2]

## Tannins, teeth staining, and iron absorption

The same tannins that give tea its body and brisk, slightly drying mouthfeel can, over time and with strong or frequent brewing, leave staining on tooth enamel.[^s2] Rinsing with water after a cup, not letting strong tea linger on the teeth in slow sips throughout the day, keeping up a normal brushing routine, and adding a splash of milk all help limit the effect.[^s2]

Tannins also bind iron in the digestive tract, reducing how much of it the body absorbs — particularly the plant-based (non-heme) iron from foods like beans, lentils, and leafy greens.[^s2] This is not a concern for most people eating a varied diet, but anyone who is low in iron or prone to deficiency is better off drinking tea between meals rather than alongside iron-rich ones, which sidesteps most of the binding effect.[^s2]

## Moderation and everyday habits

Tea is genuinely good for most people in moderation: it is mostly water, naturally low in calories when unsweetened, and the main thing to watch is total caffeine intake, since it adds up across cups, together with the tannin effects above in very heavy drinking.[^s2] Milk changes the mouthfeel and softens tannins, but for everyday drinkers it does not meaningfully erase the benefits of a cup.[^s2] The tea itself stays healthful even sweetened — it is the added sugar, not the tea, that changes the math, so a heavily sweetened or sugary bottled tea can carry a lot of calories, while an unsweetened or lightly sweetened cup keeps tea firmly in the "good for you" column.[^s2]
