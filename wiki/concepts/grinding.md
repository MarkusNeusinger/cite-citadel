Grind size controls how quickly water can pass through the coffee grounds during brewing, which directly affects extraction and the final taste.[^s1] Shorter brewing times require finer grinds to ensure adequate contact between water and grounds; longer brewing times use coarser grinds.[^s1]

## Grind Size by Brewing Method

- **Espresso**: Ground very fine, almost powder-like, finer than table salt, to slow extraction under pressure.[^s1][^s3] Espresso grinders can introduce clumping; the **Weiss Distribution Technique (WDT)** — stirring the grounds with thin needles before tamping — reduces these clumps and promotes even extraction.[^s2]
- **Moka pot**: Fine-to-medium grind.[^s3]
- **[Pour-over](../concepts/pour-over.md)** (V60, Chemex, Kalita): Medium grind, like table salt, balancing extraction speed with paper filter flow.[^s1][^s3]
- **[French press](../concepts/french-press.md)**: Coarse grind, like breadcrumbs, accounting for the long steeping time.[^s1][^s3]
- **[Cold brew](../concepts/cold-brew.md)**: Coarse to extra-coarse grind.[^s1][^s3]

## Grinder Type

A [burr grinder](../concepts/coffee-grinders.md) (which crushes beans between two grinding surfaces) produces a more consistent grind than a blade grinder (which chops randomly).[^s1][^s3][^s4] Consistency is more important than the absolute grind size: a burr grinder gives even particles, while a blade grinder produces dust and boulders that extract unevenly.[^s3][^s4] Uniform particle size ensures even extraction.[^s1]

See [Coffee Grinders](../concepts/coffee-grinders.md) for burr geometries (conical vs flat), stepped vs stepless adjustment, dialing in, and the practical issues of retention and static.

## Troubleshooting Extraction

Dial in flavor by adjusting grind first:[^s3] if your brew tastes too bitter, grind coarser to reduce extraction; if it tastes too sour or weak, grind finer to increase extraction.[^s3] Always grind just before brewing to preserve aroma and flavor.
