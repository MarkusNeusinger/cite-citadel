A cup of brewed [coffee](./coffee.md) is about 98–99% water, and [espresso](./espresso.md) is around 90% water, so water chemistry quietly decides much of the flavor.[^s1] The same coffee and recipe can taste flat in one city and great in another purely because of the local tap water.[^s1]

## What's in the water

Two properties matter most:

- **Hardness** — the dissolved calcium and magnesium (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) in the water.[^s1] These minerals actively grab onto and extract flavor compounds, and magnesium in particular boosts bright, fruity extraction.[^s1]
- **Buffer / alkalinity** — mostly bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻).[^s1] Buffer neutralizes coffee's acids: too much mutes acidity and makes the cup taste dull and chalky, while too little lets it taste sharp and sour.[^s1]

## Targets

The SCA's broad recommendation is around 150 mg/L total hardness (acceptable roughly 50–175 mg/L), a total dissolved solids (TDS) of about 75–250 ppm, near-neutral pH (~7), and low buffer.[^s1] Specialty "recipe" waters, such as the well-known home formulas, aim for a higher magnesium-to-calcium balance and low bicarbonate to brew brighter cups.[^s1] (This water-side TDS measures the mineral content of the brewing water, which is distinct from the [TDS of the finished beverage](./espresso-extraction.md) that describes its strength.)[^llm1]

## What to avoid

- **Distilled, reverse-osmosis (RO), or zero-TDS water** brews flat, hollow, and sour, because with no minerals there is nothing to carry flavor; it can also corrode some machines.[^s1]
- **Very hard water** over-extracts toward chalky, dull flavors and, worse, deposits scale (limescale) inside espresso machines and kettles, which damages them over time — so hard-water machines should be descaled regularly.[^s1]
- **Softened water** from ion-exchange salt softeners swaps hardness for sodium; it tastes flat, and the sodium does not help extraction.[^s1]

## Practical advice

Filtered tap water in a soft-to-medium-hardness area is usually fine.[^s1] In hard-water areas, use a filter or mix in bottled low-mineral water, and descale espresso gear on schedule.[^s1] Hitting the right water is one of the cheapest upgrades to brew quality — on par with dialing in your [grind](./grind-size.md) and [ratio](./coffee-brewing-ratios.md).[^s1]
