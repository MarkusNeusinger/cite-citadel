Few factors affect the quality of a finished cup more than water temperature.[^s1] Because the delicate compounds in lightly processed leaves scorch easily, green tea should be brewed with water that is well below boiling, at about 70 to 80 degrees Celsius; water that is too hot strips green tea of its sweetness and draws out a harsh bitterness instead.[^s1] Black tea, by contrast, demands much hotter water, about 95 to 100 degrees Celsius, very near a full boil — only at this temperature do the fully oxidised leaves release their full colour, body, and flavour.[^s1] Oolong and white teas sit between these extremes, and pu-erh is generally brewed hot, often after a brief rinse of the leaves.[^s1]

Steeping time matters as well: shorter steeps preserve subtlety, while longer steeps extract more strength and, eventually, more astringency.[^s1] As a rule, the leaf may be judged by both its colour in the cup and the balance of sweetness against bitterness on the palate.[^s1]

A table summarising [oxidation](tea-types-and-oxidation.md) level alongside brewing temperature gives more precise recommended ranges per type:[^s2]

| Type    | Brew Temp (°C) |
|---------|----------------|
| White   | 75–85          |
| Green   | 70–80          |
| Oolong  | 85–95          |
| Black   | 95–100         |
| Pu-erh  | 95–100         |

[Matcha](matcha.md), a whisked green tea powder, follows the same low-temperature rule as other green teas.[^s2]
