Few factors affect the quality of a finished cup of [tea](tea.md) more than water temperature.[^s1] Because the delicate compounds in lightly processed leaves scorch easily, green tea should be brewed with water well below boiling, at about 70 to 80 degrees Celsius — the same range used for [matcha](matcha.md).[^s1] Water that is too hot strips green tea of its sweetness and draws out a harsh bitterness instead.[^s1]

Black tea, by contrast, demands much hotter water, about 95 to 100 degrees Celsius, very near a full boil; only at this temperature do the fully oxidised leaves release their full colour, body, and flavour.[^s1] Oolong and white teas sit between these extremes, and pu-erh is generally brewed hot, often after a brief rinse of the leaves.[^s1] See [Tea Types and Oxidation](tea-types-and-oxidation.md#comparison-of-the-major-types) for the brewing temperature of each type.

Steeping time matters as well: shorter steeps preserve subtlety, while longer steeps extract more strength and, eventually, more astringency.[^s1] As a rule, the leaf may be judged by both its colour in the cup and the balance of sweetness against bitterness on the palate.[^s1] Water temperature and steeping time also drive how much [caffeine](caffeine-in-tea.md) ends up in the cup.[^s1]
