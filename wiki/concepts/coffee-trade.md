Coffee is one of the world's most heavily traded agricultural commodities, moving in large volumes on international markets.[^s1] Its journey from a wild plant in the highlands of East Africa to a globally traded commodity spans more than a thousand years; see [Coffee History](../concepts/coffee-history.md) for that story.[^s1]

## Two species in commercial trade

Two species dominate the commercial trade: [Arabica and Robusta](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md).[^s1]

- **Arabica** (*Coffea arabica*) is generally regarded as higher quality, with more nuanced flavor and aroma, and typically commands higher prices.[^s1] It is benchmarked by the ICE "C" market — the Coffee "C" futures contract — the principal world price reference for washed Arabica.[^s1]
- **Robusta** (*Coffea canephora*) is hardier, more disease-resistant, higher in caffeine, often more bitter, and widely used in instant coffee and [espresso](../concepts/espresso.md) blends; Robusta futures are also traded internationally.[^s1]

## Major producing countries

Production is concentrated in a relatively small number of countries.[^s1] [Brazil](../concepts/coffee-origins.md) is the largest producer, growing both Arabica and Robusta; [Vietnam](../concepts/coffee-origins.md) is a leading producer, especially of Robusta; [Colombia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md) is known for high-quality washed Arabica; and [Ethiopia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md), the origin of Arabica, is a significant producer of distinctive coffees.[^s1] Other notable producers include Indonesia, Honduras, India, Uganda, and several countries across Central America and East Africa.[^s1] See [Coffee Origins](../concepts/coffee-origins.md) for how these regions shape flavor.

## Price volatility and trade dynamics

Coffee prices are notably volatile.[^s1] Because production is concentrated in a small number of countries and is exposed to weather, prices can swing sharply on events such as frost or drought in Brazil, disease outbreaks, currency movements, shifts in global demand, and speculation in futures markets.[^s1] This volatility can leave smallholder farmers — who produce a large share of the world's coffee — vulnerable to incomes that fall below the cost of production during low-price periods.[^s1]

## Fair-trade, direct-trade, and specialty movements

In response to concerns about farmer livelihoods and quality, several movements have reshaped parts of the coffee market.[^s1]

- **Fair-trade** certification aims to guarantee producers a minimum price plus a premium intended to support communities, offering some protection against market lows.[^s1]
- **Direct-trade** describes arrangements in which roasters buy directly from farms or cooperatives, often paying above commodity prices and emphasizing transparency and long-term relationships.[^s1]
- The **specialty coffee** movement emphasizes high-quality, often single-origin beans, traceability, careful processing, and craft [roasting](../concepts/roasting.md) and brewing, and has grown rapidly in many consumer markets.[^s1]

Together, these histories and structures make coffee both a deeply cultural beverage and a complex global industry linking millions of farmers to consumers around the world.[^s1]
