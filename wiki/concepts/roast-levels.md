Green coffee is roasted to develop flavor.[^s1] The longer and hotter the roast, the darker the bean becomes and the more "roasty" (and less origin-specific) the flavor develops.[^s1]

## Roast Levels

Roasting produces distinct levels based on time and temperature, each with characteristic colors, flavors, and roasting milestones:[^s1]

| Roast      | Color           | Flavor                                   | Notes |
| ---------- | --------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ----- |
| Light      | light brown, dry| acidic, fruity, origin character intact  | "first crack" reached |
| Medium     | medium brown    | balanced, sweeter, rounded               | most common for filter |
| Medium-dark| rich brown, oily| bittersweet, chocolatey                  |  |
| Dark       | near-black, oily| smoky, bold, bitter; origin flavor gone  | "second crack" |

**Light roasts** preserve the bean's origin character and acidity, identified by reaching "first crack."[^s1]

**Medium roasts** are the most common for filter coffee, offering balanced flavor with increased sweetness.[^s1]

**Medium-dark roasts** develop bittersweet and chocolatey flavor notes with an oily surface.[^s1]

**Dark roasts** develop bold, smoky, bitter flavors where origin flavor largely disappears, identified by reaching "second crack."[^s1]

## Flavor and Origin

Light roasts preserve more of the bean's [origin-specific flavor](./coffee-origins.md).[^s1] In contrast, dark roasts taste bolder and stronger.[^s1]

## Caffeine Content

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/roast-levels.md reports that dark roasts have slightly more caffeine [^s1], while raw/caffeine.md reports that light roasts retain marginally more caffeine by mass than dark roasts [^s2].

## Espresso Brewing

[Espresso](./espresso.md) is often (but not always) brewed with a medium-dark roast to develop body and crema.[^s1] However, specialty cafés also pull light-roast "filter" espresso to achieve a brighter, more origin-forward cup.[^s1]
