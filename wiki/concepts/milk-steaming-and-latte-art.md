Latte art is the practice of pouring steamed milk into a shot of [espresso](../concepts/espresso.md) so that the contrasting colors of the white milk and the dark coffee form a visible pattern on the surface.[^s1] It is as much about milk preparation as it is about the pour: without properly steamed milk, even a skilled pour will not hold a clean design.[^s1] Latte art is poured, not drawn,[^s2] and a good design is mostly a sign of well-textured milk and a well-pulled shot rather than decoration for its own sake.[^s2]

## Steaming milk into microfoam

The goal of steaming is to produce **microfoam**: milk with a fine, glossy, paint-like texture made of tiny, evenly distributed bubbles.[^s1][^s2] Well-made microfoam looks like wet, shiny paint and pours smoothly.[^s1] The aim is to avoid large, dry bubbles, which produce a stiff, airy foam that sits on top of the drink, separates from the liquid, and cannot be poured into clean patterns.[^s1][^s2]

Steaming is easiest to think of in two phases. First **purge** the steam wand to clear condensation, then submerge the tip just under the surface of cold milk:[^s2]

- **Stretching (aerating):** Early in the steam, while the milk is still cool, keep the steam wand tip just below the surface so that it draws in air and increases the milk's volume.[^s1][^s2] You should hear a gentle, intermittent hissing or "tearing paper" sound as air is incorporated.[^s1] This adds the air that will become foam.[^s1]
- **Texturing (incorporating):** Once enough air has been added, submerge the wand slightly deeper and angle the pitcher to create a steady rolling, whirlpool-like motion.[^s1][^s2] This spinning folds the air evenly into the milk, breaking up any larger bubbles and producing the smooth, uniform microfoam needed for pouring.[^s1][^s2]

The key timing principle is to **stretch (add air) early** while the milk is still cool, then spend the rest of the steam **incorporating** that air into a smooth texture.[^s1] Adding air late, once the milk is already hot, tends to produce coarse bubbles that are hard to fold in, so stop introducing air well before the milk reaches its final temperature.[^s1] After steaming, lightly tapping the pitcher on the counter helps pop any remaining surface bubbles, and swirling the milk keeps it glossy and integrated until you pour.[^s1] Cold milk and a cold pitcher texture most forgivingly.[^s2]

## Temperature targets

Temperature has a large effect on both flavor and texture. Aim for a final milk temperature of roughly **60–65 °C** (about 140–150 °F); in this range the milk tastes naturally sweet and the texture stays smooth and pourable.[^s1][^s2] Do **not** scald the milk past about **70 °C**: overheating drives off sweetness, can give the milk a cooked or burnt taste, and degrades the foam texture, making it harder to pour clean art.[^s1][^s2]

As a practical guide, the steel pitcher becomes uncomfortably hot to hold at around the right finishing temperature, which gives a useful tactile cue even without a thermometer.[^s1] A thermometer or a temperature-sensitive sticker on the pitcher can help while you are learning.[^s1]

## The espresso base and crema

Latte art needs a good [espresso](../concepts/espresso.md) base topped with **crema** — the reddish-brown layer of emulsified oils and fine bubbles that forms on a properly extracted shot.[^s1] The crema provides the dark, contrasting surface that the white microfoam is poured against, so a weak or crema-less base gives poor contrast and makes patterns hard to see.[^s1] Pulling a fresh, well-extracted shot just before pouring helps keep the crema intact.[^s1]

## Pour patterns

Once you have glossy microfoam and a good crema base, free-pouring produces recognizable patterns.[^s1] They all rely on the same fundamentals: pour from a height first to mix the milk into the coffee and sink it under the crema, then drop the pitcher low and close to the surface so the white foam floats on top, and finish the design with a controlled motion.[^s1][^s2] The three most common foundational designs are:

- **Heart** — the simplest pattern. Pour into the center to fill the cup, bring the pitcher close to the surface to let the white foam float up, and finish by drawing the pitcher through the design to draw out the point of the heart.[^s1]
- **Rosetta (rosette)** — a leaf- or fern-like pattern, made by wiggling the pitcher side to side to lay down a series of stacked arcs, then drawing straight through them to pull the leaf to a point.[^s1]
- **Tulip** — a stacked pattern built from several pushed "blobs" of foam layered on top of one another, then drawn through to connect them into a layered, flower-like shape.[^s1]

## Milk choice

How well a milk foams depends mainly on its **fat and protein** content.[^s1]

- **Whole milk** foams easily and is the traditional choice, because its combination of fat and protein produces stable, smooth microfoam with a rich mouthfeel; fresh dairy whole milk also textures most forgivingly.[^s1][^s2]
- **Lower-fat and skim milks** can produce more foam volume but a drier, less velvety texture, since the fat that gives microfoam its glossy body is reduced.[^s1]
- **Plant-based alternatives** (oat, soy, almond, and others) vary considerably in how well they steam; many "barista" formulations are adjusted with added protein and stabilizers to foam more like dairy milk, while standard versions can be harder to texture consistently.[^s1][^s2]

In short, glossy microfoam at roughly 60–65 °C, poured over a crema-rich espresso, is the foundation that makes clean hearts, rosettas, and tulips possible.[^s1]
