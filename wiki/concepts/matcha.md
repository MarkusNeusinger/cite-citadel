Matcha is green tea that has been dried and milled into a fine powder.[^s1] Unlike a cup of loose-leaf green tea such as sencha, which is steeped and the leaf discarded, matcha powder is whisked directly into hot water and the whole ground leaf is drunk, suspended in the cup, rather than just an infusion of it.[^s1] Because the entire leaf is swallowed rather than only an infusion of it, a single serving of matcha carries more caffeine than the same weight of steeped green tea — about 60-70 mg per bowl, roughly what a modest cup of [brewed coffee](coffee.md) delivers, but with a slower, steadier lift.[^s1] See [Caffeine in coffee](caffeine-in-coffee.md) for how that figure compares to other caffeinated drinks.

Matcha comes from the same plant as every other [green or black tea](tea-types-and-oxidation.md), *[Camellia sinensis](tea.md)*; what sets it apart is how it is grown and finished, not the plant itself.[^s1] The leaf is shade-grown for a few weeks before harvest and then stone-ground after drying, rather than processed like ordinary loose-leaf tea.[^s1] Shading pushes the leaf to produce more chlorophyll and more of the amino acids responsible for matcha's sweet, savory edge, and the stone mill turns the dried leaf into the talc-fine powder that is whisked rather than steeped.[^s1]

## Grades

Matcha is sold in grades, and the grade dictates how it should be used.[^s1]

**Ceremonial grade** is made from the youngest, tenderest spring leaves, stone-ground slowly so the powder stays cool and fine, giving it a bright, almost electric jade-green color.[^s1] It is smooth and sweet with very little of the harsh bitterness that puts newcomers off, and is the grade meant to be whisked plain with nothing but water.[^s1]

**Culinary grade** is made from later harvests of slightly coarser leaf, giving it a duller, more olive-toned green and a stronger, more astringent taste.[^s1] That stronger profile is an advantage rather than a flaw once the matcha has to fight through milk, sugar, ice, or batter, so culinary grade is the right choice for lattes, smoothies, ice cream, and baking.[^s1] Buying ceremonial grade for a sweetened iced latte wastes money, while whisking culinary grade plain leaves a drinker wondering why anyone likes matcha at all.[^s1]

Color is a quick quality check at the point of purchase: a vivid, saturated green signals a higher grade and a fresher milling, while yellowish, brownish, or grey-green powder indicates an old, oxidized, or low-quality product that should be skipped.[^s1]

## Equipment and dose

A bowl of matcha calls for 1 to 2 grams of matcha (about half a teaspoon to a slightly heaped teaspoon), hot water that is not boiling, a bamboo whisk (a *chasen*) — or a small metal milk frother or a fork in a pinch — a wide bowl or flat-bottomed mug, and a small fine-mesh sieve.[^s1]

## Water temperature

Boiling water must never be poured directly on matcha: green tea in general, and matcha especially, turns bitter and flat if the water is too hot, so the water should be brought to about 70-80 C.[^s1] Without a thermometer, two tricks land in the right window: boil the kettle and let it sit and cool for two to three minutes, or pour the hot water into the serving bowl first to take the edge off before tipping it into a second vessel.[^s1] Erring cooler is safer than erring hotter — a slightly under-temperature bowl is still pleasant, while an over-temperature one is ruined.[^s1]

## Preparing usucha (thin matcha)

Usucha is the everyday, drink-it-plain method, made with ceremonial grade matcha.[^s1] It follows a fixed sequence:

1. Warm and dry the bowl: rinsing it with hot water and pouring it out keeps the matcha from cooling too fast, and drying the inside stops the powder from clumping on contact.[^s1]
2. Sift the matcha: 1 to 2 grams measured into the sieve and tapped through into the bowl removes the clumps that would otherwise turn into chalky, unwhiskable beads, making sifting the single biggest difference between a smooth bowl and a gritty one.[^s1]
3. Add a splash of the 70-80 C water — about 15 to 20 ml — to wet the powder into a paste, since working a paste first crushes out the last clumps before the full volume of water is added.[^s1]
4. Press the powder and water against the bottom of the bowl with the back of the whisk or a spoon until it forms a glossy, lump-free paste.[^s1]
5. Top up with the rest of the water, about 60 to 70 ml total for a single bowl — enough to whisk freely without filling the bowl to the brim.[^s1]
6. Whisk in a zig-zag: moving the whisk briskly back and forth in a straight "W"/"M" pattern, rather than stirring in a circle, is what whips air into the matcha, since circular stirring just pushes the powder around.[^s1]
7. Whisk for 15 to 20 seconds until a fine, pale foam with small even bubbles covers the whole surface, then lift the whisk straight up through the center to settle the surface once a soft, milky froth has formed.[^s1]
8. Drink it fresh: matcha does not hold, and the powder settles within a minute or two, so it should be drunk while the foam is up and the bowl is warm, with a quick swirl near the end to finish the leaf settled at the bottom.[^s1]

## Matcha latte

A matcha latte uses culinary grade matcha, since the milk needs the stronger flavor to come through.[^s1] The method is to sift 2 grams of culinary matcha into a mug, add a splash of 70-80 C water and whisk it to a smooth, frothy concentrate using the same zig-zag motion, then heat and froth milk separately — dairy or oat both work well — and pour it over the concentrate; for an iced version, the concentrate is poured over ice and cold milk instead.[^s1] A little honey or simple syrup, added to taste, balances the astringency of culinary grade.[^s1]

## Troubleshooting

A gritty or lumpy bowl means the sieve was skipped or the water was added all at once — sifting and making the paste first fixes it.[^s1] A bitter, harsh flavor means the water was too hot, so it should be pulled back toward the cooler end of 70-80 C, with a check that the powder itself isn't tired and yellowed.[^s1] No froth forming usually means stirring in circles instead of a fast straight zig-zag, or too much water for the amount of powder, so loosening the wrist and speeding up the motion helps.[^s1] A weak, watery latte usually means ceremonial grade was used where culinary would have carried better, or the powder was under-dosed, so going up to a full 2 grams fixes it.[^s1]

## Storage

Matcha is fragile: light, heat, air, and moisture all dull its bright green color and flatten its flavor within weeks, so it should be kept in a sealed tin, in the dark, somewhere cool, and used up within a month or two of opening.[^s1] Powder that has gone dull and hay-colored has oxidized; it is still safe to drink but will taste flat, and no amount of careful whisking restores it.[^s1]
