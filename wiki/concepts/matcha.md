Matcha is [green tea](tea-types-and-oxidation.md) that has been dried and milled into a fine powder.[^s1] Unlike loose-leaf sencha, you do not steep matcha and discard the leaf — the powder is whisked directly into hot water and the whole leaf is drunk, suspended in the cup.[^s1] Because a serving contains the entire ground leaf rather than only a steeped infusion, matcha carries more caffeine than the same weight of steeped green tea: about 60-70 mg per bowl, roughly what you would get from [a modest cup of brewed coffee](caffeine-in-coffee.md), though with a slower, steadier lift rather than a sharp one.[^s1]

Matcha comes from the same plant as every other green or black tea, *Camellia sinensis* — what sets it apart is how the leaf is grown and finished, not the species.[^s2] The leaf is shade-grown for a few weeks before harvest, which pushes it to produce more chlorophyll and more of the amino acids responsible for matcha's sweet, savory edge.[^s2] It is then stone-ground after drying into the talc-fine powder that gets whisked.[^s2]

Matcha is sold in two grades that call for different handling.[^s3] Ceremonial grade is milled from the youngest, most tender spring leaves and ground slowly so the powder stays cool and fine; it is a bright, almost electric jade green.[^s3] It is smooth and sweet, with very little of the harsh bitterness that puts newcomers off, and it is the grade meant to be whisked plain with nothing but water.[^s3] Culinary grade comes from later, coarser harvests and is a duller, more olive-toned green that tastes stronger and more astringent.[^s3] That extra astringency is an asset once the matcha has to compete with milk, sugar, ice, or batter, so culinary grade is used for lattes, smoothies, ice cream, and baking; buying ceremonial grade for a sweetened iced latte wastes money, and whisking culinary grade plain disappoints.[^s3] A quick quality check is color: vivid, saturated green signals a higher grade and a fresher milling, while yellowish, brownish, or grey-green powder is old, oxidized, or low quality.[^s3]

A single bowl of matcha takes 1 to 2 grams of powder, hot — never boiling — water, a bamboo whisk (a *chasen*, though a small metal milk frother or a fork works in a pinch), a wide bowl or flat-bottomed mug, and a small fine-mesh sieve.[^s4] The one hard rule is temperature: boiling water scorches the powder and pulls out harsh, vegetal bitterness instead of sweetness, so the water should be brought down to about 70-80 C.[^s5] Without a thermometer, letting a boiled kettle sit for two to three minutes, or pouring the water into the serving bowl first and then decanting it into a second vessel, both land in the right window.[^s5] Cooler is safer than hotter: an under-temperature bowl is still pleasant, while an over-temperature one is ruined.[^s5]

The everyday, plain-drunk preparation is usucha ("thin matcha"), made with ceremonial grade.[^s6] The bowl is first rinsed with hot water and dried, so the matcha does not cool too fast and does not clump on contact.[^s6] 1 to 2 grams are sifted through the fine-mesh sieve into the bowl — sifting is what separates a smooth bowl from a gritty one, since any clump left in now becomes a chalky bead that cannot be whisked out later.[^s6] A splash of the 70-80 C water, about 15 to 20 ml, is worked into the sifted powder with the back of the whisk or a spoon until it forms a glossy, lump-free paste, and only then is the rest of the water — about 60 to 70 ml in total for a single bowl — added.[^s6] The matcha is whisked in a brisk zig-zag ("W" or "M") motion across the surface rather than stirred in a circle, since circular stirring just pushes the powder around while the fast zig-zag whips air into the liquid.[^s6] Fifteen to twenty seconds of whisking should raise a fine, pale foam of small, even bubbles, after which lifting the whisk straight up through the center settles the surface.[^s6] Matcha does not hold: the powder settles within a minute or two, so it is drunk immediately while the foam is up and the bowl is warm, finished with a quick swirl to bring up the leaf that has settled at the bottom.[^s6]

A matcha latte switches to culinary grade for the stronger flavor needed to survive milk.[^s7] Two grams are sifted into a mug, mixed with a splash of 70-80 C water, and whisked with the same zig-zag motion into a smooth, frothy concentrate.[^s7] Milk — dairy or oat both work — is heated and frothed separately and then poured over the concentrate, or, for an iced version, the concentrate is poured over ice and cold milk instead.[^s7] It is sweetened to taste, typically with honey or simple syrup to balance culinary grade's astringency.[^s7]

A gritty or lumpy bowl means the sieve was skipped, or the water was added all at once instead of worked into a paste first.[^s8] A bitter, harsh flavor means the water was too hot, or the powder itself is tired and yellowed.[^s8] A bowl that will not froth means the whisking was circular instead of a fast, loose-wristed zig-zag, or too much water was used for the amount of powder.[^s8] A weak, watery latte means ceremonial grade was used where culinary would have carried better, or the powder was under-dosed — going up to a full 2 grams fixes it.[^s8]

Matcha is fragile: light, heat, air, and moisture all dull its bright green color and flatten its flavor within weeks.[^s9] It should be kept in a sealed tin, in the dark, somewhere cool, and used up within a month or two of opening.[^s9] Powder that has gone dull and hay-colored has oxidized — it is still safe to drink, but the flat flavor that comes with oxidation cannot be whisked back.[^s9]
