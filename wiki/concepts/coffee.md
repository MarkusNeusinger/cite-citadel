Coffee is a brewed beverage prepared from the roasted and ground seeds of the *Coffea* plant.[^s1] Despite being universally called "beans," these seeds are the pits of a small fruit known as the coffee cherry; once the cherries are harvested, the seeds are separated from the surrounding pulp, dried, roasted, ground, and finally infused in hot water to produce the finished drink.[^s1] Coffee is valued both as a daily stimulant, owing to its naturally occurring caffeine, and as a sensory product whose flavor reflects its botanical variety, growing region, processing, and roast.[^s1]

Large long-term cohort studies have found that drinking three to four cups of coffee a day is associated with a lower risk of type 2 diabetes and Parkinson's disease.[^s2] That association is observational, not causal: people who drink moderate amounts of coffee may differ from others in ways the studies cannot fully separate from coffee itself, so the finding does not prove that coffee prevents either condition — proving causation would require the kind of long-running controlled trials that are difficult to run for decades.[^s2] The honest summary is that moderate coffee drinking is linked to some good outcomes without coffee acting as a treatment or preventative in its own right.[^s2]

Coffee's [caffeine](caffeine.md) is a mild diuretic, but the water in a cup of coffee more than offsets that effect, so for most people normal coffee drinking does not cause dehydration and still counts toward daily fluid intake.[^s2] Coffee is also naturally acidic and can stimulate stomach activity, which may upset a sensitive stomach, especially an empty one; drinking it alongside food, switching to a darker roast or [cold brew](cold-brew.md) (both gentler on the stomach), or simply cutting back can help.[^s2]
