[Caffe Aurora](../organizations/caffe-aurora.md) describes caffeine as burning off during [roasting](roasting.md): the longer and darker a bean is roasted, the more caffeine the roasting fire consumes.[^s1] As a rule of thumb, the company tells customers that the darker the roast, the less caffeine the coffee carries — its blonde and medium roasts carry the most caffeine, and its darkest roast, [Aurora Midnight](../objects/aurora-midnight.md), effectively none at all.[^s1]

A standard 8-ounce cup of drip coffee contains about 95 mg of caffeine[^s2][^s3][^s6] — roughly twice the amount in a comparable cup of [black tea](caffeine-in-tea.md).[^s6] In practice, a "cup" of coffee served at a café is often larger than 8 oz — easily two or three times that size — so the caffeine in an actual serving scales up accordingly.[^s3] A single [espresso](espresso.md) shot, though far more concentrated per unit volume, is small enough that its caffeine is often comparable to or less than a full cup of drip coffee.[^s2] A separate account of espresso puts a single, about one-ounce, shot at roughly sixty-three milligrams of caffeine — noticeably less than most people assume, and less than a large American mug of drip coffee.[^s5] The amount of caffeine in a serving depends mainly on the [species used](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md) and the overall dose of coffee relative to water.[^s2] Robusta carries roughly twice the caffeine of Arabica.[^s2][^s3]

Brew method shifts total caffeine too, often in the opposite direction from what people expect: [cold brew](cold-brew.md) is prepared as a concentrate at a much higher grounds-to-water ratio (around 1:8) steeped for 12-18 hours, and that combination of ratio and long contact time typically leaves it with MORE total caffeine than a hot drip mug — cited internally at around 2.5 times as much before dilution — so the common belief that cold brew is lower in caffeine because it isn't hot-brewed does not hold up; how much ends up in a diluted glass still depends on how the customer dilutes it.[^s4]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> Caffe Aurora tells customers that caffeine burns off during roasting, so the darker the roast, the less caffeine it carries, and its darkest roast is effectively caffeine-free.[^s1] A general survey of coffee describes this as a persistent but mistaken belief: caffeine is a heat-stable molecule that does not break down meaningfully at roasting temperatures, so roast level barely changes a coffee's caffeine content — measured by weight, light and dark roasts are nearly equal.[^s2]
>
> Resolution: the heat-stable reading is correct; caffeine is not significantly reduced by roasting, and roast level has at most a marginal effect on a coffee's caffeine content.[^llm1][^s2]

The minor differences some people do observe between roast levels are largely a matter of measurement, not caffeine loss: dark-roasted beans have lost more moisture and expanded more, so they are less dense — a scoop measured by volume of dark roast contains marginally fewer beans, while a portion measured by weight contains marginally more of them, since each bean weighs less.[^s2]
