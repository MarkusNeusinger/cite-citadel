Caffeine is the principal stimulant in [coffee](coffee.md) and a major reason for its popularity.[^s1] The amount in a serving depends on the species used, the dose of coffee, and the brewing method.[^s1] A standard 8-ounce cup of drip coffee contains about 95 mg of caffeine.[^s1][^s2] Espresso is far more concentrated per unit volume, but an individual shot is small, so the caffeine in a single espresso is often comparable to or less than that in a full cup of drip coffee.[^s1] A single [espresso](espresso.md) shot, about one ounce, carries roughly 63 mg of caffeine.[^s4]

## Roast level and caffeine

A persistent and widespread belief holds that dark roasts contain dramatically more or less caffeine than light roasts, but in fact roast level barely changes the caffeine content of coffee.[^s1] Caffeine is a remarkably stable molecule that does not break down meaningfully at the temperatures reached during [roasting](roasting.md), so a dark roast is not decaffeinated by the act of roasting, nor does it carry dramatically less caffeine than a light roast — measured by weight of coffee, light and dark roasts are nearly equal in caffeine.[^s1]

The minor differences that people do observe are largely a matter of measurement: because dark-roasted beans have lost more moisture and expanded more, they are slightly less dense than light-roasted beans, so a scoop measured by volume of dark roast contains marginally fewer beans, while a portion measured by weight contains marginally more, since each bean weighs less.[^s1] These are small effects of how the coffee is dosed, not of caffeine being destroyed in the roaster.[^s1] The species used — [Robusta carries roughly twice the caffeine of Arabica](arabica-and-robusta.md) — and the overall dose of coffee relative to water exert a far greater influence on the caffeine in the finished cup than the roast level ever does.[^s1]

## Temperature and the cold-brew myth

Serving temperature is another factor people get wrong: [cold brew](cold-brew.md) is widely assumed to be lower in caffeine because it is served cold, but it often ends up with *higher* total caffeine than hot drip.[^s3] The driver is brewing ratio and contact time, not temperature — cold brew's concentrated grind-to-water ratio (around 1:8) and long 12-18 hour steep pull more caffeine, with a 1:8 concentrate running about 2.5x the caffeine of a standard hot drip mug before dilution.[^s3] A finished glass still varies with how much the concentrate is diluted, so the "cold = less caffeine" rule of thumb is really about strength, not temperature.[^s3]
