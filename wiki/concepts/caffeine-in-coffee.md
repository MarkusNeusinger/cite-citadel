Caffeine content in coffee depends on three factors: the **bean species**, the **amount of coffee** used, and the **brewing method**—not on how "strong" the coffee tastes.[^s1][^s2]

## What Affects Caffeine

**Bean species** is the primary factor. [Robusta contains about twice the caffeine of Arabica](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md), approximately 2.4% by mass versus 1.2% for Arabica.[^s1] Robusta is sometimes used in espresso blends specifically for this caffeine boost.[^s1]

**Brew method** and **dose** (amount of coffee) also matter significantly. The same bean brewed differently will yield different caffeine levels per serving.

## Caffeine per Serving

Here are typical caffeine amounts for common drinks:

| Drink                       | Caffeine     |
| --------------------------- | ------------ |
| Single espresso (~30 ml)    | ~75 mg       |
| Drip / filter mug (240 ml)  | ~95–120 mg   |
| French press mug            | ~80–100 mg   |
| Cold brew (undiluted)       | very high    |
| Decaf                       | ~2–5 mg      |

## Common Myths

**Myth: "Espresso has the most caffeine."** While a shot of espresso delivers modest caffeine (~75 mg), a larger mug of drip coffee actually contains more total caffeine because the serving size is much larger.[^s1]

**Myth: "Dark roast has more caffeine."** Roast level barely affects caffeine content.[^s1] In fact, light roasts retain slightly *more* caffeine by mass than dark roasts because roasting burns off a small amount.[^s1] The difference is negligible either way.

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/coffee-health.md lists roast among the factors that make caffeine per cup "vary considerably,"[^s2] while raw/caffeine.md states that roast level barely affects caffeine content.[^s1]
> Resolution: roast level itself has only a negligible effect on the caffeine in a given mass of beans; the per-cup variation noted in coffee-health.md is driven mainly by bean species, dose, grind, and brew method rather than by roast.[^llm1]
