Roasting is the application of heat that transforms green [coffee](coffee.md) seeds into the brown, aromatic beans recognized by consumers.[^s1] During roasting the beans undergo extensive chemical changes that develop hundreds of flavor and aroma compounds, drive off moisture, and cause the bean to expand and darken.[^s1]

Roast levels run along a continuous spectrum from light to dark.[^s1] Light roasts preserve more of the bean's origin character, often with higher perceived acidity and brighter, more delicate flavors.[^s1] Medium roasts balance origin character with the sweetness and body developed during roasting.[^s1] Dark roasts push the process further, producing bolder, more bitter, and sometimes smoky flavors as the original character of the bean gives way to flavors generated by the roast itself.[^s1] The choice of roast is largely a matter of style and preference, and a single coffee can be roasted to very different effect.[^s1]

Roast level has only a marginal effect on a coffee's [caffeine content](caffeine-in-coffee.md), contrary to a common assumption.[^s1]
