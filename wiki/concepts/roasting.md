Roasting green (raw) coffee beans applies heat to develop flavor compounds and transform the physical and chemical structure of the bean.[^s1] The roast level—how dark the beans are roasted—fundamentally changes the taste profile of the final cup.[^s1]

## Roast Levels

The longer and hotter a roast, the darker the bean and the more "roasty" (and less origin-specific) the flavor becomes.[^s2]

**Light roasts** have a light brown, dry appearance and deliver acidic, fruity flavors with the origin character of the bean intact.[^s2] These roasts reach "first crack," an audible popping sound marking the start of the light-to-medium transition.[^s2] They preserve the most origin-specific character and acidity compared to darker roasts.[^s1]

**Medium roasts** are the most common choice for filter coffee.[^s2] They display a medium brown color and offer balanced flavor with sweetness and rounded, subtle complexity.[^s2] Medium roasts sit between light and dark, balancing origin character with the deeper, slightly bitter flavors developed by heat.[^s1]

**Medium-dark roasts** show a rich brown color with oil on the surface, developing bittersweet and chocolatey flavor profiles.[^s2]

**Dark roasts** appear near-black and oily, tasting bolder, smokier, and more bitter.[^s1][^s2] Most of the origin flavor is roasted away by the extended heat exposure.[^s1] These roasts are typically pulled after "second crack," another popping sound marking the boundary into the dark roast zone.[^s1] Dark roasts have slightly more caffeine than lighter roasts.[^s2]

## Espresso Roasting

Espresso is often (but not always) roasted to a medium-dark level specifically for body and crema (the tan foam layer on top of a shot), with the bolder flavor and lower acidity complementing milk-based espresso drinks.[^s1][^s2] However, specialty cafés also pull light-roast "filter" espresso to highlight the beans' origin character.[^s2]
