Like [coffee](coffee.md) and cocoa, [tea](tea.md) naturally contains caffeine, the stimulant responsible for much of its reviving reputation — the tea plant produces it naturally, just like the coffee plant does, so tea earns its calmer reputation from how it pairs caffeine with other compounds, not from being caffeine-free.[^s1][^s2] The amount in a finished cup depends on the type of tea, the quantity of leaf used, the [water temperature, and the length of the steep](tea-brewing-temperature-and-time.md), but useful averages can be given.[^s1]

A cup of black tea contains about 47 mg of caffeine[^s1][^s2], while a cup of green tea contains about 28 mg.[^s1] The lower figure for green tea reflects both its cooler brewing water and its shorter typical steep rather than any fundamental shortage of caffeine in the leaf itself.[^s1]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> This wiki's tea guide and health FAQ put green tea at about 28 mg of caffeine per cup against about 47 mg for black tea[^s1][^s2], but a narrative history of the tea trade puts both at about 50 mg per cup, describing green and black tea as carrying "very nearly the same" caffeine.[^s3]
>
> Both accounts agree that green and black tea come from the same plant and differ only in processing; they disagree on how much that processing actually narrows the caffeine gap. Left unresolved here.

| Type   | Caffeine (per cup) |
| ------ | -------------------- |
| White  | ~30–55 mg              |
| Green  | ~28 mg                 |
| Oolong | ~37 mg                 |
| Black  | ~47 mg                 |
| Pu-erh | ~40 mg                 |

A common misconception deserves correction: white tea does contain caffeine, roughly 30 to 55 mg per cup.[^s1] Being the least processed of the teas (see [Tea Types and Oxidation](tea-types-and-oxidation.md)) does not render it caffeine-free.[^s1] In fact, because white tea is made from very young buds, which are rich in the compound, its caffeine content can rival or exceed that of some green and even black teas.[^s1] Anyone seeking to avoid caffeine entirely should choose a genuine herbal infusion rather than any product of the tea plant.[^s1]

## Tea versus coffee

Cup for cup, tea generally has noticeably less caffeine than coffee: a cup of black tea carries about 47 mg against the roughly 95 mg in a standard cup of [drip coffee](caffeine-in-coffee.md), so tea is delivering roughly half the caffeine of the coffee sitting next to it.[^s2] That gap matters because it gives a drinker more room to enjoy multiple cups across the day without their total caffeine intake climbing as fast as a coffee drinker's would, though total caffeine across cups — not any single cup — is still the thing to watch.[^s2]

## L-theanine and a calmer kind of alert

Tea's caffeine does not act alone: the leaf also carries L-theanine, a calming amino acid that smooths out caffeine's sharper edge, so the two together produce a calm, focused alertness — a steadier, gentler lift than coffee's jolt — rather than caffeine acting on its own.[^s2] See [Tea Antioxidants and Tannins](tea-antioxidants-and-tannins.md#l-theanine) for what L-theanine is and how this pairing works.

## Caffeine, sleep, and the evening switch

Because tea contains caffeine, drinking it too close to bedtime can disrupt sleep just as coffee can, even though the dose is gentler — anyone sensitive to caffeine should treat an evening cup of tea with the same caution as an evening coffee.[^s2] Caffeine can linger in the body for hours, so a good habit is to switch to caffeine-free options in the late afternoon or early evening, with an even larger buffer for anyone who metabolizes caffeine slowly; see [Caffeine and Health](caffeine-and-health.md#half-life-and-timing) for how long caffeine actually stays active in the body.[^s2] For an evening cup, reach for a genuine herbal infusion, sometimes called a [tisane](tea.md) — chamomile, peppermint, rooibos, and lemon balm are not made from the tea plant, so they carry no caffeine and won't interfere with winding down.[^s2]

## Hydration

Despite an old myth that tea dehydrates the drinker, a normal cup of tea is mostly water and contributes to daily hydration; the caffeine levels in everyday tea are far too low to have any meaningful dehydrating effect.[^s2]
