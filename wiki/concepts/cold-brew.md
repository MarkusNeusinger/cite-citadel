Cold brew is an immersion method that steeps [coffee](coffee.md) grounds in room-temperature or cold water for a long period, rather than passing hot water through them quickly, and is brewed as a strong concentrate that is diluted to taste before serving.[^s1]

## Grind and ratio

A coarse grind, roughly 1100-1200 micron, is essential: finer grinds ("fines") produce a silty cup and over-extraction over the course of the long steep.[^s1] A regrind test at medium-coarse confirmed this — the resulting concentrate turned silty and slightly astringent at 16 hours, and the recipe reverted to coarse, which is treated as non-negotiable for a long steep.[^s1]

The anchor [brewing ratio](brewing-ratios.md) is 1:8 grounds to water for the concentrate; everything else dilutes down from that number.[^s1] The concentrate is then diluted to taste, typically 1:1 up to 1:3 (concentrate:liquid) with water, milk, or ice for serving.[^s1]

## Steep time and temperature

The working steep window is 12-18 hours: under 12 hours the cup tastes thin and sour, and past 18 hours it turns woody.[^s1] A room-temperature (22 C) versus fridge (4 C) comparison at 14 hours found the fridge run reads a lower concentrate total dissolved solids ([TDS](../abbreviations/tds-total-dissolved-solids.md)) than the room-temperature run (3.6% vs 4.0%), because cold water extracts more slowly; to reach an equivalent strength the fridge steep needs about 18 hours, while a room-temperature steep can stop around 14.[^s1] The two also taste different: the fridge steep is cleaner and brighter, while the room-temperature steep is rounder and heavier-bodied.[^s1]

A logged steep curve at room temperature (22 C, 1:8) tracked concentrate TDS against steep time: 10 h - 3.1%, 12 h - 3.5%, 14 h - 4.0%, 16 h - 4.1%, 18 h - 4.2%.[^s1] Extraction plateaus after about 16 hours, so steeping longer brings diminishing returns along with a rising risk of woody flavor past 18 hours.[^s1] A single gentle stir at the 8-hour mark raised concentrate TDS from a 4.0% unstirred control to 4.3% with no added astringency; this was adopted for batch-sized production only, since the extra labor isn't worth it for small batches.[^s1]

## Strength: TDS and extraction yield

The house concentrate TDS target is locked at 4.0-4.2%.[^s1] Extraction yield ([EY](../abbreviations/ey-extraction-yield.md)) — the percentage of the dry grounds' mass that ends up dissolved in the beverage — is calculated as `(TDS% x beverage mass) / dry grounds mass`.[^s1] For a room-temperature 14-hour run (TDS 4.0%, 1820 g draw-off, 250 g grounds), this works out to about 29.1% EY, notably higher than the roughly 20% EY of the same bean brewed hot drip.[^s1] Despite its lower temperature, cold brew's long steep extracts more of the bean's total soluble material than a quick hot brew does, and does so gently enough to avoid bitterness.[^s1]

## Water and filtering

The recipe started with filtered water at about 38 ppm TDS, then was changed to mineral-added water at about 60 ppm (via a mineral sachet), which produced a sweeter, more defined concentrate at 4.2% TDS while keeping acidity low; water softer than about 30 ppm produces a flat, hollow cup.[^s1]

Filtering matters too: the house spec draws the concentrate off through a stainless-steel screen followed by a single paper filter polish, which removes silt and leaves a glassy-clear cup, whereas filtering through nylon alone leaves more body but a cloudy result with sediment at the bottom of the keg.[^s1]

## Caffeine

Cold brew is widely assumed to be lower in caffeine because it is served cold, but that is a myth — it often ends up with a *higher* total caffeine content than hot drip coffee.[^s1] The reason is the brewing ratio and time, not the temperature: a large grounds-to-water ratio (about 1:8) combined with a 12-18 hour steep pulls a lot of caffeine into the concentrate, to the point that a 1:8 cold brew concentrate runs about 2.5x the caffeine of a standard hot drip mug before dilution.[^s1] What ends up in a finished glass still varies with how much a drinker dilutes the concentrate, but per gram of grounds used, cold brew is the more caffeinated method.[^s1] See [Caffeine in coffee](caffeine-in-coffee.md) for how this compares with other factors that drive caffeine content.

Because cold brew's caffeine lingers, staff guidance is not to sell a full-strength cold brew too late in the day — flagged on the menu cards as a caution for after-2pm orders.[^s1] See [Caffeine and health](caffeine-and-health.md) for how this bench figure for caffeine's half-life compares with the estimate used elsewhere in this wiki.[^s1]

## Serving, milk, and nitro

Concentrate is diluted to taste, typically 1:1 up to 1:3 with water or milk plus ice; the house default serving dilution is 1:2.[^s1] For milk service, 1:1 concentrate to whole milk over ice is the best seller, since the milk carries the body; oat milk works well as the default dairy alternative, while almond milk goes thin.[^s1]

Charging the same concentrate with nitrogen (N2) for a nitro tap gives a creamier mouthfeel and a perceived sweetness increase, even though TDS and caffeine content are unchanged (about 4.0% TDS either way) — the nitro effect is about texture and perception, not extraction chemistry.[^s1]

## Shelf life

Sealed concentrate keeps in the fridge for about 10-14 days before its acidity starts creeping up; once diluted into a ready-to-drink glass it lasts only 2-3 days before flavor drops off.[^s1]

## Batch scale-up

A scaled batch of 5 kg grounds to 40 kg water (still 1:8), steeped 18 hours at room temperature in a 19 C kitchen, produced a concentrate TDS of 4.0%, consistent with small-batch results, and yielded about 36 L of concentrate off the press.[^s1]

## House spec

The locked house cold brew spec: coarse grind (burr setting 9.5), 1:8 ratio, steeped 14-16 hours at room temperature or 18 hours in the fridge; a stainless-steel screen draw-off followed by a single paper filter polish; mineral water around 60 ppm; concentrate TDS target 4.0-4.2%; served diluted 1:2 by default.[^s1] The two talking points used at the bar are that cold brew is smoother and less acidic than hot coffee, and that — counterintuitively — it has more total caffeine than hot drip; both claims are true and both are used to sell it.[^s1]
