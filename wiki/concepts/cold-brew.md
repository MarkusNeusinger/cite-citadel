Cold brew is coffee steeped in cold or room-temperature water for 12–24 hours, then filtered; crucially, the water is never heated.[^s1][^s2] Because it never gets hot, it extracts acids and bitter compounds slowly and selectively rather than quickly, producing a cup that is smooth, round, sweet, low in acidity, and often chocolatey or nutty.[^s1][^s2] People who find hot coffee too sharp on the stomach often get along much better with cold brew.[^s2] It rewards patience more than equipment — a jar and a strainer are enough — which is part of why it has moved from a niche café offering to a common at-home brew.[^s2]

## Making Cold Brew

**Grind size**: Use a coarse, even [grind](../concepts/grinding.md) — similar to or coarser than a [French press](../concepts/french-press.md) grind; a fine grind makes the brew cloudy and over-extracts into bitterness.[^s1][^s2]

**Ratio**: A common approach is to brew a concentrate at roughly 1:8 (coffee to water) by weight, then dilute the finished concentrate to taste with water, milk, or a milk alternative.[^s1][^s2] If you prefer a ready-to-drink strength, brew nearer 1:15 from the start.[^s2] See [Brewing Ratios](../concepts/brewing-ratios.md) for how cold brew compares with other methods.

**Steeping**: Steep for 12–24 hours, then filter the grounds out.[^s1][^s2] Longer steeps give a fuller, sweeter cup up to a point, but past about 24 hours the brew can turn woody.[^s2] Steeping on the counter gives a faster, punchier result, while the fridge gives a cleaner one.[^s2]

**Filtering**: Pour the brew off the grounds through a fine mesh, then again through a paper filter or cloth if you want it crystal clear.[^s2]

## Storage

Stored as a concentrate in the fridge, cold brew keeps well for up to about two weeks, so a single batch can last most of a week.[^s1][^s2] See [Coffee Storage](../concepts/coffee-storage.md) for storing the roasted beans themselves.

## Cold Brew vs. Iced Coffee

Cold brew is often confused with iced coffee, but the two are different.[^s1][^s2] Iced coffee is ordinary hot-brewed coffee that has been chilled or poured over ice — or flash-brewed directly onto ice — so it keeps the bright, acidic character of a hot cup, whereas cold brew is its own process and tastes noticeably mellower.[^s1][^s2]

## Caffeine Content

The two raw sources disagree on how much caffeine cold brew delivers:

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/cold-brew.md says that because the concentrate is strong, its caffeine per serving can be high, depending entirely on how much you dilute it.[^s1] raw/cold-brew-guide.md says that because cold brew is made without heat, very little caffeine is drawn out of the grounds, so a glass carries only a small amount of caffeine — a relaxed, low-stimulant option later in the day.[^s2]
>
> Resolution: caffeine is highly water-soluble, and cold brew's long steep with a high coffee dose extracts substantial caffeine, so the concentrate is generally high in caffeine rather than low; the amount in a finished glass depends mainly on how much the concentrate is diluted.[^llm1]

See [Caffeine in Coffee](../concepts/caffeine-in-coffee.md), which likewise lists undiluted cold brew as very high in caffeine.

## Serving

Cold brew takes milk and sweeteners gracefully, and over ice it stays flavorful instead of turning watery the way melting ice dilutes a hot pour.[^s2] Its flavor — smooth, sweet, and easy to drink black — is the headline reason people reach for it.[^s2] Common ways to serve it:[^s2]

- Concentrate cut 1:1 with water or milk over ice.[^s2]
- A splash of cream and a little simple syrup for a smooth iced-latte feel (see [milk drinks](../concepts/milk-drinks.md)).[^s2]
- Concentrate stirred into milk and blended with ice for a frappé-style drink.[^s2]
