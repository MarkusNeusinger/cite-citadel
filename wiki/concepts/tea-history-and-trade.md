Tea originated in China, where its cultivation and consumption can be traced across several thousand years of recorded and semi-legendary history.[^s1] According to legend, the beverage was discovered by the Emperor Shennong in 2737 BCE, when leaves from a wild tea shrub are said to have drifted into a pot of water he was boiling.[^s1] Charmed by the aroma and the reviving effect of the resulting infusion, the emperor is reputed to have championed the drink.[^s1] The story is traditional rather than documented, but it captures the antiquity the Chinese have long ascribed to [tea](tea.md): in the hill country of southwest China, where the wild camellia grew, people are said to have drunk a brew that "cleared the head and steadied the hand" for a thousand years of legend before any of it was written down.[^s1][^s2]

In China the leaf was first a medicine, then a discipline, and only later a pleasure.[^s2] Early drinkers boiled it into a thick, bitter decoction, sometimes with salt or ginger; centuries afterward the powder method arrived, in which dried leaf was ground fine and whisked with hot water into a jade-coloured froth; and only centuries after that did the loose-leaf infusion familiar today become the common habit of scholars and farmers alike.[^s2]

From China the practice spread outward along trade and religious routes.[^s1] Tea travelled to Japan with Buddhist monks, who carried both the leaves and the disciplined ritual of preparing them, eventually giving rise to the celebrated Japanese tea ceremony.[^s1] The monks themselves had gone to China to study the sutras and found that the brew kept them awake through long nights of meditation; when they returned home they brought back both the seeds and the customs, planting the leaf in temple gardens as a sacred crop.[^s2] What had been a Chinese habit became, on Japanese soil, a slow, deliberate ritual of whisked powder and quiet rooms, of guest and host bound by a shared and wordless courtesy — the monks had meant only to stay awake, and ended up founding an entire aesthetic.[^s2]

For many centuries tea remained an essentially Asian beverage.[^s1] It moved along overland caravan routes and the coastal junk trade but did not yet move west in any quantity, and Europe knew nothing of it.[^s2] That changed in the 17th century, when European ships began rounding the Cape in earnest and the great chartered trading companies turned their eyes toward the spice ports and silk markets of the East.[^s1][^s2]

## The leaf comes west

The Portuguese were among the first Europeans to taste tea: their carracks, ranging out from footholds on the China coast, sent home curious reports of a bitter Eastern infusion the mandarins drank from tiny cups, said to be good for the digestion and the temper.[^s3] But it was the Dutch who first made a business of it — Dutch trading fleets began carrying small consignments of the dried leaf back to Amsterdam, where it sold at first as an exotic medicine to those who could afford novelty, before seeping outward into the rest of Europe from the Dutch warehouses, along the same shipping lanes that carried pepper, porcelain, and silk.[^s3]

Tea reached England later still, and there it went on to change the nation's daily life more completely than anywhere else on earth.[^s3] In the years around the middle of the 17th century the leaf was still a rarity in London — discussed in the new coffee houses, sold by a handful of apothecaries and merchants, and priced far beyond the reach of ordinary households.[^s3]

## Thornbury & Lin, of London and Canton

It was in this narrow, expensive moment that the merchant house of [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md) first brought tea to England, in 1657.[^s4] The firm was the joint creation of [Edmund Thornbury](../persons/edmund-thornbury.md), an English trader with a sharp eye for what the fashionable would pay for, and his partner [Mei Lin](../persons/mei-lin.md), a Canton merchant of formidable reach who knew the inland tea districts and the men who controlled them.[^s4] Thornbury kept the London end of the business — the warehouses, the buyers among the gentry, the negotiations with customs — while Mei Lin worked the Canton waterfront, arranging the chests, judging the grades, and seeing that what was promised in spring was actually loaded by autumn.[^s4] Where other traders of the day dealt at arm's length through middlemen, Thornbury and Lin shared the risk and the knowledge directly; the arrangement was unusual for its era and was said to be the source of the firm's fortune.[^s4]

From the very beginning the house traded coffee as well as tea: the two commodities travelled in the same holds, were insured under the same policies, and were sold from the same counting-house ledgers, since the ships that fetched tea from the China coast called along the way at the ports where coffee was gathered.[^s4] Indeed the early history of the two beverages in Europe runs along a single set of rails: the same routes carried both, the same trading houses financed both, and the same captains who knew the winds to Canton knew the winds to the coffee ports as well — see [coffee history and trade](coffee-history-and-trade.md) for that story from the coffee side.[^s4] In the firm's own ledgers the tea and coffee columns sat side by side, and a poor year in one was often steadied by a good year in the other.[^s4]

The house's own blog gives a different date for that first shipment; see [Thornbury and Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md) for the discrepancy.

## A habit becomes a nation

From those first costly chests, the English habit grew quickly: what began as a luxury of the wealthy spread, decade by decade, through every rank of society, until tea was no longer a fashion but a fixture — the drink of the laborer's break and the lady's drawing room alike.[^s5] The appetite reshaped the trade that fed it: demand once measured in pounds came to be measured in shiploads, whole fleets were ordered around the tea calendar, and the leaf became so woven into the public finances that the taxes on it stirred quarrels that echoed far beyond the tea table.[^s5]

It is worth pausing on the leaf itself: by this account, a cup of green tea carries about 50 mg of caffeine, very nearly the same as a cup of black tea, since the two come from the same plant, *Camellia sinensis*, and differ only in how the picked leaf is handled and dried.[^s5] [Caffeine in tea](caffeine-in-tea.md) has a different accounting, with black tea carrying substantially more caffeine per cup than green.

## The shape of what followed

By the close of the 17th century the pattern was set that the next two centuries would only deepen: the leaf had come west from China, taken root in Japan through the patience of the monks, been carried to Europe first by Portuguese curiosity and then by Dutch enterprise, and found in England a second home so devoted that the world would come to think of tea as half an English thing.[^s6] The trading houses thickened into great companies and the companies into instruments of state, and the simple act of drinking an infusion of dried leaves became entangled with politics, taxation, and the movement of silver across oceans.[^s6]
