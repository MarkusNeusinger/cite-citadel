Brewing extracts the soluble compounds of ground [coffee](coffee.md) into water.[^s1] The ratio of coffee to water is one of the most important variables in determining strength and balance; a typical brew ratio is about 1:15 to 1:18 by weight, meaning roughly one gram of ground coffee for every 15 to 18 grams of water.[^s1] A higher proportion of coffee yields a stronger, more concentrated cup, while a higher proportion of water yields a lighter, more diluted one.[^s1]

Grind size, water temperature, and contact time also shape the result: finer grinds and longer contact times increase extraction, while coarser grinds and shorter contact times reduce it.[^s1] Different brewing devices, from drip machines and pour-over cones to French presses and espresso machines, are matched to particular grind sizes and ratios.[^s1] [Espresso](espresso.md) sits at the concentrated extreme: a typical double shot uses about eighteen grams of coffee to draw about thirty-six grams of liquid — roughly 1:2 by weight — in twenty-five to thirty seconds, far more concentrated and quick than drip or pour-over.[^s3]

To compare recipes objectively rather than by taste alone, enthusiasts measure a brew's strength as its total dissolved solids ([TDS](../abbreviations/tds-total-dissolved-solids.md)) and calculate the extraction yield ([EY](../abbreviations/ey-extraction-yield.md)) for a given grind and ratio.[^s1][^llm1]

[Cold brew](cold-brew.md) inverts the usual ratio: rather than brewing close to serving strength, it steeps at a far more concentrated ratio of about 1:8 grounds to water and dilutes the result down to taste afterward, typically 1:1 up to 1:3 with water or milk.[^s2]
