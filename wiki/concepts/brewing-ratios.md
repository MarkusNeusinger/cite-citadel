The brewing ratio is the mass of coffee relative to the mass of water used. A lower second number produces a stronger cup; a higher ratio produces a weaker one.[^s1]

## Standard Ratios by Method

Different brewing methods require different ratios to achieve optimal extraction:[^s1]

| Method        | Ratio    | Example            |
| ------------- | -------- | ------------------ |
| Espresso      | ~1:2     | 18 g → 36 g out    |
| Moka pot      | ~1:7     | strong, intense    |
| [Pour-over](../concepts/pour-over.md)     | ~1:16    | 25 g → 400 g       |
| [French press](../concepts/french-press.md)  | ~1:15    | 30 g → 450 g       |
| [Cold brew](../concepts/cold-brew.md)     | ~1:8 (concentrate) | dilute to taste |
| [AeroPress](../concepts/aeropress.md)     | ~1:13–1:17 | 15 g → 200–250 ml[^s3] |

### Espresso Ratio Terminology

Espresso shots are named by their brew ratio (yield ÷ dose):[^s2]

- **Ristretto** — "restricted", ~1:1 to 1:1.5. Shorter, sweeter, more syrupy.
- **Normale** — the standard ~1:2. Balanced extraction and flavor.
- **Lungo** — "long", ~1:3 or more. Larger, weaker, more bitter as extraction runs on.

## General Guidelines

The "golden ratio" is approximately 1:16–1:18, a common all-purpose filter ratio that works well for most drip and pour-over methods.[^s1] Always weigh your coffee and water with a scale rather than using volume scoops, which are unreliable.[^s1]

## Troubleshooting

If your coffee tastes weak or sour, use more coffee or grind finer.[^s1] If it tastes bitter, use less coffee or grind coarser.[^s1]
