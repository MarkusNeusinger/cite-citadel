Coffee was first cultivated in the **Ethiopian highlands**, where the [Arabica](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md) species (*Coffea arabica*) originates.[^s1][^s2] Its journey from a wild plant in East Africa to one of the world's most widely consumed beverages and most heavily [traded commodities](../concepts/coffee-trade.md) spans more than a thousand years and touches nearly every continent.[^s2]

## Origins in the Ethiopian highlands

*Coffea arabica* originated in the highlands of southwestern Ethiopia, where the plant still grows wild in the forest understory today.[^s2] Ethiopia is widely regarded as the genetic birthplace of Arabica coffee and retains an enormous diversity of indigenous coffee varieties.[^s2] Popular legend credits a goatherd named **Kaldi** with noticing the stimulating effect of the coffee cherry on his goats, though this story is folklore rather than documented history.[^s2] The practices of consuming and eventually roasting and brewing coffee took root in this region before spreading outward.[^s2]

## Cultivation and trade through Yemen and the Arab world

From Ethiopia, coffee spread through Yemen and the broader Arab world, where coffee drinking became embedded in social and cultural practice.[^s1] Organized cultivation is firmly associated with **Yemen**: by the 15th century, coffee was grown on terraced farms in the Yemeni highlands and consumed across the Arab world, including in Sufi religious communities that valued it for keeping worshippers awake during devotions.[^s2] The Red Sea port of **Mocha** (Mokha) became so central to the coffee trade that its name became a generic term for coffee itself.[^s2] For a long period the Arab world guarded its monopoly on cultivation, and beans were often treated to prevent germination before export.[^s2] Coffeehouses spread across cities such as Mecca, Cairo, and later Istanbul, becoming important centers of social and intellectual life.[^s2]

## Naming and etymology

The word "coffee" entered European languages via Turkish *kahve*, which itself derives from Arabic *qahwa*.[^s1] This linguistic journey mirrors the historical trade route from Arab lands through the Ottoman Empire to Europe.[^s1]

## Spread to Europe

Coffee reached Europe in the 17th century, where it became popular first among elites and eventually the broader population.[^s1] It arrived through trade with the Ottoman Empire and Venetian merchants, and by the 17th century coffeehouses had opened in cities including Venice, London, Paris, Vienna, and Amsterdam — establishments that became hubs of commerce, news, and conversation.[^s2]

## Colonial cultivation

European demand soon outstripped the supply from Yemen, and colonial powers sought to cultivate coffee in their own territories.[^s2] The Dutch established plantations in their colonies, notably on the island of Java (in present-day Indonesia), which is why "java" became another nickname for coffee.[^s2] The French, Dutch, and others introduced coffee to the Caribbean and to Central and South America in the 18th century.[^s2] Over time **Brazil** emerged as a major producer and the largest coffee-growing nation in the world.[^s2] This colonial expansion shifted the center of production from the Arabian Peninsula to the Americas and parts of Asia, often relying on plantation systems and, in many regions, on enslaved or coerced labor.[^s2] In this way coffee became one of the most traded commodities in the world.[^s1]

## Modern global production

Today, coffee is grown across the equatorial "Bean Belt" (tropical regions between approximately 23°N and 23°S latitude). Brazil is the world's largest producer, and Vietnam is the second largest.[^s1] Other major producers include [Colombia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md), [Ethiopia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md), [Kenya](../concepts/coffee-origins.md), and many others. Coffee remains one of the most popular caffeinated drinks on the planet, and its modern life as a globally [traded commodity](../concepts/coffee-trade.md) is covered separately.[^s1]
