The AeroPress is a manual coffee brewer, invented by Alan Adler and introduced in 2005, that produces a single cup or a small concentrate by combining immersion steeping with gentle pressure.[^s1] It is built around two nested cylinders — a brewing chamber and a plunger fitted with a rubber seal.[^s1] Ground coffee and hot water steep together inside the chamber, and then the user presses the plunger down by hand, forcing the brewed coffee through a thin paper micro-filter into the cup or carafe below.[^s1] This makes it a hybrid method, drawing on both the full immersion of a [French press](../concepts/french-press.md) and the filtered, pressurized push of an [espresso](../concepts/espresso.md)-style device, though the pressure it generates is very low compared with a true espresso machine.[^s1]

## How it works

- **Immersion phase**: the coffee grounds sit submerged in hot water for a short time, extracting flavor evenly as in a steeped brew.[^s1]
- **Pressure phase**: pressing the plunger pushes the water through the bed of grounds and the filter, speeding up the final separation and adding a small amount of pressure-driven extraction.[^s1]
- **Filtration**: a circular paper micro-filter sits in a detachable cap that screws or clips onto the end of the chamber, and the fine paper captures most of the suspended oils and fine sediment, producing a notably clean cup with little grit.[^s1]

The paper filter is the main reason an AeroPress cup tastes clean and bright rather than heavy.[^s1] Because it traps oils and micro-fines, the resulting coffee has low body relative to a [French press](../concepts/french-press.md) and generally low bitterness, with a clarity that highlights the bean's flavors.[^s1] Some people prefer a reusable metal filter, which lets more oils through and yields a heavier, more textured cup, but the standard kit ships with paper filters.[^s1]

## Standard and inverted methods

There are two widely used orientations:[^s1]

- **Standard (upright) method**: the chamber sits cap-down directly on top of the mug or carafe, and coffee and water are added to the chamber to steep there.[^s1] The advantage is simplicity and stability; a minor drawback is that some dripping can occur through the filter before you press.[^s1]
- **Inverted method**: the brewer is assembled upside down, with the plunger at the bottom and the open chamber facing up, so coffee steeps without any liquid draining through the filter.[^s1] After steeping, the cap and filter are attached, and the whole device is carefully flipped onto the mug and pressed.[^s1] This gives more control over total steep time and avoids early dripping, at the cost of a slightly trickier, more spill-prone flip.[^s1]

Both methods produce similar results; the choice is largely a matter of personal preference and how much control over steep time you want.[^s1]

## A typical recipe

A common, reliable starting point:[^s1]

- **Coffee**: about 15 g of medium-fine [ground coffee](../concepts/grinding.md) — somewhat finer than drip, coarser than espresso.[^s1]
- **Water**: about 200–250 ml of hot water, typically in the range of roughly 80–90 °C (slightly off the boil).[^s1]
- **Steep**: combine the grounds and water, give a brief stir, and let steep for about 1–2 minutes.[^s1]
- **Press**: insert the plunger and press down slowly and steadily, taking roughly 20–30 seconds, until you hear a hiss as air reaches the puck.[^s1]

The resulting brew can be drunk as is or, if brewed as a stronger concentrate, diluted with hot water to taste in an [Americano](../concepts/espresso.md)-like style.[^s1] Ratios, grind size, water temperature, and steep time can all be adjusted, and the device responds predictably to changes, which makes [dialing it in](../concepts/brewing-ratios.md) straightforward.[^s1]

## Why people like it

- **Very forgiving**: small errors in grind, dose, or timing rarely ruin the cup, making it friendly for beginners.[^s1]
- **Highly portable and durable**: it is lightweight, made of sturdy plastic with no glass to break, and quick to use, which makes it popular for travel, camping, and the office.[^s1]
- **Fast and easy to clean**: after pressing, unscrew the cap and push the plunger the rest of the way to eject the spent puck and filter in one motion, and a quick rinse leaves it ready for the next brew.[^s1]
- **Clean, low-bitterness cup**: the paper micro-filter removes oils and sediment, giving a smooth, clear result with bitterness kept low.[^s1]

## Practical tips

- Rinsing the paper filter with a little hot water before brewing can reduce any papery taste and preheat the cup.[^s1]
- Finer grinds and longer steeps increase strength and extraction, while coarser grinds and shorter steeps lighten the cup.[^s1]
- Because the device is so adjustable, it rewards experimentation, and an enthusiast community shares many recipes built around these same basic principles.[^s1]
