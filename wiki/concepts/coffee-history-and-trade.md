Coffee was first cultivated in the highlands of Ethiopia, where *Coffea arabica* grows wild and where the practice of preparing the seeds as a drink is believed to have originated.[^s1] From this East African cradle, coffee crossed the Red Sea and took root in Yemen, where it was systematically cultivated and traded; the Yemeni port of Mocha became so closely associated with the commodity that its name endures as a coffee term to this day.[^s1]

Through Yemen and the wider Arab world, coffee spread as both a commercial good and a social institution, and coffeehouses became centers of conversation, commerce, and learning across the region long before the drink was known in Europe.[^s1] By the 17th century, coffee had reached the European continent, where it was initially regarded as an exotic novelty before rapidly becoming a fixture of urban life; European coffeehouses, like their predecessors in the Arab world, grew into gathering places for merchants, writers, and political thinkers.[^s1]

As demand grew, colonial powers carried coffee cultivation to tropical regions across the globe, establishing plantations throughout Asia, Africa, and the Americas, which fundamentally reshaped the geography of production.[^s1] Today the largest producer of coffee in the world is Brazil, whose vast plantations supply an enormous share of the global market; Vietnam ranks second, having risen to prominence largely on the strength of [Robusta](arabica-and-robusta.md) cultivation.[^s1] Other significant producers include Colombia, Indonesia, Ethiopia, and several nations of Central America.[^s1]

In 17th-century Europe, coffee's spread was closely intertwined with tea's: the same ships that fetched tea from the China coast typically called at coffee-gathering ports along the way, so merchant houses often carried both commodities in the same holds, insured under the same policies and tracked in the same ledgers.[^s2] The London-and-Canton firm [Thornbury and Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md) is one such example — it traded coffee alongside tea from its founding in 1657, and a poor year in one commodity was often steadied by a good year in the other.[^s2] See [tea history and trade](tea-history-and-trade.md) for that story from the tea side.
