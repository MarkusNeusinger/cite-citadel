[Coffee](coffee.md) was first cultivated in the highlands of Ethiopia, where *Coffea arabica* grows wild and where the practice of preparing the seeds as a drink is believed to have originated.[^s1] From this East African cradle, coffee crossed the Red Sea and took root in Yemen, where it was systematically cultivated and traded; the Yemeni port of Mocha became so closely associated with the commodity that its name endures as a coffee term to this day.[^s1]

Through Yemen and the wider Arab world, coffee spread as both a commercial good and a social institution, and coffeehouses became centers of conversation, commerce, and learning across the region long before the drink was known in Europe.[^s1] By the 17th century coffee had reached Europe, where it was initially regarded as an exotic novelty before rapidly becoming a fixture of urban life; European coffeehouses, like their predecessors in the Arab world, grew into gathering places for merchants, writers, and political thinkers.[^s1]

As demand grew, colonial powers carried coffee cultivation to tropical regions across the globe, establishing plantations throughout Asia, Africa, and the Americas, which reshaped the geography of production.[^s1] Today the largest producer of coffee in the world is Brazil, whose vast plantations supply an enormous share of the global market; Vietnam ranks second, having risen to prominence largely on the strength of [Robusta](arabica-and-robusta.md) cultivation.[^s1] Other significant producers include Colombia, Indonesia, Ethiopia, and several nations of Central America.[^s1]

## A trade shared with tea

Coffee's arrival in Europe ran on rails shared with [tea](tea.md): the same shipping routes that carried tea from the China coast often called at the ports where coffee was gathered, the same trading houses financed both commodities, and the two drinks arrived in Europe almost as twins.[^s2] The merchant house [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md), for instance, is said to have traded coffee and tea together in the same holds from the start, a poor season in one steadied by a good one in the other.[^s2] See [Tea History](tea-history.md) for tea's own path from China to England.[^s2]
