Espresso is a brewing method, not a roast or bean type.[^s3] It forces hot water through a bed of finely ground coffee under approximately 9 bar of pressure, producing a concentrated shot in a short time.[^s1] Any coffee can be pulled as espresso, though the flavor depends heavily on the bean, roast level, and grind.

## Espresso Specifications

By traditional Italian standard, a **single shot** uses about 7 grams of coffee and is pulled in approximately 25 seconds, yielding roughly 25–30 ml of liquid.[^s1] A **double shot** uses about 14 grams of coffee and produces approximately 50–60 ml.[^s1]

The concentrated extraction under pressure produces **crema**, a reddish-brown foam layer on the surface caused by emulsified oils and carbon dioxide.[^s3] [Robusta](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md) beans produce more crema than [Arabica](../concepts/arabica-and-robusta.md), which is why they are traditionally used in Italian espresso blends for this effect.[^s1][^s3]

### Grind and Basic Technique

Espresso requires a **fine grind**, finer than table salt, which is the main lever for dialing in the shot.[^s3] A **single shot** has about 63 mg of caffeine.[^s3]

> [!CONTRADICTION]
> raw/espresso.md says a single shot has about 63 mg of caffeine [^s3]; raw/caffeine.md says a single espresso (~30 ml) has ~75 mg [^caffeine]. The actual value may vary by dose and extraction yield.

## Espresso-Based Drinks

Most café espresso drinks are variations of espresso plus milk or water in different proportions:[^s1]

- **Cappuccino**: typically equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and milk foam
- **Latte**: espresso with more steamed milk and less foam
- **Flat white**: espresso with velvety microfoam (thin milk layer)
- **Americano**: espresso diluted with hot water
- **Macchiato**: espresso "marked" with a small amount of milk foam

## Roasting for Espresso

[Espresso is often roasted to a medium-dark level](../concepts/roasting.md) to develop body and crema, as the bolder, less acidic profile works well with milk.[^s1]

## Extraction and Tuning

### The Main Variables

Modern specialty espresso practice doses **18–20 g for a double** in a standard basket, a departure from the older 7 g/14 g café standard.[^s2] The four main variables are **dose** (grams in), **yield** (grams of liquid out), **grind**, and **time**.[^s2] To hit a target brew time, hold the dose and ratio fixed and adjust **grind** as the primary tuning lever.[^s2] A typical recipe is 18 g in → 36 g out (a 1:2 ratio) in 25–30 seconds.[^s2]

If the shot runs too fast (under 20 seconds, "gushing"), it is **under-extracted** — the cup tastes sour, thin, and salty.[^s2] Grind finer to slow the flow.[^s2] If it runs too slow (over 35 seconds, "dripping"), it is **over-extracted** — the cup tastes bitter, harsh, and ashy.[^s2] Grind coarser to speed it up.[^s2]

### Extraction Yield and Strength

**Extraction yield (EY)** is the percentage of dry coffee that dissolves into the cup. The widely-cited sweet spot is roughly **18–22% EY**; below that tastes sour and under-extracted, above that tastes bitter and over-extracted.[^s2] **TDS** (total dissolved solids, measured with a refractometer) describes strength — espresso runs very high, around 8–12% TDS, versus ~1.2–1.5% for [filter coffee](../concepts/brewing-ratios.md). Strength (TDS) and extraction (EY) are different axes: you can have high strength with low extraction (weak but syrupy) or low strength with high extraction (thin but fully extracted).[^s2]

### Puck Preparation

Even extraction requires an even puck. **Channeling** — water boring a fast path through a weak spot — is the main enemy and causes a simultaneously sour-and-bitter shot.[^s2] Two key techniques ensure consistency:[^s2]

- **Distribution**: break up clumps and level the grounds. **WDT** (Weiss Distribution Technique — stirring the grounds with thin needles before tamping) reduces clumping from the grinder.
- **Tamping**: compress level with steady pressure; consistency matters more than force.

### Pre-Infusion and Pressure Profiling

**Pre-infusion** wets the puck at low pressure before ramping to full ~9 bar, which saturates it evenly and reduces channeling.[^s2] Machines with **pressure profiling** let you shape the pressure curve over the shot — for example, a gentle ramp, a peak, then a decline — to soften harshness or boost sweetness.[^s2]

### Temperature and Temperature Surfing

Brew temperature sits around 90–96 °C.[^s2] Higher temperatures extract more (more body and bitterness); lower temperatures favor acidity.[^s2] On single-boiler machines, "temperature surfing" times the shot to the right point in the heating cycle to hit the desired temperature. Lighter roasts usually want hotter water; darker roasts a little cooler.[^s2]
