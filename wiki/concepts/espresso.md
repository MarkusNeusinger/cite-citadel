Espresso is a [brewing](brewing-ratios.md) method made by forcing hot water through a compact bed of very finely ground [coffee](coffee.md) at roughly nine bars of pressure.[^s1] The barista tamps the grounds flat and level, locks the portafilter into the machine's group head with a quarter turn, and starts the pump; the shot pulls for roughly twenty-five to thirty seconds.[^s1] The window is narrow: a shot pulled too fast comes out thin and sour, while one pulled too slow turns bitter and scorched.[^s1]

A typical double shot starts with about eighteen grams of ground coffee and yields about thirty-six grams of liquid in the cup — roughly a 1:2 ratio by weight, far more concentrated than most [drip brewing ratios](brewing-ratios.md).[^s1]

## Crema

As a shot pulls, a fragile reddish-brown foam of emulsified oils and dissolved gas called crema settles across the top of the liquid; it is the visible signature of the extraction pressure, and an experienced barista can read it to judge whether the grind and tamp were correct.[^s1]

## Caffeine

A single shot of espresso, about one ounce, carries roughly 63 mg of [caffeine](caffeine-in-coffee.md) — less than a standard cup of drip coffee, even though espresso is far more concentrated per unit of volume.[^s1]

## Bar culture

Drinking espresso is traditionally a quick transaction at a standing bar: order, drink it in a few swallows, set the cup down, pay, and leave, all of which can be over in about ninety seconds.[^s1] An espresso bar at its best works as a social leveling place — people of different professions and status stand at the same counter, drink the same drink at the same price, with no table service separating them into different classes.[^s1] [Caffe Aurora](../organizations/caffe-aurora.md) was built around this idea that the bar itself, not any single roast, is the real product.[^s1]
