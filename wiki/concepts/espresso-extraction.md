**Espresso extraction** is the process of dissolving coffee solids out of a fine, compacted puck under roughly 9 bar of pressure, and "dialing in" is the practice of tuning the brew variables to hit a target flavor.[^s1] This page builds on the [espresso](./espresso.md) basics and focuses on how the shot extracts and how you tune it.

## The variables, and which to move first

The main levers are dose (grams of dry coffee in), yield (grams of liquid espresso out), grind, and time.[^s1] Modern specialty practice doses 18–20 g for a double in a standard basket, rather than the older 7 g single / 14 g double café standard.[^s1] The usual method is to hold the dose and ratio fixed and use grind as the primary lever to hit the target time.[^s1]

A typical recipe is 18 g in to 36 g out — a 1:2 ratio — in 25–30 seconds.[^s1] A shot that runs too fast (gushing, under 20 seconds) is under-extracted and tastes sour, thin, and salty, so you grind finer.[^s1] A shot that runs too slow (dripping, over 35 seconds) is over-extracted and tastes bitter, harsh, and ashy, so you grind coarser.[^s1] For how particle size maps to extraction speed across methods, see [grind size](./grind-size.md).

## Brew ratio terminology

The brew ratio (yield divided by dose) has named ranges.[^s1] A **ristretto** ("restricted") is about 1:1 to 1:1.5 — shorter, sweeter, and more syrupy.[^s1] A **normale** is the standard ~1:2.[^s1] A **lungo** ("long") is about 1:3 or more — larger, weaker, and more bitter as extraction runs on.[^s1] See also [coffee brewing ratios](./coffee-brewing-ratios.md).

## Extraction yield and TDS

**Extraction yield (EY)** is the percentage of the dry coffee that dissolves into the cup, and the widely-cited sweet spot is roughly 18–22% EY; below that the shot tastes sour and under-extracted, above it tastes bitter and over-extracted.[^s1] **TDS** (total dissolved solids), measured with a refractometer, describes *strength* rather than extraction: espresso runs very high at around 8–12% TDS, versus about 1.2–1.5% for filter coffee.[^s1] Strength (TDS) and extraction (EY) are different axes.[^s1]

## Puck preparation

Even extraction requires an even puck.[^s1] The main enemy is **channeling** — water boring a fast path through a weak spot — which produces a shot that is simultaneously sour and bitter.[^s1] Distribution breaks up clumps and levels the grounds, and the Weiss Distribution Technique (WDT) — stirring the grounds with thin needles — reduces clumping from the grinder.[^s1] Tamping compresses the bed level with steady pressure, where consistency matters more than force.[^s1]

## Pre-infusion and pressure profiling

**Pre-infusion** wets the puck at low pressure before ramping to the full ~9 bar, which saturates it evenly and reduces channeling.[^s1] Machines with **pressure profiling** let you shape the pressure across the shot — for example a gentle ramp, a peak, then a decline — to soften harshness or boost sweetness.[^s1]

## Temperature

Brew temperature sits around 90–96 °C.[^s1] Higher temperatures extract more, adding body and bitterness, while lower temperatures favor acidity.[^s1] On single-boiler machines, "temperature surfing" times the shot to the right point in the heating cycle.[^s1] Lighter [roasts](./roast-levels.md) usually want hotter water, while darker roasts want it a little cooler.[^s1]
