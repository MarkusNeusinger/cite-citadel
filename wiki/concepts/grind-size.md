**Grind size** is the primary variable controlling how fast water extracts coffee from grounds.[^s1] By controlling grind size, you control extraction speed and flavor: match the grind to your brewing method's contact time, and you've won half the battle.[^s1]

## Why Grind Size Matters

The fundamental principle: the faster the method (shorter contact time), the finer you grind; the slower the method (longer contact time), the coarser you grind.[^s1] This is because finer particles expose more surface area, extracting more quickly; coarser particles extract more slowly.

## Grind Size by Brewing Method

Different brewing methods require different grind sizes:

- **Espresso** — fine (powdery, finer than table salt).[^s1] Uses high pressure and short contact time.
- **Moka pot** — fine-to-medium.[^s1]
- **Pour-over (V60, Chemex, Kalita)** — medium, like table salt.[^s1] The filter restricts flow, so a medium grind balances extraction speed.
- **Drip machine** — medium.[^s1]
- **French press** — coarse, like breadcrumbs.[^s1] Long steep requires coarse grind to avoid over-extraction and sludge.
- **Cold brew** — coarse to extra-coarse.[^s1] Steeps for many hours, so coarse grounds prevent over-extraction and muddy flavors.

## Consistency Beats Absolute Size

A **consistent grind is more important than the absolute size**.[^s1] A burr grinder produces uniform particles that extract evenly, while a blade grinder produces a chaotic mix of dust and boulders that extract unevenly, leading to muddy, harsh coffee.[^s1] Invest in a burr grinder for better-tasting results.

## Dialing In Flavor

Use grind adjustment as your primary troubleshooting lever:[^s1]

- **Coffee tastes too bitter** → grind coarser (reduces extraction)
- **Coffee tastes too sour or weak** → grind finer (increases extraction)
