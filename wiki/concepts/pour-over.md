**Pour-over** is a manual filter brewing method where hot water is poured over a bed of medium-ground coffee sitting in a paper filter, with gravity pulling the brew through into a cup or carafe.[^s1] The result is a clean, bright, tea-like body.[^s1]

## Common Drippers

Several popular dripper designs dominate pour-over brewing:

- **Hario V60** — cone shape with a single large hole, enabling fast flow and consistent results, very popular worldwide.[^s1]
- **Chemex** — uses thick bonded paper filters, producing an exceptionally clean cup with minimal oils and sediment, though brewing is slower.[^s1]
- **Kalita Wave** — flat-bottom design with three small holes, offering more forgiving and consistent extractions than cone designs.[^s1]

## Brewing Parameters

### Grind Size

Use a **medium grind**, roughly like table salt or coarse sand.[^s1] A grind that is too fine leads to clogging and bitter, over-extracted coffee; too coarse runs through weak and sour.[^s1]

### Brewing Ratio

The typical [brewing ratio](./coffee-brewing-ratios.md) is approximately **1:16** — for example, 25 g of coffee to 400 g of water.[^s1]

### Water Temperature

Brew with water heated to approximately **92–96 °C**, just off the boil.[^s1]

### Brewing Time and Bloom

A typical pour-over finishes in approximately **2.5–3.5 minutes**, including a **30–45 second bloom** phase.[^s1] The bloom involves wetting the grounds first and allowing them to de-gas before continuing the pour.[^s1]
