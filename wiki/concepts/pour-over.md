Pour-over is a manual filter brewing method where hot water is poured over a bed of medium-ground coffee sitting in a paper or metal filter, and gravity pulls the brew through into a cup or carafe.[^s1] The resulting coffee has a clean, bright, tea-like body.[^s1]

## Common Dripper Types

Several dripper designs dominate pour-over brewing, each with distinct characteristics:[^s1]

**Hario V60** — A cone-shaped dripper with a single large hole that allows fast flow and is very popular among enthusiasts.[^s1]

**Chemex** — A glass brewing vessel that uses thick bonded paper filters, producing an exceptionally clean cup with a slower brew time than other drippers.[^s1]

**Kalita Wave** — A flat-bottomed dripper with three small holes, designed to be more forgiving of technique and grind variation.[^s1]

## Brewing Parameters

**Grind size**: Use a medium grind, roughly like table salt or coarse sand.[^s1] Too fine a grind will clog the filter and over-extract, creating a bitter cup; too coarse a grind will run through too quickly, under-extracting and producing a weak, sour cup.[^s1]

**Ratio**: Brew at approximately 1:16 (coffee to water) — for example, 25 g of coffee to 400 g of water.[^s1]

**Water temperature**: Pour water that is just off the boil, approximately 92–96 °C.[^s1]

**Brewing time and bloom**: A pour-over usually finishes in about 2.5–3.5 minutes total.[^s1] Start with a 30–45 second "bloom" where you wet the grounds first and let them de-gas before continuing to pour.[^s1]
