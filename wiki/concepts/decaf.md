Decaffeinated ("decaf") coffee is coffee from which most of the caffeine has been removed, while trying to preserve as much of the coffee's flavor and aroma as possible.[^s3] In every commercial method the caffeine is taken out of the **green (unroasted) beans**, before [roasting](../concepts/roasting.md) takes place — both because caffeine is far easier to extract from the raw bean and because removing it later would interfere with the flavor development that happens during roasting.[^s3]

All mainstream methods share the same goal: to remove roughly **97% or more** of the caffeine, using one of three primary approaches.[^s1][^s3] The removal is never complete: a typical cup of decaf still retains a small residual amount of caffeine, usually about **2–5 mg per cup**, compared with the roughly **80–100 mg** found in a typical cup of [regular brewed coffee](../concepts/caffeine-in-coffee.md).[^s1][^s2][^s3] Decaf retains many of the other compounds found in regular coffee, including [antioxidants](../concepts/coffee-and-health.md), which makes it a useful option for people who want to limit their caffeine intake.[^s2]

## How decaffeination works

Every process follows the same broad principle. The green beans are first moistened or soaked so that they swell and the caffeine becomes mobile, and then a **solvent** — which may be water itself, carbon dioxide, or an organic solvent — is used to draw the caffeine out.[^s3] The beans are then dried back to their original moisture level and shipped to roasters.[^s3] The main differences between methods lie in which solvent is used and how selectively it targets caffeine versus the other flavor compounds.[^s3]

## Swiss Water Process

The Swiss Water Process is a **solvent-free** method that uses only water and an **activated carbon filter**.[^s3] A first batch of green beans is soaked in hot water, which dissolves both caffeine and flavor compounds, creating a caffeine-rich liquid.[^s3] This liquid is passed through an activated carbon filter sized to trap the larger caffeine molecules while letting the dissolved flavor compounds pass through, producing a flavor-saturated, caffeine-free liquid often called "green coffee extract."[^s3]

Fresh batches of green beans are then soaked in this extract. Because the liquid is already saturated with flavor compounds but contains no caffeine, only the caffeine migrates out of the beans into the water, leaving most of the flavor behind in the bean.[^s3] This makes the Swiss Water Process popular with people who want a chemical-free decaf, and it is commonly certified organic.[^s3]

## Carbon dioxide (CO₂) process

The CO₂ process uses **supercritical carbon dioxide** as the solvent.[^s3] Carbon dioxide is held at high pressure and temperature so that it behaves as a "supercritical fluid," with properties between a liquid and a gas.[^s3] In this state it is highly selective for caffeine, binding to caffeine molecules while largely leaving the compounds responsible for flavor and aroma untouched.[^s3]

The supercritical CO₂ is circulated through moistened green beans, picks up the caffeine, and is then moved to a separate chamber where the caffeine is removed from the CO₂ (often using water or activated carbon), allowing the CO₂ to be recycled.[^s3] Because it is so selective and uses a substance that is naturally present in air and food and leaves no harmful residue, the CO₂ method is well regarded for flavor preservation, though its equipment is expensive, so it is often used for large commercial volumes.[^s3]

## Solvent-based processes

Solvent (or "direct"/"indirect") methods use an organic solvent to dissolve the caffeine.[^s3] In a typical solvent process, the beans are steamed or soaked to open their pores, rinsed repeatedly with the solvent to strip out the caffeine, and then steamed again to drive off residual solvent before drying.[^s3] Two solvents dominate:

- **Ethyl acetate** — a compound that occurs naturally in fruits such as ripening bananas and in sugarcane.[^s3] Coffee decaffeinated with ethyl acetate, especially when the solvent is derived from sugarcane, is frequently marketed as "natural" decaf or "sugarcane process" decaf, and the method is common in coffee-growing countries such as [Colombia](../concepts/coffee-origins.md).[^s3]
- **Methylene chloride** (also called dichloromethane) — an organic solvent that is highly effective and selective for caffeine, helping preserve flavor.[^s3] It is widely used and considered safe in finished coffee because residual levels are extremely low and the solvent largely evaporates during processing and roasting, though its use has drawn some consumer scrutiny.[^s3]

## Flavor trade-offs

No decaffeination method is perfectly neutral.[^s3] Because caffeine is removed along with some flavor compounds, and because the soaking and drying steps stress the beans, decaffeinated coffee can taste slightly flatter, less aromatic, or more muted than its caffeinated counterpart.[^s3] The methods that are more selective for caffeine — particularly the CO₂ and Swiss Water processes — tend to preserve more of the original character than older solvent processes.[^s3] Modern processing and high-quality green beans have narrowed this gap considerably, and well-made decaf can be very close in flavor to regular coffee.[^s3]
