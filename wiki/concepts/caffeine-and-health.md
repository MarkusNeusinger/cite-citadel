Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors in the brain.[^s1] Adenosine is the molecule that builds up over the course of the day and produces "sleep pressure," the drowsy feeling that grows the longer a person has been awake.[^s1] Caffeine's shape is similar enough to adenosine that it occupies the same receptors without activating them, which stops the brain's "time to wind down" signal and produces alertness — caffeine does not add energy, it masks tiredness.[^s1] Because caffeine only blocks the receptors and does not remove the adenosine molecule itself, the backed-up adenosine reaches the receptors all at once once the caffeine wears off, which is why some people feel a sudden slump later in the day.[^s1]

## Half-life and timing

Caffeine's half-life is about 5 hours: roughly five hours after a cup, half of the caffeine consumed is still in the bloodstream, and half of that remains five hours after that, and so on.[^s1] As a result, a strong afternoon coffee can still leave a noticeable amount of caffeine active in the body at bedtime, even if the drinker no longer feels wired.[^s1] A good rule of thumb is to have the last full-strength coffee at least six to eight hours before sleep, pushed earlier for slow metabolizers, or replaced with decaf in the evening.[^s1]

The half-life varies meaningfully between individuals: genetics, liver enzyme activity, smoking, certain medications, and pregnancy can all stretch or shorten it, so "about 5 hours" is a solid average for a healthy adult but not a fixed number — someone who finds that coffee keeps them up at night may simply be a slow metabolizer.[^s1]

> This wiki's coffee-and-caffeine FAQ puts caffeine's half-life at about 5 hours[^s1], but a coffee shop's internal [cold brew](cold-brew.md) bench notes, used for staff menu-card timing guidance, put it at about 3 hours[^s2].
>
> Resolution: about 5 hours is the better-supported figure for a typical healthy adult; the 3-hour bench number looks like a simplified rule of thumb for quick service-timing decisions rather than a carefully sourced estimate.[^llm1]

## Dose, hidden sources, and decaf

A standard 8 oz cup of drip coffee contains about 95 mg of caffeine, the baseline figure used for comparison.[^s1] In practice a "cup" is often bigger than 8 oz, and a large café coffee can easily contain two or three times that amount.[^s1] Beyond [brewing method, grind size, dose, water temperature, and contact time](brewing-ratios.md), the bean species is one of the biggest factors driving caffeine content, since [Robusta carries roughly twice the caffeine of Arabica](arabica-and-robusta.md).[^s1]

Caffeine also hides in foods and drinks beyond coffee: chocolate, [many teas](caffeine-in-tea.md), some sodas, energy drinks, and certain pain relievers and supplements all contain it, and count toward a daily limit just as coffee does.[^s1] Decaf coffee is not entirely caffeine-free — "decaffeinated" does not mean zero caffeine, and decaf retains a small trace of the regular amount, though it keeps most of the flavor while removing the large majority of the caffeine, making it a reasonable substitute later in the day or for anyone cutting back.[^s1]

## Safe limits and pregnancy

For most healthy adults, a few cups of coffee spread across the day is generally considered fine; problems tend to start when caffeine piles up quickly, with jitteriness, a racing heart, anxiety, and trouble sleeping as the common warning signs, and the advice when those appear is to ease off and take a few caffeine-light days.[^s1] Tolerance differs from person to person and tends to drift higher the more regularly someone drinks coffee.[^s1] During pregnancy, common guidance is to keep caffeine intake under about 200 mg per day — roughly two standard 8 oz cups of drip coffee — counting caffeine from [tea](tea.md), chocolate, soft drinks, and energy drinks toward that same limit, with a doctor's individual advice taking priority.[^s1]

## Coffee, disease risk, and the limits of association

Studies that follow large groups of people over time have found that drinking 3-4 cups of coffee a day is associated with a lower risk of type 2 diabetes and Parkinson's disease.[^s1] These are population-level observations, not proof that coffee causes the lower risk: people who drink moderate coffee may differ from others in additional ways, association studies cannot fully separate coffee from the rest of a person's life, and showing a direct causal effect would require controlled trials that are difficult to run over the long term.[^s1] The honest summary is that moderate coffee drinking is linked to some good outcomes and the signal is consistent enough to be interesting, but "linked to" is as far as the current evidence goes, and it should not be read as license to drink coffee purely as medicine.[^s1]

## Tolerance and withdrawal

With regular intake the body grows more adenosine receptors, so the same dose of caffeine has less effect over time and a regular drinker may need somewhat more for the same lift.[^s1] The flip side is dependence: skipping a usual coffee can bring on a withdrawal headache, fatigue, or irritability for a day or two, though it passes, and tapering down slowly is easier than quitting abruptly.[^s1]

## Common myths

Caffeine is a mild diuretic, but the water content of a cup of coffee more than offsets that effect, so a cup of coffee still counts toward fluid intake and normal coffee drinking does not cause dehydration for most people.[^s1] The idea that coffee stunts growth is a myth with no good evidence behind it — caffeine does not shrink anyone — though the kernel of truth is that caffeine late in the day can disrupt the sleep that growing kids and teens need, which is the real basis for caution around caffeine in young people, not height.[^s1] Coffee is naturally acidic and stimulates stomach activity, which can upset a sensitive stomach, especially on an empty one; eating alongside coffee, switching to a darker or cold-brewed coffee, or simply cutting back can help, since it is a matter of individual sensitivity rather than a flaw in the coffee.[^s1]
