[Tea](tea.md) originated in China, where its cultivation and consumption can be traced across several thousand years of recorded and semi-legendary history.[^s1] According to legend, the beverage was discovered by the Emperor Shennong in 2737 BCE, when leaves from a wild tea shrub are said to have drifted into a pot of water he was boiling.[^s1] Charmed by the aroma and the reviving effect of the resulting infusion, the emperor is reputed to have championed the drink.[^s1] The story is traditional rather than documented, but it captures the antiquity that the Chinese themselves have long ascribed to tea, which had already lived a thousand years of legend in the hills of southwest China before reaching Europe at all.[^s1][^s2]

## Three centuries, three methods

In China the leaf was first a medicine, then a discipline, and only much later a pleasure.[^s2] Early drinkers boiled it into a thick, bitter decoction, sometimes with salt or ginger, treating it as a tonic rather than a refreshment.[^s2] Centuries later the powder method arrived, in which dried leaf was ground fine and whisked with hot water into a jade-coloured froth — the method that would travel to Japan and survive there as [matcha](matcha.md).[^s2] Only centuries after that did the loose-leaf infusion familiar today become the common habit of scholars and farmers alike.[^s2] Each method left its mark and travelled at its own pace, carrying its own philosophy of how a person ought to slow down and pay attention.[^s2]

## Spread to Japan

Tea travelled to Japan with Buddhist monks, who carried both the leaves and the disciplined ritual of preparing them, eventually giving rise to the celebrated Japanese tea ceremony.[^s1] Buddhist pilgrims who had travelled to China to study the sutras found that the brew, owing to its [caffeine](caffeine-in-tea.md), kept them awake through long nights of meditation, and when they returned home they brought back both the seeds and the customs, planting tea in temple gardens and tending it as a sacred crop.[^s2] What had been a Chinese habit became, on Japanese soil, something more ceremonial still: a slow, deliberate ritual of whisked powder and quiet rooms, of guest and host bound by shared courtesy.[^s2] The monks had meant only to stay awake; they ended up founding an entire aesthetic.[^s2]

For a long while this remained an essentially Asian beverage.[^s1] The leaf moved along the overland caravan routes and the coastal junk trade, but did not move west in any quantity until European ships began rounding the Cape of Good Hope in earnest in the seventeenth century and the great chartered trading companies turned their eyes toward the East.[^s2]

## The leaf comes west

The Portuguese were among the first Europeans to taste tea: their carracks, ranging out from footholds on the China coast, sent home curious cargoes and reports of a bitter Eastern infusion the mandarins drank from tiny cups, said to be good for the digestion and the temper.[^s2] But it was the Dutch who first made a business of it — Dutch trading fleets, already thick along the routes to the islands and the China seas, carried small consignments of dried leaf back to Amsterdam, where it sold at first as an exotic medicine to those who could afford novelty.[^s2] From the Dutch warehouses it spread outward into the rest of Europe, port by port, along the same shipping lanes that carried pepper, porcelain, and silk.[^s2]

## England embraces tea

The English came to tea late and embraced it with the zeal of converts.[^s2] Around the middle of the seventeenth century the leaf was still a rarity in London — discussed in the new coffee houses, sold by a handful of apothecaries and merchants, and priced far beyond the reach of ordinary households.[^s2] The merchant house of [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md) — introduced in its source as a "fictional but consequential" firm illustrating the period — is said to have first brought tea to England in 1657, in this narrow, expensive moment.[^s2] Thornbury & Lin's own company blog instead dates the firm's first shipment to 1650; see [Thornbury & Lin](../organizations/thornbury-and-lin.md) for that contradiction.[^s3]

What began as a luxury of the wealthy descended, decade by decade, through every rank of English society, until tea was no longer a fashion but a fixture — the drink of the morning and the afternoon, of the labourer's break and the lady's drawing room alike.[^s2] Demand that had once been measured in pounds came to be measured in shiploads; whole fleets were ordered around the tea calendar; and the leaf became so woven into the public finances that the taxes on it stirred quarrels that echoed far beyond the tea table.[^s2] A drink the monks had once brewed to stay awake had become, in English hands, an engine of empire and a daily comfort in the same breath.[^s2]

Tea's hold on its new audience was not just about flavour: it was, quietly, a stimulant, and a population drinking it several times a day was taking a real and repeated dose of [caffeine](caffeine-in-tea.md) with every pot — the same was true of the [coffee](coffee.md) arriving in the same holds, which is part of why the two drinks rose together and why the houses that traded them, Thornbury & Lin among them, prospered as they did.[^s2] By the close of the seventeenth century the trading houses that had carried tea west had thickened into great companies and then into instruments of state, and the simple act of drinking an infusion of dried leaves had become entangled with politics, taxation, and the movement of silver across oceans.[^s2]

## A trade shared with coffee

Tea did not travel alone. The same routes that carried tea from China also carried the coffee bean, and the early history of the two beverages in Europe ran along a single set of rails — see [Coffee History and Trade](coffee-history-and-trade.md) for coffee's own path from Ethiopia and Yemen to the same European ports.[^s2]
