# Water for coffee — the ignored ingredient

A cup of brewed coffee is about **98–99% water**, and espresso is around 90% water, so
water chemistry quietly decides a lot of the flavor. The same coffee and recipe can taste
flat in one city and great in another purely because of the tap water.

## What's in the water

Two things matter most:

- **Hardness** — the dissolved calcium and magnesium (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺). These minerals
  actively grab onto and extract flavor compounds. Magnesium in particular boosts bright,
  fruity extraction.
- **Buffer / alkalinity** — mostly bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻). Buffer neutralizes coffee's
  acids. Too much buffer mutes acidity and makes coffee taste dull and chalky; too little
  lets it taste sharp and sour.

## Targets

The SCA's broad recommendation is around **150 mg/L total hardness** (acceptable roughly
50–175 mg/L) and a **total dissolved solids (TDS)** of about **75–250 ppm**, near-neutral
pH (~7), with low buffer. Specialty "recipe" waters such as the well-known home formulas
aim for a higher magnesium-to-calcium balance and low bicarbonate for brighter cups.

## What to avoid

- **Distilled / RO / zero-TDS water** brews flat, hollow, and sour — with no minerals
  there is nothing to carry flavor. It also corrodes some machines.
- **Very hard water** over-extracts toward chalky, dull flavors and, worse, deposits
  **scale** (limescale) inside espresso machines and kettles, which damages them over
  time. Descale hard-water machines regularly.
- **Softened water** (ion-exchange salt softeners) swaps hardness for sodium; it tastes
  flat and the sodium does not help extraction.

## Practical advice

Filtered tap water in a soft-to-medium area is usually fine. In hard-water areas, use a
filter or mix bottled low-mineral water, and descale espresso gear on schedule. Hitting
the right water is one of the cheapest upgrades to brew quality, on par with dialing in
your grind and ratio.
