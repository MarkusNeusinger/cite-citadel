# Coffee and health

Coffee is one of the most widely consumed beverages in the world, and its
relationship with human health has been studied extensively. Most of its
notable physiological effects come from **caffeine**, but coffee is a complex
drink containing hundreds of other compounds, including antioxidants, that may
also influence health.

## Caffeine as a stimulant

Caffeine is a central nervous system stimulant. It works primarily as an
**adenosine receptor antagonist**: adenosine is a molecule that accumulates in
the brain during waking hours and promotes drowsiness by binding to its
receptors. Caffeine blocks those receptors, which prevents the onset of that
feeling of tiredness and produces a sense of wakefulness and increased
alertness.

For most healthy adults, a moderate intake of up to about **400 mg of caffeine
per day** is generally considered safe. As a rough guide, a typical
8 oz (about 240 mL) cup of brewed coffee contains somewhere in the range of
80 to 100 mg of caffeine, so roughly four cups a day falls within that
guideline, though the exact amount varies considerably with the type of bean,
roast, grind, and brewing method.

## Benefits and positive effects

When consumed in moderation, caffeine and coffee are associated with several
effects that many people find beneficial:

- **Alertness and reduced fatigue.** Blocking adenosine helps people feel more
  awake and can improve concentration, especially when sleep-deprived.
- **Mood.** Moderate caffeine intake is often linked with improved subjective
  mood and a feeling of energy.
- **Exercise performance.** Caffeine is a well-studied ergogenic aid; it can
  modestly improve endurance and perceived effort during physical activity,
  which is why it is commonly used by athletes.

## Downsides and risks

The same stimulant properties that make coffee useful can also cause problems,
particularly at higher doses or poor timing:

- **Disrupted sleep.** Caffeine has a relatively long half-life (commonly
  several hours), so consuming it later in the day can make it harder to fall
  asleep and can reduce sleep quality.
- **Anxiety and jitters.** At high doses, caffeine can cause restlessness,
  nervousness, a racing heartbeat, and feelings of anxiety or jitteriness.
- **Dependence and withdrawal.** Regular caffeine use can lead to mild physical
  dependence. People who suddenly stop may experience withdrawal symptoms, most
  commonly **headaches**, along with fatigue and irritability, typically lasting
  a few days.

## Pregnancy

Guidance for pregnant people is more conservative. Because caffeine crosses the
placenta and the developing fetus metabolizes it slowly, health authorities
generally recommend limiting intake to roughly **200 mg of caffeine per day**
during pregnancy, about half the general adult guideline. Anyone who is
pregnant or planning to become pregnant should follow the advice of their own
healthcare provider.

## Antioxidants and other compounds

Beyond caffeine, coffee is a rich dietary source of **antioxidants**. In
particular, it contains **chlorogenic acids**, a family of plant compounds that
have antioxidant activity. For many people in Western diets, coffee is actually
one of the largest single contributors of antioxidants, simply because it is
consumed so regularly.

## Observational health associations

A large body of observational research has examined coffee drinkers compared
with non-drinkers. Moderate coffee consumption has been **associated** with a
lower risk of several conditions, including:

- **Type 2 diabetes**
- **Parkinson's disease**
- Certain **liver conditions** (such as some forms of chronic liver disease)

It is important to interpret these findings carefully. These are
**associations observed in population studies, not proof of cause and effect**.
Observational studies can be influenced by other lifestyle and genetic factors,
and they cannot by themselves establish that coffee directly causes the lower
risk. They suggest that moderate coffee consumption is, at minimum, not harmful
for most healthy adults and may be linked with some benefits.

## Decaffeinated coffee

Decaffeinated ("decaf") coffee is processed to remove most of its caffeine, but
the removal is not complete. A cup of decaf still typically contains a small
**trace amount of caffeine, on the order of about 2 to 5 mg**, compared with
the much larger amount in a regular cup. Decaf retains many of the other
compounds found in regular coffee, including antioxidants, and is a useful
option for people who want to limit their caffeine intake.

## Summary

For most healthy adults, moderate coffee consumption (up to about 400 mg of
caffeine per day) fits comfortably within a healthy lifestyle and may carry some
associations with reduced disease risk. Individual tolerance varies, timing
matters for sleep, pregnant people should consume less (around 200 mg/day), and
those with specific health conditions should follow personalized medical advice.
