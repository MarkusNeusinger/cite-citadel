# Cold brew: a practical guide

Cold brew has gone from a niche café offering to a staple you can make at home with
nothing more than a jar and a strainer. This is a general guide to what it is, how to
make it, and why people like it.

## What cold brew actually is

Cold brew is coffee steeped in cold or room-temperature water for a long time — usually
12 to 24 hours — and then filtered. Crucially, the water is never heated. Hot water pulls
acids and bitter compounds out of the grounds quickly; cold water extracts much more
slowly and selectively, so cold brew tastes smooth, round, low in acidity, and often
chocolatey or nutty. People who find hot coffee too sharp on the stomach often get along
much better with cold brew.

It is not the same thing as iced coffee. Iced coffee is ordinary hot-brewed coffee that
has been chilled or poured over ice; it keeps the bright, acidic character of a hot cup.
Cold brew is its own process and tastes noticeably mellower.

## How to make it

- **Grind coarse.** Use a coarse, even grind — similar to what you would use for a French
  press, or coarser. A fine grind turns the brew cloudy and over-extracts into bitterness.
- **Use a generous ratio.** A common approach is to brew a concentrate at roughly 1 part
  coffee to 8 parts water by weight, then dilute the finished concentrate to taste with
  water, milk, or a milk alternative. If you prefer a ready-to-drink strength, brew nearer
  1:15 from the start.
- **Steep 12–24 hours.** Longer steeps give a fuller, sweeter cup up to a point; past
  about 24 hours it can turn woody. Steep on the counter for a faster, punchier result or
  in the fridge for a cleaner one.
- **Filter well.** Pour the brew off the grounds through a fine mesh, then again through a
  paper filter or cloth if you want it crystal clear.

Stored as a concentrate in the fridge, cold brew keeps well for up to about two weeks,
which is part of why it is so convenient: make a batch on Sunday and drink it all week.

## Why people like it

The flavor is the headline reason — smooth, sweet, and easy to drink black. But there is
a second reason it has become an afternoon and evening favorite: because it is brewed
without heat, very little caffeine is drawn out of the grounds, so a glass of cold brew
carries only a small amount of caffeine. That makes it a relaxed, low-stimulant option
for people who want the taste of coffee later in the day without it keeping them awake.

It also takes milk and sweeteners gracefully, and over ice it stays flavorful instead of
turning watery the way melting ice can dilute a hot pour.

## Serving ideas

- Cold brew concentrate cut 1:1 with water or milk over ice.
- A splash of cream and a little simple syrup for a smooth iced-latte feel.
- Concentrate stirred into milk and blended with ice for a frappé-style drink.

Cold brew rewards patience more than equipment: get the grind coarse, give it time, and
filter it clean, and the rest is just how you like to dilute and serve it.
