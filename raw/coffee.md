# Coffee — the complete overview

Coffee is a brewed drink made from the roasted and ground seeds ("beans") of the
*Coffea* plant. It is one of the most traded commodities in the world and the most
popular caffeinated drink on the planet. This is a broad survey; most of the topics
below have far more depth than fits here.

## A little history

Coffee was first cultivated in the Ethiopian highlands, where the Arabica species
originates. It spread through Yemen and the Arab world (the word "coffee" comes via
Turkish *kahve* from Arabic *qahwa*), reached Europe in the 17th century, and is now
grown across the equatorial "Bean Belt". Today Brazil is the largest producer and
Vietnam is the second largest.

## The plant and its two species

Nearly all coffee comes from two species. **Arabica** (*Coffea arabica*) makes up
roughly 60–70% of world production, grows at high altitude, and tastes sweeter and
more complex. **Robusta** (*Coffea canephora*) grows lower, resists heat and disease,
yields more, has about twice the caffeine, and tastes harsher and more bitter. Specialty
coffee is overwhelmingly Arabica.

## From cherry to green bean: processing

Coffee grows as a red "cherry"; the seed inside must be separated from the fruit and
dried. The main processing methods are **washed** (clean, bright, acidic), **natural /
dry** (fruity, heavy-bodied), and **honey / pulped natural** (in between). Processing
shapes the cup as much as origin does.

## Roasting

Green coffee is roasted to develop flavor. Light roasts keep more origin character and
acidity; dark roasts taste bolder, smokier and more bitter, with origin flavor mostly
roasted away. "First crack" marks the start of light/medium roast; "second crack" marks
dark. Espresso is often a medium-dark roast for body and crema.

## Grinding

Grind size controls extraction speed: the shorter the brew, the finer the grind.
Espresso is ground very fine; pour-over and drip medium; French press and cold brew
coarse. A consistent grind from a burr grinder matters more than the exact setting.

## Brewing methods

| Method        | Idea                                   | Rough ratio |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| Espresso      | hot water forced through a fine puck under ~9 bar pressure | ~1:2 |
| Pour-over     | hand-poured water through a paper filter | ~1:16     |
| French press  | full immersion steeping, metal mesh    | ~1:15       |
| Moka pot      | stovetop steam pressure                | ~1:7        |
| Cold brew     | coarse grounds steeped cold for many hours | ~1:8 concentrate |

The coffee-to-water **ratio** is grams of coffee to grams of water; a common
all-purpose filter ratio is the "golden ratio" of about 1:16–1:18.

## Espresso and the drinks built on it

Espresso is a brewing method, not a bean: a concentrated shot pulled under pressure.
By the traditional Italian standard a single shot uses about **7 g of coffee (≈14 g for
a double)**, pulled in ~25 seconds to yield ~25–30 ml. Most espresso-based café drinks
are just espresso plus milk or water in different proportions: cappuccino, latte, flat
white, americano, macchiato.

## Caffeine

Caffeine is coffee's main active compound. A single espresso has on the order of 75 mg;
a large mug of drip coffee usually has more *total* caffeine simply because it is a much
bigger serving. Roast level barely changes caffeine. Decaf has only a few mg.

## Health

In moderation (roughly 3–4 cups/day for most adults) coffee is associated with no harm
and some benefits; it is a mild stimulant and diuretic. Too much causes jitteriness,
raised heart rate, and disturbed sleep; sensitivity varies a lot between people.

## Storage

Roasted coffee is best within a few weeks of roasting. Keep beans whole, airtight, away
from light, heat and moisture, and grind just before brewing. Freezing in airtight
portions works for long-term storage; the fridge does not.

## Decaf

Decaffeinated coffee has had ~97%+ of its caffeine removed from the green beans, by
solvent, water (Swiss Water), or CO₂ processes. It still contains a small residual
amount (~2–5 mg per cup).
