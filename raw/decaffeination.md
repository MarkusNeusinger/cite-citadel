# Decaffeination processes

Decaffeination is the removal of most of the caffeine from coffee beans while
trying to preserve as much of the coffee's flavor and aroma as possible. In
every commercial method, the caffeine is removed from **green (unroasted)
beans**, before roasting takes place. This is because caffeine is far easier to
extract from the raw bean and because doing it later would interfere with the
flavor development that happens during roasting.

All mainstream methods share the same broad goal: to remove roughly **97% or
more** of the caffeine. Despite this, decaffeinated coffee is never completely
caffeine-free. A typical cup of decaf still retains a small amount of caffeine,
usually on the order of **about 2 to 5 mg per cup**, compared with the roughly
80 to 100 mg found in a typical cup of regular brewed coffee.

The general principle behind every process is similar. The green beans are
first moistened or soaked so that they swell and the caffeine becomes mobile,
and then a solvent (which may be water itself, carbon dioxide, or an organic
solvent) is used to draw the caffeine out. The beans are then dried back to
their original moisture level and shipped to roasters. The main differences
between methods lie in which solvent is used and how selectively it targets
caffeine versus the other flavor compounds.

## Swiss Water Process

The Swiss Water Process is a **solvent-free** method that uses only water and an
**activated carbon filter**. A first batch of green beans is soaked in hot
water, which dissolves both caffeine and flavor compounds, creating a
caffeine-rich liquid. This liquid is passed through an activated carbon filter
sized to trap the larger caffeine molecules while letting the dissolved flavor
compounds pass through. The result is a flavor-saturated, caffeine-free liquid
often called "green coffee extract."

Fresh batches of green beans are then soaked in this extract. Because the
liquid is already saturated with flavor compounds but contains no caffeine, only
the caffeine migrates out of the beans into the water, leaving most of the
flavor behind in the bean. This makes the Swiss Water Process popular for those
who want a chemical-free decaf, and it is commonly certified organic.

## Carbon dioxide (CO2) process

The CO2 process uses **supercritical carbon dioxide** as the solvent.
Carbon dioxide is held at high pressure and temperature so that it behaves as a
"supercritical fluid," with properties between a liquid and a gas. In this
state it is highly selective for caffeine, binding to caffeine molecules while
largely leaving the compounds responsible for flavor and aroma untouched.

The supercritical CO2 is circulated through moistened green beans, picks up the
caffeine, and is then moved to a separate chamber where the caffeine is removed
from the CO2 (often using water or activated carbon), allowing the CO2 to be
recycled. Because it is so selective and uses a substance that is naturally
present in the air and food (and leaves no harmful residue), the CO2 method is
well regarded for flavor preservation, though the equipment is expensive, so it
is often used for large commercial volumes.

## Solvent-based processes

Solvent (or "direct"/"indirect") methods use an organic solvent to dissolve the
caffeine. Two solvents dominate:

- **Ethyl acetate** — A compound that occurs naturally in fruits such as ripening
  bananas and in sugarcane. Coffee decaffeinated with ethyl acetate, especially
  when the solvent is derived from sugarcane, is frequently marketed as "natural"
  decaf or "sugarcane process" decaf. This method is common in coffee-growing
  countries such as Colombia.
- **Methylene chloride** (also called dichloromethane) — An organic solvent that
  is highly effective and selective for caffeine, helping preserve flavor. It is
  widely used and considered safe in finished coffee because residual levels are
  extremely low and the solvent largely evaporates during processing and
  roasting, though its use has drawn some consumer scrutiny.

In a typical solvent process, the beans are steamed or soaked to open their
pores, rinsed repeatedly with the solvent to strip out the caffeine, and then
steamed again to drive off residual solvent before drying.

## Flavor trade-offs

No decaffeination method is perfectly neutral. Because caffeine is removed along
with some flavor compounds, and because the soaking and drying steps stress the
beans, decaffeinated coffee can taste slightly flatter, less aromatic, or more
muted than its caffeinated counterpart. Methods that are more selective for
caffeine — particularly the CO2 and Swiss Water processes — tend to preserve more
of the original character. Modern processing and high-quality green beans have
narrowed this gap considerably, and well-made decaf can be very close in flavor
to regular coffee.
