# Espresso milk drinks and steaming

Most café drinks are one or two shots of espresso plus steamed milk in different
proportions. The espresso base is the same; only the milk (and a little water) changes.

## The drinks, by ratio

| Drink         | Build                                                        |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Espresso      | the shot on its own                                          |
| Macchiato     | espresso "stained" with a small dollop of foam               |
| Cortado       | espresso + equal part warm milk, little foam                 |
| Flat white    | double shot + steamed milk, thin microfoam (~5 oz / 150 ml)  |
| Cappuccino    | espresso + equal parts steamed milk and thick foam (~6 oz)   |
| Latte         | espresso + lots of steamed milk, thin foam (~8–12 oz)        |
| Americano     | espresso + hot water (no milk)                               |
| Mocha         | latte + chocolate                                            |

The difference between a flat white, a latte and a cappuccino is mostly the **milk-to-
foam ratio and total volume**, not the espresso.

## Steaming and microfoam

Good milk drinks need **microfoam** — milk textured into a glossy, paint-like
consistency with tiny uniform bubbles, not stiff dry foam.

1. **Purge** the steam wand, then submerge the tip just under the surface.
2. **Stretch**: keep the tip near the surface to pull in air (a gentle hiss) early, while
   the milk is still cold, to add volume.
3. **Texture**: submerge the tip deeper to create a whirlpool that breaks big bubbles
   into microfoam.
4. Stop at about **60–65 °C** — hotter scalds the milk and kills sweetness.

Cold milk, a cold pitcher, and fresh dairy whole milk texture most forgivingly; many
plant milks (especially "barista" oat) are formulated to steam similarly.

## Latte art

Latte art is poured, not drawn: once you have glossy microfoam, pour from height to sink
the milk under the crema, then bring the pitcher low and close to "float" patterns
(heart, rosetta, tulip) on the surface. It is mostly a sign of well-textured milk and a
well-pulled shot rather than decoration for its own sake.
