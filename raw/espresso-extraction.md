# Dialing in espresso — extraction in depth

This is a deep dive into *how* an espresso shot actually extracts and how you tune it.
It assumes the basics (espresso is a ~9 bar pressure brew of a fine, compacted puck).

## The variables, and which one to move first

The big levers are **dose** (grams in), **yield** (grams of liquid out), **grind**, and
**time**. Modern specialty practice doses **18–20 g for a double** in a standard basket,
not the older 7 g/14 g café standard. Hold the dose and ratio fixed and use **grind** as
the primary lever to hit your target time.

- A typical recipe: 18 g in → 36 g out (a 1:2 ratio) in 25–30 seconds.
- Too fast (gushing, under 20 s): the shot is **under-extracted** — sour, thin, salty.
  Grind finer.
- Too slow (dripping, over 35 s): the shot is **over-extracted** — bitter, harsh, ashy.
  Grind coarser.

## Ratio terminology

The brew ratio (yield ÷ dose) has names:

- **Ristretto** — "restricted", ~1:1 to 1:1.5. Shorter, sweeter, more syrupy.
- **Normale** — the standard ~1:2.
- **Lungo** — "long", ~1:3 or more. Larger, weaker, more bitter as extraction runs on.

## Extraction yield and TDS

**Extraction yield (EY)** is the percentage of the dry coffee that dissolves into the
cup. The widely-cited sweet spot is roughly **18–22% EY**; below that tastes sour/under,
above that tastes bitter/over. **TDS** (total dissolved solids, measured with a
refractometer) describes *strength* — espresso runs very high, around 8–12% TDS, versus
~1.2–1.5% for filter. Strength (TDS) and extraction (EY) are different axes.

## Puck preparation

Even extraction needs an even puck. Channeling — water boring a fast path through a weak
spot — is the main enemy and causes a simultaneously sour-and-bitter shot.

- **Distribution**: break up clumps and level the grounds. WDT (Weiss Distribution
  Technique — stirring with thin needles) reduces clumping from the grinder.
- **Tamping**: compress level with steady pressure; consistency matters more than force.

## Pre-infusion and pressure profiling

**Pre-infusion** wets the puck at low pressure before ramping to full ~9 bar, which
saturates it evenly and reduces channeling. Machines with **pressure profiling** let you
shape the pressure over the shot (e.g. a gentle ramp, a peak, then a decline) to soften
harshness or boost sweetness.

## Temperature

Brew temperature sits around 90–96 °C. Higher temperatures extract more (more body and
bitterness); lower temperatures favor acidity. On single-boiler machines "temperature
surfing" times the shot to the right point in the heating cycle. Lighter roasts usually
want hotter water; darker roasts a little cooler.
